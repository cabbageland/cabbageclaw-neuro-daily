# Confirmatory efficacy and safety trial of magnetic seizure therapy versus right unilateral ultra-brief electroconvulsive therapy in depression (CREST-MST)

## Basic info

* Title: Confirmatory efficacy and safety trial of magnetic seizure therapy versus right unilateral ultra-brief electroconvulsive therapy in depression (CREST-MST): a randomised, double-blind, non-inferiority trial in Canada and the USA
* Authors: Daniel M. Blumberger and colleagues
* Year: 2026
* Venue / source: Lancet Psychiatry / PMC author manuscript
* Link: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41997695/
* Date surfaced: 2026-05-25
* Why selected in one sentence: It materially upgrades the evidence base for magnetic seizure therapy by testing it against modern right-unilateral ultra-brief ECT in a real non-inferiority design instead of another pilot teaser.

## Quick verdict

* Highly relevant

This is one of the stronger device-comparator papers in recent interventional psychiatry. The big reason to keep it is not that MST beat ECT. It did not. The reason is that MST achieved non-inferior remission within the pre-specified margin while showing a much cleaner autobiographical-memory profile. That makes the tradeoff structure more real and less aspirational.

## One-paragraph overview

The CREST-MST trial randomized adults with major depressive disorder to either magnetic seizure therapy or right-unilateral ultra-brief electroconvulsive therapy across three academic sites. Treatment continued until remission, dropout, or twenty-one sessions. The co-primary outcomes were depression remission and worsening of autobiographical memory. MST produced a somewhat lower remission rate than ECT but still met the non-inferiority criterion, while memory worsening was far less common under MST. The paper therefore does something useful and uncommon: it turns a device-hype story into an explicit efficacy-versus-cognitive-cost comparison.

## Key questions this summary must address

### 1. What problem is the paper trying to solve?
ECT works, but many patients refuse it because of cognitive adverse effects, especially memory concerns. The paper asks whether MST can preserve much of the antidepressant efficacy while reducing that cognitive burden.

### 2. What is the method?
A multisite randomized double-blind parallel-group non-inferiority trial comparing MST against right-unilateral ultra-brief ECT in adults with major depressive disorder. Participants received up to twenty-one treatments or stopped earlier for remission or dropout.

### 3. What is the method motivation?
Magnetic seizure therapy induces therapeutic seizures more focally than ECT and is thought to spare medial-temporal structures more effectively. The mechanistic bet is that this focality lowers cognitive harm without losing too much antidepressant effect.

### 4. What data does it use?
Two hundred thirty-nine randomized participants with major depressive disorder across Canada and the United States, with depression ratings, autobiographical-memory testing, side-effect reports, and broader neurocognitive outcomes.

### 5. How is it evaluated?
The co-primary efficacy endpoint was remission on the twenty-four-item Hamilton Rating Scale for Depression. The co-primary cognitive endpoint was clinically meaningful worsening on the autobiographical memory test. Secondary outcomes included response, suicidal ideation, side effects, and exploratory neurocognitive measures.

### 6. What are the main results?
Remission was numerically higher in the ECT arm, twenty-seven point eight percent versus twenty-two point five percent, but the difference stayed within the fifteen-percent non-inferiority margin, so MST met the trial’s efficacy criterion. Memory worsening was much more common with ECT than MST, roughly seventeen percent versus under three percent. More ECT participants also withdrew because of non-serious adverse events.

### 7. What is actually novel?
The real novelty is evidentiary, not conceptual. MST has been around as a promising idea, but this trial gives it a serious comparator frame and a large enough sample to matter more than the older pilot literature.

### 8. What are the strengths?
Large randomized comparator design, modern ECT comparator instead of a straw-man older protocol, explicit co-primary cognitive outcome, and clinically interpretable tradeoff framing.

### 9. What are the weaknesses, limitations, or red flags?
Enrollment ended below target sample size, so the evidentiary margin is not as comfortable as planned. The remission rates are not spectacular in either arm. And calling MST a first-line convulsive therapy on the basis of non-inferiority plus better memory may still be somewhat aggressive given access, device availability, and remaining practical constraints.

### 10. What challenges or open problems remain?
We still need clearer patient-selection logic, durability data, broader functional outcomes, and better understanding of which patients most value the cognitive tradeoff enough to prefer MST over ECT.

### 11. What future work naturally follows?
Longer-term follow-up, pragmatic implementation studies, subgroup analyses for who benefits most from the tradeoff, and more explicit work linking seizure properties and focality to cognitive outcomes.

### 12. Why does this matter for cabbageland?
Because it is a good example of an intervention paper that earns its claims by making the tradeoff explicit. For interventional psychiatry more broadly, it supports the standard that new modalities should be judged against serious comparators and clinically meaningful collateral costs.

### 13. What ideas are steal-worthy?
Use co-primary benefit and collateral-cost endpoints when new interventions promise a cleaner risk profile. Treat focality claims as meaningful only when they show up in a real cognitive outcome, not just in field-model diagrams.

### 14. Final decision
Keep. This is one of the more decision-relevant recent comparator trials in the depression-device stack.
