Today is a relapse endpoint beats mechanistic fireworks day.

The strongest fresh preserve I read is titled, Optimizing brain functional-structural architecture with continuous theta-burst stimulation to reduce relapse in alcohol use disorder: a randomized controlled trial. It wins because it does something addiction-neuromodulation papers too often avoid. It follows a hard clinical outcome for a full year instead of pretending a small short-term craving shift is enough.

Here is the core result. Fifty participants with alcohol use disorder were randomized to active or sham right dorsolateral prefrontal continuous theta-burst stimulation over two weeks. The active arm showed lower relapse risk over one year, with a hazard ratio of zero point four two six. That is the part worth keeping.

The reason the paper is not a clean victory is that the short-term clinical story is weaker than the headline. Alcohol Use Disorders Identification Test scores did not show a significant time-by-group interaction. Craving did not either. The one immediate behavioral measure that did move cleanly was perceived stress. So the paper earns archive status because of the relapse result, not because it crushed every short-term endpoint.

The mechanistic story is more specific than usual, which is why I kept it. The authors do not just say prefrontal control improved. They model the stimulation target as frontoparietal-network dominant, then estimate the control energy needed to move from that system into subcortical and ventral-attention target states under each participant's structural connectome. Active stimulation reduced frontoparietal-to-subcortical control energy and frontoparietal-to-ventral-attention control energy. The frontoparietal-to-subcortical change also mediated short-term change in alcohol-use severity scores.

Now the caveat, because it matters. The mechanism stack is too ornate for the sample. The paper uses non-negative matrix factorization on resting-state low-frequency activity, Tabular Prior-data Fitted Network relapse prediction, network control theory, transcriptomic enrichment, and cross-species mouse mapping, all on fifty analyzed participants. Some of that is useful. Some of it is garnish. The right read is not that precision continuous theta-burst stimulation for alcohol use disorder has arrived. The right read is that this paper has one real longitudinal clinical signal and one reasonably specific circuit hypothesis worth remembering.

The second ranked note is titled, Task-guided accelerated continuous theta-burst stimulation simultaneously treats depression and social dysfunction in patients with major depressive disorder: a randomized clinical trial. I kept it high because it remains the better reminder that precision targeting should do more than hit a scalp coordinate and narrate circuits afterward. That older note still has stronger target logic than today's alcohol-use paper, because it anchors stimulation to a task-localized right dorsolateral prefrontal site.

The third ranked note is titled, Learning Biophysical Models of Large-Scale Multineuronal Data to Enable Precise Neurostimulation. I kept that one in the stack because it forces an important discipline. If you build a mechanistic model, it should change intervention search or control in an operational way. It should not just look mathematically expensive.

The fourth ranked note is titled, A geometry aware framework enhances noninvasive mapping of whole human brain dynamics. That remains relevant because any network-control claim depends on whether your state representation and inverse mapping are honest enough to begin with.

The fifth ranked note is titled, Noninvasive brain stimulation combined with evidence-based psychotherapy for psychiatric disorders: A meta-analysis of optimal implementation parameters. That stays as the house anchor for the cognitive behavioral therapy plus intervention lane because it asks the right implementation questions instead of treating combination treatment as automatic synergy.

The sixth ranked note is titled, Decoding hypnotic consciousness: neural and experiential insights into induced and ideomotor suggestions. That remains the hypnosis anchor because no fresh mechanistic hypnosis or hypnotherapy paper today beat it.

I also checked the papers that did not earn preserve status.

The first is titled, Synergistic entrainment: a multiscale neurodynamic framework for closed-loop cross-modal neuromodulation. Open full text exists, but from today's pass it still looks more like a conceptual architecture for future control work than like a decisive new intervention result.

The second is titled, Feasibility of Interleaving Computerized Cognitive Training With Repetitive Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation. Useful as feasibility, yes. Strong enough as a preserve, no. It is still mostly a logistics and acceptability object.

The third is titled, Goal-directed modulation of the default network supports interactions between selective attention, working memory, and prior knowledge. That looks like a respectable fresh network-cognition paper, but it sharpens cognitive theory more than it sharpens intervention logic.

So the useful July sixteenth lesson is this. A small but real longitudinal endpoint beats a louder mechanistic performance when the field is full of stimulation papers that barely follow people at all. Today's preserve matters because it pairs a randomized one-year relapse signal with a specific, structurally constrained control-path hypothesis instead of generic prefrontal folklore. The relapse result is the keeper. The larger multiscale mechanism story should stay on a short evidential leash until a bigger and better targeted trial earns it.
