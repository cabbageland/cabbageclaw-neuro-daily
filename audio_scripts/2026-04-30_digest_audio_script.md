Today is a targeting-and-measurement honesty day. The common pattern is that neuromodulation logic only gets better when papers are forced to show what was really engaged, what was really measured, and where the inference still breaks.

The first paper is directly relevant, titled, Target engagement in a head-to-head clinical trial of dysphoric versus anxiosomatic transcranial magnetic stimulation targets. This is the most relevant paper today. In a randomized head-to-head trial, patients with depression and anxiety received thirty transcranial magnetic stimulation sessions at either a dorsolateral prefrontal target aimed at dysphoric symptoms or a dorsomedial prefrontal target aimed at anxiosomatic symptoms. The useful result is not a clean precision-TMS victory. Pre to post connectivity change was not clearly target-specific and did not predict symptom improvement very well. What did matter was baseline connectivity from the chosen stimulation site to the intended circuit. Stronger baseline site-to-circuit connectivity predicted greater circuit change and better anxiety improvement, especially in the anxiosomatic arm. The reason this matters is simple. It suggests that baseline architectural suitability may be a better biomarker than post-treatment connectivity theater.

The second paper is directly relevant, titled, Temporal Interference Stimulation Modulates Resting State Functional Connectivity of Motor Circuit in Parkinson's Disease. Twenty-three patients with mild to moderate Parkinson's disease underwent an acute twenty-minute session of one hundred thirty hertz temporal-interference stimulation aimed at the subthalamic nucleus, plus an active sham condition in a randomized double-blind crossover design. Compared with sham, stimulation decreased connectivity between the targeted subthalamic nucleus and striatal regions, and increased connectivity with primary motor cortex. That is interesting because it gives at least some human circuit-level evidence that the intervention is perturbing the intended motor loop. The main caveat is that this remains a small acute imaging study. It does not yet prove deep-target specificity or durable clinical benefit.

The third paper is adjacent but high value, titled, A geometry aware framework enhances noninvasive mapping of whole human brain dynamics. This is a methods paper, not a therapy paper, but it may be more leverage-heavy than many therapy papers. The authors derive patient-specific geometric basis functions from each person's cortical surface and use those modes as the prior for reconstructing EEG and magnetoencephalography source dynamics. They validate the approach across benchmarks, task data, resting-state networks, intracranial stimulation, and epilepsy settings. The useful read here is that many network-control and targeting claims depend on source reconstructions that may be worse than advertised. If geometry-constrained reconstruction is better, then a lot of downstream inference gets less cartoonish.

The fourth paper is directly relevant, titled, Effective correction of extreme capacitive artifacts in TMS-EEG via windowed detrending. This paper compares standard detrending and independent component analysis based cleanup against a windowed detrending approach that models the charging and discharging phases of the artifact separately. The important result is that independent component analysis is acceptable when artifacts are mild, but starts to fail in the severe regimes where the data are actually ugly. Windowed detrending looks more robust there. That matters because TMS-EEG claims about excitability and connectivity can quietly collapse when preprocessing only works in easy conditions.

The broader takeaway is simple. The best paper today says baseline circuit architecture may matter more than induced connectivity change for precision transcranial magnetic stimulation. The temporal-interference paper is interesting because it pushes a deep-target claim toward human circuit evidence, though the evidence is still preliminary. The geometry-aware source-imaging paper improves the measurement substrate underneath network models and targeting logic. And the TMS-EEG artifact paper reminds us that measurement cleanup is part of the causal inference stack, not janitorial trivia.
