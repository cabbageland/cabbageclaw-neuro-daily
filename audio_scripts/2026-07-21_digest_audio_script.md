Today is a deep-target stimulation is only as believable as the measurement stack day.

The top fresh preserve is titled, Neural dynamics of temporal interference stimulation monitoring by soft liquid metal interfaces across neural systems. It gets the top rank because it fixes a chronic temporal-interference weakness at the right layer. Most temporal-interference papers want credit for field geometry first and physiology later. This paper does something harder. It builds soft liquid-metal interfaces that can actually record from inside brain organoids and mouse hippocampus, then uses them to ask what temporal interference does to spiking, synchronization-based connectivity, neuronal avalanches, and latent population dynamics.

That does not make temporal interference clinically solved. But it does make the physiology story less fake. In organoids, the paper reports higher spiking than control or uniform low-frequency stimulation, stronger functional connectivity, and increased synaptic and differentiation markers without obvious toxicity. In mouse brains, the story is different. The networks become denser but also more globally reorganized, with weaker average pairwise synchronization and higher avalanche efficiency. The useful point is that the response depends on tissue maturity, not that one single metric goes up.

That makes the new note a good complement to existing archive anchors. Temporal interference stimulation for major depressive disorder: right lingual gyrus as a predictor is still the best current human T I S note in the repo, because it at least connects treatment heterogeneity to a measurable baseline state. Direct delivery of modulated kilohertz frequency waveforms enable simultaneous electrical stimulation and recording with minimal artifact is still the cleaner note on whether the recording stack can survive stimulation at all. And On the feasibility of temporal interference stimulation of human brains using two arrays of electrodes still matters because it treats optimization burden and hardware realism as part of intervention credibility. Today’s new paper adds the missing physiology-monitoring bridge between those themes.

Two fresh depression papers were inspected and both stayed below preserve level. The first is titled, Brain contrastive modeling reveals depression subtypes with distinct treatment response and progression. It is not weak. The paper uses a contrastive variational autoencoder over resting-state f A L F F maps, validates two depression subtypes across multiple cohorts, and reports better medication and repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation response in one subtype. The problem is that it overlaps heavily with the archive’s existing depression-heterogeneity notes, and the paper itself admits that direct r T M S outcome prediction is not effective under leave-one-site-out validation. So the subtype association story is stronger than the actual precision-treatment story.

The second fresh depression paper is titled, Cross-sectional and longitudinal analysis of prefrontal intermittent theta-burst stimulation-evoked hemodynamic responses in major depressive disorder. That one was also inspected through the accepted-manuscript PDF. It is useful as treatment-monitoring context, but it does not earn a canonical note. There is no significant baseline difference between depressed participants and healthy controls in the stimulation-evoked hemodynamic response. What survives is a post-treatment correlation between changes in the hemodynamic response and symptom improvement. That is interesting, but it is weaker than a real pretreatment stratifier and weaker than the repo’s existing stimulation-evoked prediction anchor.

The standing-interest lanes were checked directly and stayed thin. The hypnosis and hypnotherapy pass mostly surfaced protocol and case-report level material. The cognitive behavioral therapy plus interventional pass was similar, mostly weakly related reviews or comorbidity discussions rather than a strong mechanistic hybrid-care note. So the day stays small on purpose.

The main takeaway is simple. Temporal-interference work only starts getting interesting when someone pays the observability bill. Today’s preserve matters because it moves the discussion from envelope-field theater toward measurable internal neural dynamics, while still leaving the translational and mechanistic hard parts very much unfinished.
