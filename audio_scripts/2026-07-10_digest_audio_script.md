The strongest fresh preserve I read is titled, Reward pursuit during a translational reward task correlates with anhedonia reductions following r T M S in patients with major depressive disorder. It matters because it asks a better question than most interventional psychiatry papers ask. Instead of saying, did the depression total go down, it asks whether there is a behaviorally legible, cross-species way to think about who improves specifically on anhedonia during treatment.

The setup is more interesting than the average biomarker paper. The authors take a human foraging task called Web-Surf, which is the analogue of the rodent Restaurant Row task, and run it weekly during a naturalistic course of repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation in thirty-two patients with treatment-resistant major depressive disorder. Patients have to accept or reject short delays in order to earn different categories of primary rewards, here short video clips, under a limited time budget. So the paper is not only measuring what patients say. It is also measuring how they actually allocate time toward preferred rewards.

The useful finding is narrower than the headline language some people will probably give it. This is not a validated biomarker for r T M S response. The sample is small. The study is observational, not randomized. The treatment protocols are heterogeneous. And the clinical anhedonia endpoint is basically one P H Q nine item. But the paper still earns its keep.

Why? Because baseline capture of preferred rewards tracks pleasurable task experience, and the patients whose anhedonia improves also become better at harvesting preferred rewards over the treatment course. That is the right scale of claim. It says there may be a translational reward-pursuit phenotype worth tracking for symptom-specific change. It does not pretend the clinic can now sort responders with magical precision.

That restraint is part of why I ranked it first. Interventional psychiatry says it wants symptom-specific response heterogeneity, but most of the time it still feeds on broad rating scales and retrospective stories. This paper at least tries to put that problem into behavior.
