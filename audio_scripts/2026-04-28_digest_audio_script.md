Welcome to the April 28 Neuro Daily at Cabbageland!

Today is a state-aware targeting and control-geometry day. The strongest paper is Digital twin brain reveals state-specific stimulation targets for abnormal brain dynamics in tinnitus. The broader pattern is that the better papers stop pretending stimulation is either perfectly generic or perfectly focal.

Here is the ranked list.

The first paper is directly relevant, titled, Digital twin brain reveals state-specific stimulation targets for abnormal brain dynamics in tinnitus. The authors build a tinnitus-specific digital twin brain from multimodal imaging, identify two abnormal whole-brain states, and then run more than one point six four million virtual stimulations to ask which targets move which state. The useful claim is that sensory-dominant abnormalities and cognitive-default-mode abnormalities may need different targets. Parieto-occipital regions look more important for the sensory state, while dorsolateral prefrontal cortex looks more important for the cognitive state. What makes this worth keeping is that the authors do not stop at simulation. They test whether the model-derived response maps predict effects in an independent repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation dataset. The main caveat is that this is still a model-dependent framework, so it is a framing advance, not a solved targeting system.

The second paper is adjacent but highly valuable, titled, Controlling the human connectome with spatially diffuse input signals. This is a network-control paper, not a clinical trial. The core move is to stop modeling control inputs as independent point injections at single nodes. Instead, inputs spread spatially with strength that decays with distance, which is much closer to how cortex and stimulation devices actually behave. Under that assumption, whole-brain state transitions require much less control energy, and near-optimal strategies can use far fewer input sites. The reason this matters is simple. If your actuator model is wrong, your control conclusions are probably wrong too. The caveat is that the framework is still abstract and depends on assumptions about spatial decay and linear dynamics.

The third paper is directly relevant, titled, Circadian limbic and thalamic beta oscillations drive slow-adapting dual-threshold adaptive deep brain stimulation in Tourette syndrome. This is a single-patient proof of concept, so the evidence is thin. But the control logic is unusually sane. The authors use chronic beta rhythms from ventral capsule or ventral striatum and centromedian-parafascicular thalamus to reduce stimulation automatically during sleep while keeping daytime stimulation stable. That is a real operational problem with a real biomarker, not just adaptive theater. An accidental sixteen-night period with adaptive mode off gave a rough comparison suggesting modest tic improvement and larger depression improvement during the adaptive period. The main caveat is obvious: one patient is nowhere near enough for a strong clinical claim.

The fourth paper is directly relevant, titled, Anatomy-informed recommendations for electrode montage and shape in electrical stimulation methods: a tDCS case study. Using finite-element simulations in five hundred ninety Human Connectome Project-Aging participants, the authors show that montage choice and head anatomy strongly reshape where electric fields actually land. Skull thickness, cerebrospinal-fluid thickness, cortical thickness, local gyrification, and sulcal depth all matter. The useful read is that standardized tDCS protocols do not deliver standardized brain dose. The caveat is that this remains a simulation and targeting paper, not an outcome paper.

The broader takeaway is that neuromodulation gets more credible when papers respect state and geometry. The tinnitus digital twin asks which target changes which pathological state. The diffuse-input control paper shows that realistic spread changes the control problem itself. The Tourette adaptive deep brain stimulation case suggests that slow circadian adaptation may be more deployable than flashier closed-loop fantasies. And the tDCS montage paper reminds us that anatomy still decides a lot of what the protocol really is.
