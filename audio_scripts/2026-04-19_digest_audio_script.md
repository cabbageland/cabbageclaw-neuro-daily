The pattern today is that the most useful papers are the ones that improve target selection logic rather than just reporting another average treatment effect. The strongest work helps decide where to stimulate, how to rank candidate contacts, or how to think about symptom-specific networks. The clinical paper with the flashiest headline is still the one that needs the most restraint.

Here is the ranked list.

The first paper is directly relevant, titled, Multimodal Image Guidance in Subthalamic Deep Brain Stimulation for Parkinson's Disease. This is the most relevant paper today. The core question is a practical one: can imaging help choose the best stimulation contact within a patient, instead of merely explaining why some patients do better than others? The authors combine contact coordinates, modeled electric fields, tract activation estimates, and structural and functional network features in a ridge-regression framework. In unseen group-level data, the model explains only about twelve percent of the variance, so this is not a great general outcome predictor. But the more important result is that, at the individual level, it identifies the optimal clinical contact or a neighboring contact in all but one case. That is still imperfect, but it is clinically meaningful. It suggests that multimodal imaging may become useful as a programming aid rather than just as a retrospective storytelling machine.

The second paper is directly relevant, titled, Non-invasive Neuromodulation Targeting Approach by Mapping Stimulations and Lesions That Modify Visual Memory. This is not a treatment trial. It is a target-discovery study. The authors combine data from transcranial magnetic stimulation, penetrating head trauma, and ischemic stroke, then look for a shared connectivity-defined network associated with visual-memory change. They report convergence onto a visual-memory network and, more interestingly, they report that the direction of the effect flips depending on age at the time of injury or stimulation. That age interaction is then replicated in an independent preclinical Alzheimer’s disease dataset. The practical point is not that the paper discovers one perfect memory target. The practical point is that it uses perturbation-based evidence to derive candidate targets more honestly than most memory-stimulation papers do.

The third paper is directly relevant, titled, Transcranial temporal interference stimulation targeting the subthalamic region for motor symptoms in Parkinson's disease: a pilot, randomised, double-blind, sham-controlled crossover study. This is one of the sharper early human temporal-interference papers. Thirty people with Parkinson's disease, off medication, received a single individualized twenty-minute stimulation session or sham in crossover order. The active condition beat sham on short-term motor improvement and responder rate, with no serious adverse events. That is genuinely interesting. But it is still a small acute pilot, and the deeper question remains unresolved: did temporal interference selectively modulate the subthalamic target, or did it produce a broader field effect that happened to help motor symptoms in the short term? So this should update curiosity more than confidence.

A fourth paper stayed below full-note threshold, titled, Higher-order intrinsic routes support flexible task-evoked communication. The idea is that task-specific communication may arise not from wholesale network rewiring, but from selective recruitment of indirect higher-order routes already embedded in the resting-state scaffold. That is a useful systems-level framing for intervention logic, but it is still too far from direct stimulation evidence to preserve as a core note today.

The broader takeaway is simple. Precision neuromodulation papers become valuable when they narrow real intervention choices. The image-guided subthalamic DBS paper matters because it tries to rank contacts within patients. The visual-memory mapping paper matters because it derives targets from convergent perturbation logic and keeps heterogeneity, especially age, in view. The temporal-interference Parkinson's paper is worth tracking, but it is still an early pilot in a domain where anatomy and mechanism are easy to oversell.
