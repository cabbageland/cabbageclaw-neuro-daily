Today’s pattern is simple. Target names are cheap. The harder and more valuable work is figuring out which brain state matters, whether the brain actually reacts to stimulation in a useful way, and whether the hardware can physically deliver the field people keep talking about.

The most relevant paper today is titled, Temporal interference stimulation for major depressive disorder: right lingual gyrus as a predictor. This is a multicenter randomized double-blind sham-controlled depression trial with seventy-six patients. The useful part is not the usual deep-target marketing. It is the attempt to explain heterogeneity. Within the active group, responders had lower baseline low-frequency fluctuation amplitude in the right lingual gyrus, and that feature gave a modest response-prediction signal. My read is that this is more valuable as a state-prediction paper than as proof that temporal interference has solved deep noninvasive psychiatry. The main caveat is that the biomarker looks moderate rather than robust, and the mechanistic link from lingual gyrus baseline state to right-amygdala-targeted stimulation is still thin.

The second paper is titled, Utilizing stimulation-evoked hemodynamic activity to predict antidepressant response to intermittent theta-burst stimulation in adults with major depression. This one is smaller, with forty-four depressed participants plus healthy controls for reliability analysis, but the logic is sharper. The authors measure prefrontal hemodynamic response during concurrent theta-burst stimulation and functional near-infrared spectroscopy, then ask whether that immediate evoked response predicts who improves after four weeks of treatment. That is a better biomarker posture because it sits closer to target engagement. The classifier metrics sound strong, but the reliability range is mixed enough that I would treat this as promising instrumentation logic, not deployment-ready precision treatment.

The third paper is titled, Model-based analysis of stop-signal data reveals robust neural and clinical correlates of evidence accumulation but not inhibition. This is the main adjacent computational paper. It uses a richer stop-signal model in the large IMAGEN cohort and argues that the clinically meaningful signal lives more in evidence accumulation efficiency, response caution, and go-failure variables than in classic inhibition speed. The reason this matters is that a lot of psychiatry and intervention rhetoric still talks as if poor inhibition is the central latent problem. This paper says that may be the wrong variable.

The fourth paper is titled, A Self-adaptive ultrasound patch for transcranial multimodal dynamic focusing. This is a methods paper, not a therapeutic result. I kept it because it addresses a real constraint. If wearable transcranial ultrasound cannot conform to irregular head geometry while preserving dynamic focusing and phase compensation, then a lot of future closed-loop talk is physically unserious. The paper looks useful as hardware groundwork, not as proof of efficacy.

The takeaway is that good neuromodulation work is getting harder to fake. You need a target, but you also need a relevant state variable, a believable readout of engagement, and hardware that can actually do what the control story assumes. Today’s strongest papers each help with one part of that chain, but none of them close it end to end.
