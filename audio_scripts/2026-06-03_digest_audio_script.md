Short overview
The top keep today is Principles of Physiologic Closed-Loop Controllers in Neuromodulation in Neuromodulation. This is not a flashy new device paper. It is better than that. The useful move is to operationalize adaptive neuromodulation as a control-and-risk stack: stimulation dose is amplitude plus waveform/timing plus location; biomarkers can be reactive or predictive; control can be major-loop or minor-loop; and every automated loop needs explicit monitoring, actuation limits, fallback modes, and logs. The paper also earns extra credit for being more concrete than a generic perspective, because it walks through RNS for epilepsy, adaptive Parkinson control, and ECAP-guided spinal cord stimulation instead of hiding behind slogans.
The freshest clinical keep is Low-intensity focused ultrasound targeting striatal circuits in schizophrenia: feasibility, safety, and effects on hallucinations and striatal-temporal functional connectivity in Brain Stimulation. I inspected the accessible full medRxiv version corresponding to the later journal publication. The study is a first-in-human randomized within-subject crossover feasibility trial with only three schizophrenia patients, so nobody sane should read it as efficacy proof. But it is still worth preserving because it tests a direct mechanistic idea: if auditory hallucinations depend partly on aberrant striatal to superior temporal coupling, can noninvasive deep-target LIFU acutely shift that circuit and move symptoms with it? The reported answer is provisionally yes, with more than fifty percent acute symptom reductions after active nucleus accumbens and caudate-head stimulation and concurrent decreases in striatal-temporal connectivity, but the sample is too small and the sham comparisons too fragile to claim much more than proof of concept.
The third paper in the ranking is the existing Beyond where: When and how brain stimulation drives state transitions note. I am reusing it rather than writing a new note because it is still the right computational anchor for the day. The closed-loop-controller paper says control systems need explicit biomarkers, setpoints, and failure handling. The schizophrenia ultrasound paper says deep targets only matter insofar as they change a symptom-relevant circuit state. The state-transition paper provides the sharper baseline idea behind both: good intervention logic is about moving a system through a dynamical landscape, not just stimulating a named coordinate and hoping the story writes itself.

The CBT-plus-interventional side produced a UCLA pilot of coaching plus rTMS for depression, but the signal was adjunctive, small, and less interesting than the two papers above.

Most relevant paper
Principles of Physiologic Closed-Loop Controllers in Neuromodulation
This gets top rank because it is the most durable thing surfaced today. The schizophrenia ultrasound paper is more immediately dramatic, but it is still a tiny feasibility signal. The controller paper is a better long-horizon keep because it sharpens how future adaptive-neuromodulation papers should be read at all. Its best contribution is not any one device example. It is the demand that a serious closed-loop paper must say what dose is being adjusted, what kind of biomarker is being used, what failure modes are anticipated, what fallback behavior exists, and whether the loop is actually controlling a symptom-relevant variable or just a convenient proxy. That is a good standard, and the field badly needs one.
Novelty / framing / baseline impact
For adaptive neuromodulation: the PCLC paper gives a cleaner vocabulary for separating reactive versus predictive biomarkers, major-loop versus minor-loop control, and human-in-the-loop versus human-on-the-loop monitoring.
For interventional psychiatry: the striatal LIFU paper is a useful proof-of-concept because it pairs a deep target with a symptom-relevant circuit hypothesis instead of pretending that deep access alone is enough.
For computational control framing: today still supports the state-transition baseline that controllability is dynamical, not merely anatomical.
For CBT and hypnosis interest checks: the fresh material I found was weaker than the main picks. The rTMS-plus-coaching pilot suggests adjunctive coaching may help, but it does not yet establish a strong sequencing or mechanistic complementarity story. The new hypnosis framework sharpens trait labels more than it sharpens intervention logic.
One-paragraph takeaway
The useful pattern today is that better intervention papers start by admitting what kind of system they are actually manipulating. The closed-loop paper says adaptive neuromodulation needs explicit biomarkers, dose variables, and fallback behavior. The schizophrenia ultrasound paper says a deep target only matters if it changes a symptom-relevant circuit and not just because it sounds impressive. And the state-transition anchor says both claims should be understood dynamically, not cartographically. Put together, the right lesson is simple: define the variable, define the controller, define the state, then talk about therapy.
