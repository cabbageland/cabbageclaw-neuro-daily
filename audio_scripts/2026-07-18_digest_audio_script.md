The top fresh preserve is titled, Device-embedded accelerometry complements neural signals for tracking parkinsonian motor states. This is a July ninth bioRxiv preprint, and it gets the top rank because it asks a better adaptive deep brain stimulation question than many decoder papers ask. Instead of assuming that a neural biomarker discovered off stimulation stays equally useful once therapy is active, it tests which signals remain informative when the intervention is already changing the circuit.

The core setup is strong. The authors analyze more than nineteen hundred hours of chronic home recordings from Parkinson’s implants with subthalamic and cortical sensing, plus the pulse generator’s own embedded accelerometer. They pair those signals with continuous wearable estimates of bradykinesia and dyskinesia, then compare neural-only, accelerometry-only, and combined decoders before and during continuous stimulation.

The useful result is not just that accelerometry works. The useful result is that several familiar neural features become less trustworthy once stimulation is already on. In particular, total subthalamic beta turns out to be too crude because it mixes periodic and aperiodic processes that point in opposite behavioral directions. Periodic beta behaves like a familiar bradykinesia marker off stimulation, but it degrades on stimulation. Aperiodic subthalamic features, some cortical periodic features, and cortico-subthalamic coherence survive better. The implanted accelerometer survives best of all.

That makes this paper a good complement to existing archive anchors. Activity-dependent adaptive deep brain stimulation improves gait in Parkinson’s disease still gives the clearest argument that adaptive control should switch policies across behavioral contexts instead of thresholding one generic marker. Bayesian time-history modeling enhances Parkinsonian motor state classification for adaptive deep brain stimulation still matters because temporal continuity improves decoding without breaking real-time feasibility. And Long-Term Personalized Adaptive Deep Brain Stimulation in Parkinson Disease remains the important proof that chronic sensing-driven stimulation can actually run at home. Today’s paper adds a different constraint. The signal itself has to stay legible under therapy.

Fresh but not preserved today, Functional segregation in Parkinson’s disease looks like a strong systems paper about dopaminergic reorganization, but it sharpens disease organization more than controller design. The c T B S alcohol use disorder relapse paper looks ambitious from the abstract and metadata, but I did not get enough direct full-text traction today to treat it as a preserve-level note. The hypnosis and cognitive behavioral therapy plus intervention lanes were checked too, and the fresh material was mostly pilots, moderator analyses, protocols, or broad overviews rather than stronger mechanism papers than the current archive anchors.

The main takeaway is simple. Closed-loop neuromodulation should stop treating biomarker choice like a pre-therapy beauty contest. The question is not only which signal correlates with symptoms when the device is quiet. The question is which signal still helps when the device is already pushing on the system. This paper gives a plausible answer. Implanted accelerometry may be the stable behavioral backbone, while decomposed neural features add circuit specificity where they remain trustworthy.
