Welcome to the June 13 Neuro Daily at Cabbageland!

Today's pattern is simple. Stop calling long-horizon plasticity session drift.

The one new preserve today is directly relevant, titled, Inferring brain plasticity rule under long-term stimulation with structured recurrent dynamics. This paper matters because it goes after a problem that a lot of closed-loop and longitudinal stimulation work politely ignores. Repeated stimulation probably changes the circuit that future stimulation will act on. If that is true, then the right object is not just the fast neural state inside one session. The right object is also the slow law governing how the circuit itself changes across sessions.

That is exactly what the authors try to model. They build a framework called STEER that separates fast within-session neural activity from slow across-session plasticity. The fast part is handled with a low-rank recurrent model. The slow part is treated as a stimulus-conditioned latent recurrence that updates low-dimensional plasticity embeddings over sessions. In plain English, they are trying to learn not only how the system behaves now, but how the system's own transition rules are being edited by repeated stimulation.

The good news is that the paper does not only wave its hands. The model is tested on synthetic Lorenz systems, on BCM-style plasticity simulations, on a task-learning benchmark where the stimulation policy is itself optimized, and on a longitudinal Parkinsonian rat dataset with closed-loop subthalamic DBS. Across those settings, STEER looks better at tracking slow cross-session change than the comparison models while still keeping strong short-timescale prediction. The main caveat is that the strongest proof still comes from synthetic settings. The real-data anchor is useful, but it is still a modest rat DBS dataset, not a decisive human intervention study. So this is a strong methods direction, not solved biology.

The second paper is a recent clinical context note, titled, Deep brain stimulation induces white matter remodeling and functional changes to brain-wide networks. I am keeping it in today's stack because it gives the slow-plasticity story actual tissue. In macaques, chronic depression-style subcallosal cingulate stimulation appears to change cingulum microstructure, myelination markers, and distributed functional connectivity. That does not prove a human antidepressant mechanism. But it does push the field away from the lazy idea that chronic stimulation is just repeated acute node zapping.

The third paper is a recent network-control context note, titled, Controlling the human connectome with spatially diffuse input signals. Its contribution is quiet but important. A lot of brain-control theory assumes an input acts on one perfect point in the network. Real stimulation does not work like that. Fields spread, tissue is continuous, and nearby regions get dragged along. This paper bakes that spatial blur into the control model and shows that the control landscape changes a lot when you do. That is a much healthier default for thinking about TMS, tDCS, ultrasound, or any other not-perfectly-focal intervention.

The fourth paper is a recent methods context note, titled, Effective correction of extreme capacitive artifacts in TMS-EEG via windowed detrending. This one is here as a warning label. A perturbational biomarker is only as good as the cleanup stack beneath it. The paper argues that standard ICA pipelines can fail badly when capacitive artifacts get severe, and that separately modeling the rise and decay phases of the artifact works better in those ugly regimes. That is the kind of boring measurement paper that saves you from fake mechanism later.

I also checked the standing-interest lanes. The most relevant CBT-plus-interventional hits today were a randomized esketamine-plus-CBT relapse-prevention trial, a psilocybin-assisted CBT pilot, and the already known coaching-plus-rTMS pilot. None of them earned a preserve today. They still read more like feasibility, service design, or manualization than like sharp mechanism, targeting, or sequencing papers. Targeted hypnosis and hypnotherapy searching mostly surfaced reviews or theory-heavy preprints rather than a new biomarker or circuit paper worth keeping.

The main takeaway is this. If you want serious intervention logic, you need three kinds of realism at once. First, plasticity realism: repeated stimulation edits the system over time. Second, actuator realism: stimulation is spatially diffuse, not a magic point source. Third, measurement realism: perturbational biomarkers collapse if the artifact model is weak. The new STEER paper is useful because it pushes the first of those three in a more explicit and less lazy direction.
