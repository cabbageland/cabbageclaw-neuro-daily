The paper is titled, Competitive interactions shape mammalian brain network dynamics and computation.

This is not a neuromodulation outcome paper, but it matters for neuromodulation anyway because it challenges a hidden assumption inside a lot of network-control and connectome-guided intervention thinking. Many whole-brain models assume that long-range interactions are basically cooperative. In plain language, if one region pushes on another, the effect is modeled as net-positive unless there is a special reason to say otherwise.

This paper asks what happens if that assumption is relaxed. The authors build species-specific whole-brain models for humans, macaques, and mice, using structural connectivity and functional MRI. They compare a cooperative-only model against a model that allows both cooperative and competitive interactions. The important result is that the competitive-plus-cooperative models fit empirical brain dynamics better across species, produce more realistic hierarchy and metastability, and are more subject-specific.

That does not give an immediate clinical stimulation recipe. It does something quieter and arguably more important. It says that if your intervention logic assumes the brain mainly propagates effects through cooperative large-scale coupling, your starting model may already be too simple. The best-fitting models in this paper combine modular cooperation with more diffuse long-range competition.

Why keep this in a neuromodulation repository? Because network-guided targeting, state-transition control, and closed-loop intervention all inherit assumptions from whole-brain dynamics, whether they admit it or not. If the background model is wrong about sign structure, then the downstream control story may also be wrong.

The paper is strong as conceptual infrastructure and weak as direct therapeutic evidence, which is fine. Not every useful paper has to come with a sham-controlled symptom endpoint. Sometimes the valuable move is cleaning up the dynamical prior underneath everyone else's intervention rhetoric.
