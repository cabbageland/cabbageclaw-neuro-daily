Today’s paper is titled, Temporal interference stimulation for major depressive disorder: right lingual gyrus as a predictor.

Why was it selected? Because it is a sham-controlled human temporal interference stimulation paper that is more useful for its response-heterogeneity logic than for generic deep-target branding.

Quick verdict. Highly relevant. This is one of the more interesting recent temporal interference papers because it at least tries to connect outcome to a measurable baseline brain state instead of pretending anatomy alone solves depression treatment. The caveat is that the biomarker is still modest and statistically fragile.

Here is the overview. This paper reports a secondary analysis from a multicenter randomized double-blind sham-controlled trial of right-amygdala-targeted temporal interference stimulation in major depressive disorder. Seventy-six patients were randomized across two hospitals, with baseline resting-state functional MRI and symptom ratings. Within the active arm, responders and nonresponders were compared to identify baseline imaging differences, and lower amplitude of low-frequency fluctuations in the right lingual gyrus emerged as the main candidate predictor of later symptom improvement. The paper is useful because it asks the right question, namely why some patients benefit, but the biomarker remains only moderately convincing.

What problem is the paper trying to solve? It is trying to determine whether temporal interference stimulation has therapeutic value in depression and whether baseline brain-state features can predict who responds.

What is the method? The authors ran a multicenter randomized double-blind sham-controlled depression trial, then performed a secondary imaging analysis within the active arm to compare baseline resting-state functional MRI features between responders and nonresponders.

What data does it use? Seventy-six major-depression patients from two clinical sites, with baseline resting-state functional MRI and Hamilton Depression Rating Scale measurements, plus follow-up outcomes at weeks three and eight.

How is it evaluated? By comparing baseline low-frequency fluctuation amplitude between responders and nonresponders, correlating that marker with symptom improvement, and testing predictive performance with logistic regression and receiver-operating-characteristic analysis.

What are the main results? Fourteen patients, about forty-four percent of the active group, were classified as responders. Responders had lower baseline right lingual-gyrus low-frequency fluctuation amplitude, and the reported area under the curve was about zero point seven one four.

What is actually novel? Not temporal interference by itself. The more useful novelty is the attempt to connect outcome heterogeneity in a sham-controlled human trial to a baseline imaging marker.

The strengths are the real sham-controlled multicenter design and the explicit heterogeneity analysis. The weaknesses are the small active-arm responder set, the moderate predictive performance, and the thin mechanistic story linking the lingual-gyrus signal to the stimulation target.

Why does this matter for Cabbageland? Because it is a decent example of how a noninvasive deep-target paper gets more interesting when it stops selling anatomy and starts asking about patient-specific state.

Final decision. Keep. Useful as a heterogeneity paper, not as a victory lap for temporal interference.
