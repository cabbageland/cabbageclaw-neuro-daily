Decoding hypnotic consciousness: neural and experiential insights into induced and ideomotor suggestions.

Quick verdict. Highly relevant.

This is a strong keep for the hypnosis and suggestion-mechanism thread. The paper is not clinically large, and the sample is intentionally selected for hypnotic responsiveness, but the measurement stack is exactly the right instinct. It treats hypnosis as a dynamic brain-body-experience transition rather than a generic relaxation state, and it separates disrupted agency from overt motor behavior during an ideomotor challenge.

The study compares resting baseline, hypnotic induction, and an ideomotor arm-rigidity challenge against voluntary wakeful simulation. During induction, EEG changes unfold gradually: parieto-occipital alpha suppression at mid-induction, generalized theta increase at late induction, stronger frontoparietal theta connectivity, and reduced parasympathetic cardiac modulation. That pattern argues against a simple relaxation story and points toward active top-down reorganization.

The ideomotor challenge is the best part. Participants split into tremblers and non-tremblers despite reporting comparable disruptions in agency. Tremblers tried to move while experiencing the action as constrained or involuntary. Non-tremblers often reported that movement felt impossible or that the motor command could not start. In tremblers, the EEG signature included increased frontoparietal gamma and reduced delta connectivity relative to voluntary simulation, consistent with motor conflict or sensorimotor prediction error.

The main caveat is scope. This is a small, selected, healthy sample, and the challenge is one suggestion type. It is mechanistic evidence, not proof of clinical benefit. Still, it gives the hypnosis project a much better vocabulary: induction state, agency disruption, motor strategy, prediction conflict, and autonomic regulation.

Final decision. Keep. This is one of the more useful recent hypnosis papers for turning suggestion into a concrete state-change problem.
