Predictive value of subgenual cingulate normative connectivity to TMS treatment site for antidepressant response in routine clinical practice: a prospective, multisite cohort study.

Basic info. Title: Predictive value of subgenual cingulate normative connectivity to TMS treatment site for antidepressant response in routine clinical practice: a prospective, multisite cohort study. Authors: Sanaz Khosravani and colleagues. Year: 2026. Venue: Molecular Psychiatry. Why selected: it is a valuable real-world pressure test of the anti-subgenual TMS targeting heuristic.

Quick verdict. Highly relevant. This is worth preserving because the main result is mostly negative. In a prospective multisite routine-practice cohort, normative connectivity from the delivered left dorsolateral prefrontal cortex site to subgenual cingulate did not meaningfully predict symptom improvement on its own. That does not invalidate network targeting, but it does weaken the habit of talking as if anti-subgenual targeting is already a robust standalone personalization rule.

One-paragraph overview. The paper asks whether one of the best-known precision-TMS ideas survives contact with routine clinical heterogeneity. Sixty-six patients with major depressive disorder received three to eight weeks of standard-care left dorsolateral prefrontal repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation, with actual stimulation sites recorded using MRI neuronavigation. The authors estimated each site’s functional connectivity to subgenual cingulate from a large normative resting-state dataset and tested whether that predicted improvement in Beck Depression Inventory scores. The main answer is no. The primary association was null, while baseline anxiety and psychiatric comorbidity explained more variance once added in post-hoc models.

Model definition. This paper does not present a trainable predictive model in the accessible abstract text. It combines a normative-connectivity targeting metric with observational outcome analysis.

Inputs. Actual delivered TMS site locations, normative resting-state connectivity between those sites and subgenual cingulate, depression outcome scores, and clinical covariates such as baseline anxiety and psychiatric comorbidity.

Outputs. The main output is the association between site-to-subgenual connectivity and antidepressant response. Secondary outputs are post-hoc models that add clinical covariates.

Training objective, or loss. No trainable optimization loss is specified in the accessible abstract. The analysis is correlational and regression-based.

Architecture or parameterization. Observational cohort analysis using a normative-connectivity metric plus post-hoc multivariable modeling.

What problem is the paper trying to solve? It tests whether a famous network-targeting heuristic for depression TMS actually predicts response in routine practice rather than only in cleaner research cohorts.

What is the method? Patients received daily left dorsolateral prefrontal repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation as part of standard care. Stimulation sites were recorded across multiple days. The authors estimated normative connectivity from each delivered site to subgenual cingulate and tested its relationship to change in depression symptoms, with post-hoc analyses adding clinical covariates.

What is the method motivation? If antidepressant efficacy depends on stimulating a cortical entry point that is functionally anti-correlated with subgenual cingulate, then stronger negative connectivity should predict better outcome. This paper asks whether that story actually holds up in a more clinically messy sample.

What data does it use? Sixty-six treatment-seeking individuals with major depressive disorder, plus a normative resting-state reference from one thousand healthy individuals.

How is it evaluated? The primary evaluation is the correlation between change in Beck Depression Inventory and normative connectivity from the delivered TMS site to subgenual cingulate. Secondary analyses add clinical covariates. The study also checks whether inconsistency of site placement could explain the result.

What are the main results? The primary association is null. Normative site-to-subgenual connectivity does not predict antidepressant response on its own. Site inconsistency does not appear to explain that null result. When clinical covariates are added post hoc, baseline anxiety and psychiatric comorbidity account for much more of the variance.

What is actually novel? The novelty is not the anti-subgenual idea itself. The novelty is the prospective multisite routine-practice test that pressures that idea under realistic heterogeneity.

What are the strengths? It asks a real translational question. It uses actual delivered stimulation sites rather than idealized targets. And it reports an inconvenient answer instead of forcing a success story.

What are the weaknesses, limitations, or red flags? The connectivity metric is normative rather than individualized. The design is observational rather than randomized. Routine-practice protocol heterogeneity may blur signal. And because I inspected abstract-level text only, I do not have a clean read on medication effects, durability, or exact regression details.

What challenges or open problems remain? The big open question is whether individualized connectivity, symptom dimensions, or dynamic state measures outperform static normative connectivity in real clinics.

What future work naturally follows? Prospective comparisons between normative and individualized targeting would be the obvious next step. It would also be useful to test whether anti-subgenual logic works better inside richer multivariable response models instead of as a standalone rule.

Why does this matter for cabbageland? Because intervention logic should survive contact with heterogeneity. This paper shows that a plausible network story can still be too weak to govern personalization on its own.

What ideas are steal-worthy? Treat actual delivered stimulation sites as data. Test precision-neuromodulation claims against heterogeneity variables, not just against prettier connectomic explanations. And take negative translational results seriously.

Final decision. Keep. This is not a triumphant precision-targeting paper, and that is exactly why it is valuable.
