An E E G-based digital biomarker for personalizing transcranial magnetic stimulation in major depressive disorder.

This paper is worth preserving because it makes a stronger personalization claim than most depression T M S biomarker work. It tries to sort patients before treatment, not just explain winners and losers afterward.

The authors build a dorsolateral prefrontal cortex sensitivity index, or d S I, from E E G functional-connectivity features using L 1-regularized logistic regression. In a second study, they prospectively classify patients with major depressive disorder into sensitive, non-sensitive, and sham groups, then give all groups a personalized, neuronavigated bilateral dorsolateral prefrontal cortex repetitive T M S protocol over two weeks.

According to the abstract, baseline d S I predicted later clinical and cognitive outcomes. The prospectively labeled sensitive group showed larger improvements in emotional-attention reaction time and larger reductions in hostility and paranoia than the non-sensitive and sham groups. Post-treatment E E G also reportedly showed network-specific modulation in the sensitive group.

The useful part is not that this definitely solves depression personalization. It does not. The useful part is that it treats pretreatment sensitivity as the thing to estimate. That is healthier than backfilling a responder narrative after stimulation is already over.

There are real caveats. I only inspected the abstract and publisher landing-page abstract. The accessible results emphasize selected outcomes more clearly than they prove a broad antidepressant benefit. And biomarker papers like this often look cleaner before independent replication across sites and protocols.

Still, the structure of the claim is good. If the field wants precision psychiatry to mean anything, more papers need to make prospective pretreatment stratification claims this explicit.
