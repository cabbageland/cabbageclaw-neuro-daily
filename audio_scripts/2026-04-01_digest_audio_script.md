Welcome to the April first Neuro Daily at Cabbageland!

Today is a model-versus-evidence day. The strongest paper is a computational one, not because the benchmark numbers are obviously trustworthy, but because it asks a better question. Instead of treating neuromodulation response as a direct imaging-to-label shortcut, it tries to build individualized virtual brain models and estimate whether stimulation moves each patient toward a healthier network state. The second paper is smaller but more grounded in actual control logic. It looks at whether motor performance in Parkinson's disease can be decoded from invasive and noninvasive brain signals under deep brain stimulation on and off conditions. The third paper is clinically provocative but very small: a two-year follow-up of tract-aware deep brain stimulation for post-traumatic stress disorder in four patients.

Here is the ranked list.

The first paper is directly relevant, titled, Predicting Neuromodulation Outcome for Parkinson's Disease with Generative Virtual Brain Model. This is the most relevant paper today. The useful part is not the phrase foundation model. The useful part is the counterfactual framing. The authors say they pretrained a generative virtual brain model on more than five thousand resting-state sessions, then fine-tuned it on Parkinson's cohorts receiving temporal interference or deep brain stimulation. They report high fidelity to empirical functional connectivity and strong response prediction in both internal and small prospective validation settings. If that survives full-method inspection, the real contribution is that treatment selection is being framed as a personalized state-transition problem rather than another static biomarker benchmark.

The second paper is directly relevant, titled, Invasive and Non-Invasive Neural Decoding of Motor Performance in Parkinson's Disease for Personalized Deep Brain Stimulation. This one is less flashy and more operational. Nineteen patients performed a drawing task across sessions with deep brain stimulation on and off. The authors used patient-specific decoding from electroencephalography and, in a much smaller subset, electrocorticography. They report significant decoding in most sessions and show that stimulation changed the speed-accuracy trade-off, increasing speed while reducing accuracy. That matters because adaptive deep brain stimulation should not only ask whether a neural signal is decodable. It should ask what behavioral variable is being optimized, and what gets worse when something else improves.

The third paper is directly relevant, titled, Long-term outcome of subgenual cingulum and uncinate fasciculus deep brain stimulation for the treatment of post-traumatic stress disorder. This is the clinically interesting paper with the loudest caveat. The target rationale is at least mechanistically legible, and two-year follow-up in psychiatric deep brain stimulation is worth noticing. The authors report three long-term responders and one partial responder out of four patients, with sustained improvement in post-traumatic stress symptoms, depression, and functioning. But the sample is four. So this belongs in the keep-for-context bucket, not the now-we-know bucket.

A fourth paper is mostly citation material, titled, Standalone Augmented Reality Neuronavigation System for Accurate Pulse Delivery in Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation. It is useful as infrastructure because cheaper and easier navigation could matter for real-world targeting quality. But the evidence here is phantom non-inferiority on pulse-delivery accuracy, not clinical improvement.

The broader takeaway is simple. The best papers today are the ones that make intervention logic explicit. The virtual brain paper says response prediction should depend on how stimulation changes an individualized network model. The motor decoding paper says adaptive control should stay tied to actual behavior under real stimulation conditions. The post-traumatic stress disorder paper says tract-aware psychiatric targeting may be worth pursuing, but the evidence is still feasibility-level. So the real progress today is not stronger efficacy proof. It is better problem formulation.
