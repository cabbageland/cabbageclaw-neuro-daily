Today’s paper is titled, Model-based analysis of stop-signal data reveals robust neural and clinical correlates of evidence accumulation but not inhibition.

Why was it selected? Because it is a useful computational psychiatry correction. It argues that broad decision-efficiency variables explain more of the neural and clinical signal than the field’s usual inhibitory-control metric.

Quick verdict. Useful. This is not a stimulation paper, but it is good intervention-adjacent cleanup. The paper makes an uncomfortable point: when you fit a richer mechanistic model to stop-signal behavior, the clinically meaningful signal seems to live more in evidence accumulation and response-caution variables than in canonical inhibition speed.

Here is the overview. Using the large longitudinal IMAGEN cohort, the authors apply the RDEX-A-B-C-D model, a racing diffusion ex-Gaussian framework, to stop-signal task behavior at ages nineteen and twenty-three. They relate the resulting latent decision-process parameters to substance use with elastic-net regression and identify predictive functional-connectivity patterns with connectome-based predictive modeling. The central result is that conventional inhibition-focused parameters were weakly linked to substance use and brain connectivity, while more general decision-making variables such as evidence accumulation efficiency, response caution, and go-failure probability carried stronger signal.

What problem is the paper trying to solve? It is trying to determine which latent cognitive processes measured by stop-signal behavior actually relate to substance-use vulnerability and its neural correlates.

What is the method? Fit a richer mechanistic stop-signal model to behavior in a large longitudinal cohort, then test how the resulting latent parameters relate to substance use and distributed brain connectivity.

What data does it use? The IMAGEN longitudinal youth cohort, with more than one thousand participants, stop-signal task data at ages nineteen and twenty-three, and associated functional connectivity.

How is it evaluated? By relating model parameters to substance-use outcomes with elastic-net regression and by identifying predictive whole-brain connectivity patterns with connectome-based predictive modeling.

What are the main results? Parameters indexing inhibitory control had weak or no meaningful links to substance use and only weak connectivity associations. In contrast, evidence accumulation efficiency, decision threshold, and go-failure-related parameters were stronger predictors of cannabis and cigarette use and had clearer network correlates.

Why does this matter for Cabbageland? Because it sharpens the language around what an intervention might actually be trying to change. Improving inhibition may be too blunt if the real lever is broader decision efficiency or response caution.

Final decision. Keep as adjacent infrastructure. It is not a treatment paper, but it improves how future psychiatry and neuromodulation claims should be framed.
