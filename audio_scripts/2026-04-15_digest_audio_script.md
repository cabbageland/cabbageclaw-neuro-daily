Today is a stop-treating-targeting-folklore-as-validation day. The most relevant paper is titled, Predictive value of subgenual cingulate normative connectivity to TMS treatment site for antidepressant response in routine clinical practice: a prospective, multisite cohort study. The reason it matters is simple. The field has repeated the anti-subgenual targeting story for years, often as if it were already a robust personalization rule. This paper tests that story in a more heterogeneous routine-practice sample and gets a mostly negative answer.

Sixty-six patients with major depressive disorder received three to eight weeks of standard-care left dorsolateral prefrontal repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation. The actual stimulation sites were recorded with MRI neuronavigation. The authors then estimated each site’s normative functional connectivity to subgenual cingulate using a large healthy reference dataset. The main result is that this connectivity measure did not meaningfully predict symptom improvement on its own. Once clinical covariates were added, baseline anxiety and psychiatric comorbidity explained more of the variance. That does not prove network targeting is useless. But it does say the simple anti-subgenual heuristic is too thin for messy real clinics.

The second paper is directly relevant, titled, Causal connectivity maps derived from single-pulse interleaved TMS functional MRI. This is a methods paper, but it is the right kind. In more than eighty participants, the authors selected personalized cortical targets intended to engage either subgenual anterior cingulate cortex or basolateral amygdala, then measured TMS-evoked blood-oxygen-level-dependent responses while controlling for motion, pain, and somatosensory confounds. The useful result is not that they found a magic psychiatric target. The useful result is that image-guided TMS can generate reproducible downstream circuit maps, which is a more causal complement to the usual resting-connectivity targeting story.

The third paper is directly relevant, titled, Controlling the human connectome with spatially diffuse input signals. This is the computational-network paper for today. Most brain-network control models assume inputs act on isolated nodes, but real stimulation spreads through space. The authors change the model so inputs decay with distance instead of staying perfectly local. Under that assumption, brain-state transitions often require less energy and fewer effective input signals, and the resulting control maps line up with independent functional, metabolic, genetic, and neurochemical gradients. The reason this matters is that intervention logic built on unrealistic point-input assumptions may already be optimizing the wrong object.

There is also one paper that stayed below note threshold, titled, Clinical utility and prospective of TMS electroencephalography: updated review from an international expert group. It is reasonable field-status citation material, but it is still a review, not a preserve-worthy mechanistic advance.

The main takeaway today is that better neuromodulation logic will require more respect for heterogeneity, more causal measurement, and more realistic control assumptions. It is not enough to have a pretty target story. The target story has to survive real patients, real perturbation data, and real stimulation physics.
