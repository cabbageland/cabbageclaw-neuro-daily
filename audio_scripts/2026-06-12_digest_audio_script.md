Today's pattern is this. Brain-state control needs real kinematics, real perturbation fingerprints, and less adjunct-theory mush.

The strongest paper is directly relevant, titled, A week in the life of the human brain reveals stable states punctuated by chaotic-like transitions. This is the top keep because it gives a serious picture of how large-scale neural states actually move. Instead of treating the brain as a series of static connectivity maps, it follows week-long intracranial recordings in twenty people and shows that brain activity spends long periods in relatively stable states, then breaks into rapid, chaotic, and circuitous transitions when behavior changes. The authors use a deep-learning and Koopman-operator modeling stack to argue that slow center-manifold dynamics track physiology and wake-sleep depth, while fast off-manifold dynamics carry exploratory transitions. The strongest practical point is that sleep deprivation makes those transitions more chaotic and more circuitous. That matters because it turns intervention timing and state estimation into first-class problems instead of afterthoughts.

The second paper is directly relevant, titled, OFC-induced network modularity improves positive symptoms and attentional alertness in schizophrenia: a combined rTMS-fMRI study. This is the most intervention-facing paper in today's set. It comes from a randomized, double-blind, sham-controlled right orbitofrontal cortex stimulation trial in first-episode, drug-naive schizophrenia. The clinical signal is real enough to keep. Symptoms improved more in the active group, cognition improved more in the active group, and the network analysis found a selective reduction in orbitofrontal to default-mode integration, especially in anterior default-mode regions centered on ventromedial prefrontal cortex. The useful part is that the paper at least tries to connect a psychiatric stimulation target to a specific downstream circuit effect. The main caveat is that the more elaborate mechanism story, especially the mediation and Granger analyses, is still exploratory glue rather than hard proof.

The third paper is directly relevant, titled, Loss of regional theta differentiation in TMS-EEG response marks network dysfunction in psychosis risk. This one is the clean perturbational biomarker paper. Healthy controls showed a site-specific theta hierarchy after stimulation. The biggest theta response came from left dorsolateral prefrontal stimulation, then left posterior parietal, then dorsomedial prefrontal. The clinical high-risk group lost that differentiation because the theta response flattened upward across sites. Gamma did not carry the story. The value here is that psychosis-risk circuitry becomes a direct perturbation question instead of another generic dysconnectivity claim. The authors interpret the elevated theta response as potentially compensatory, which is plausible but not yet proven.

Put together, the three papers form a decent stack. The first says real brains do not change state by taking neat straight lines. The second says a psychiatric stimulation target may help by selectively decoupling orbitofrontal and default-mode communication. The third says early dysfunction can look like losing site-specific perturbation fingerprints. That is a much better set of ideas for intervention design than static connectome theater.

I also checked the standing-interest lanes. A recent pilot paper on mental health coaching plus repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation for depression includes an internet-based cognitive behavioral therapy arm, but it is still a small adjunct-services study and not a serious mechanistic psychotherapy-plus-intervention paper. And targeted recent searching did not surface a new hypnosis or hypnotherapy paper worth preserving today.

The main takeaway is simple. If you want better interventions, stop flattening everything into static connectivity. Estimate state better, respect transition geometry, and ask whether a circuit preserves meaningful perturbation structure under direct stimulation.
