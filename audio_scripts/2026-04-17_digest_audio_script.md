The pattern today is that the better papers refuse one-dimensional targeting logic. They do not just ask where to stimulate. They ask whether the target is actually reachable, whether it sits inside the right network scaffold, and whether the brain is in the right dynamical regime to move at all.

Here is the ranked list.

The first paper is directly relevant, titled, Low-intensity transcranial focused ultrasound amygdala neuromodulation: a double-blind sham-controlled target engagement study and unblinded single-arm clinical trial. This is the most relevant paper today. The reason is simple. Noninvasive psychiatric neuromodulation still has a credibility problem when it claims to affect deep subcortical circuits without proving that it reached them. This paper first uses MRI-guided focused ultrasound to the left amygdala during functional MRI in a double-blind within-subject sham-controlled design, with twenty-nine patients and twenty-three healthy comparison participants. Active stimulation reduced left-amygdala blood-oxygen-level-dependent signal on average and also produced patient-related hippocampal and insular differences. The same patient group then went through fifteen repetitive ultrasound sessions over three weeks in an open-label treatment phase. That part is not a clean efficacy test, so the symptom gains should be treated cautiously. But the intervention was reportedly well tolerated, no serious adverse events were reported, symptom scores improved, and amygdala responses to emotional faces decreased. The useful point is not that focused ultrasound psychiatry is solved. The useful point is that this paper treats deep-target engagement as part of the therapeutic claim rather than a decorative side note.

The second paper is directly relevant, titled, Structural and Functional Connectivity Predict the Effects of Direct Brain Stimulation on Memory. This is a strong network-and-closed-loop paper because it makes timing and target embedding answer to the same intervention logic. The authors analyzed fifty adults with epilepsy across sixty-one intracranial stimulation sessions during a verbal delayed free-recall task. Closed-loop stimulation delivered during classifier-detected poor encoding states improved recall on average, while random stimulation did not. More importantly, the sites that produced greater memory benefit were the ones more strongly structurally coupled to a distributed verbal-encoding network. Functional connectivity pointed in a similar direction but looked weaker than structural embedding. The reason this matters is that adaptive timing is not magic. A good trigger does not rescue a bad target.

The third paper is directly relevant, titled, Beyond where: When and how brain stimulation drives state transitions. This is a mechanistic modeling preprint rather than an intervention paper, but the framing is better than most. The authors fit subject-specific whole-brain Hopf models to magnetoencephalography data across five frequency bands and ask why some regions respond more strongly to stimulation and which nodes are best for moving between brain states. Their answer is that optimal targets are not defined mainly by functional labels. They are defined by dynamical regime. Regions with smaller oscillation radius and greater temporal variability respond more strongly, and the relevant determinants differ by state and frequency band. Slower frequencies look more phase-driven, while faster ones depend more on network synchrony. That does not immediately produce a clinic-ready protocol, but it is a much healthier control-theoretic frame than static target folklore.

A final methods paper stayed below full-note threshold, titled, Multimodal Imaging-Based Targeting Approach for Network-Level Brain Stimulation. It looks useful as a targeting scaffold because it combines resting-state and task functional MRI to ask whether a nominal focal transcranial direct current stimulation target actually overlaps the network territory one cares about. But it reads more like a sensible workflow than a decisive intervention result, so I kept it as adjacent inspiration rather than promoting it.

The broader takeaway is this. Precision neuromodulation needs at least three things at once. First, a target that can actually be reached, not just named. Second, a target that is structurally or functionally embedded in the computation or circuit you want to change. Third, the right state logic, because even a good target may be poorly controllable in the wrong dynamical regime. The amygdala ultrasound paper is strongest on reachability and direct engagement. The memory-stimulation paper is strongest on network embedding. The state-transition paper is strongest on when and how a perturbation can move the system.
