Today's pattern is phase honesty before network bravado.

The most relevant new preserve is directly relevant, titled, Phase estimation with autoregressive padding, or PEAP, addressing inaccuracies and biases in EEG analysis. This paper matters because a lot of phase-dependent EEG-TMS and closed-loop timing work quietly assumes the phase estimator is already trustworthy. The authors show that several common methods can systematically overproduce some phases and shift others in time. Their fix is straightforward and good. Instead of filtering first and then trying to repair the damaged edge of the signal, they pad the unfiltered signal with an autoregressive forecast before filtering. That pushes the strongest filtering artifact away from the sample you actually care about. In seventy-two participants spanning young controls, older controls, and chronic stroke, that approach performs best on accuracy and largely avoids the worst phase-bin biases. The main caveat is that this is still offline validation, not a full real-time deployment. But it is exactly the kind of calibration paper that matters because bad phase estimation can quietly poison the rest of the intervention story.

The second paper is adjacent inspiration, titled, QUIET: Quantifying Underutilized Influential Edges for Targeted Synchronization. This is a network-control paper, but it asks a better question than the usual node-centric framing. Instead of pretending the main issue is which brain region to push, it asks which white-matter connections are structurally influential while still being functionally underused. The paper calls those quiet highways. The framework combines edge controllability with mutual information between the endpoint time series, then searches for edge sets that can synchronize a target network with less off-target disruption. It performs well across synthetic topologies, then gets used on Human Connectome Project data and a dexmedetomidine sedation cohort. The useful idea is pathway selectivity. The main caution is that the whole result still lives inside tractography, coupled phase oscillators, and model-derived control energy. So this is a good hypothesis generator, not a stimulation oracle.

The third paper is an existing clinical anchor, titled, Deep brain stimulation induces white matter remodeling and functional changes to brain-wide networks. I am keeping it in today's stack because it ties long-horizon intervention logic to actual tissue and network change. Chronic subcallosal cingulate stimulation in macaques appears to alter cingulum microstructure and rebalance distributed functional connectivity. That is not clinical proof, but it is much better than talking about chronic stimulation as if it were just repeated acute perturbation with a fancier schedule.

The fourth paper is an existing computational anchor, titled, Inferring brain plasticity rule under long-term stimulation with structured recurrent dynamics. This one matters because it treats repeated stimulation as a system-identification problem for a moving circuit law. Instead of hiding across-session change inside vague drift, it tries to learn an explicit slow plasticity dynamic. That is exactly the kind of framing that becomes more plausible once you take the white-matter remodeling story seriously.

I also checked the standing-interest lanes. Targeted hypnosis and hypnotherapy searching mostly surfaced heart-rate-variability-heavy or review-style material rather than a new biomarker or circuit paper worth preserving. Targeted CBT-plus-interventional psychiatry searching mostly surfaced feasibility, relapse-prevention, or manualization-scale work, including a psilocybin-assisted CBT pilot, rather than a sharp mechanism or sequencing paper.

The main takeaway is this. If you want serious intervention logic, measurement honesty has to come first. PEAP says your phase variable can be biased before the stimulation even happens. QUIET says your control pathway assumptions may also be too crude if you only think in nodes. The existing SCC DBS and STEER notes then remind us that repeated intervention can reshape tissue and circuit law over time. Put differently, if the sensed state is biased and the assumed pathway is sloppy, the controller may still look smart on paper while remaining basically folklore.
