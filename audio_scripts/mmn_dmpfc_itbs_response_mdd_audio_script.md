This paper is titled, Auditory mismatch-negativity predicts response to dorsomedial prefrontal intermittent theta-burst stimulation in major depressive disorder. The quick verdict is, useful.

The reason to keep it is not that it proves a biomarker for depression stimulation. It does not. The reason to keep it is that it asks the predictor question in a more serious way than most of the field does. This is a randomized, double-blind, sham-controlled analysis inside a dorsomedial prefrontal cortex intermittent theta-burst stimulation trial, not just a pile of open-label correlations pretending to be precision medicine.

Here is the paper in plain language. The authors wanted to know whether auditory mismatch negativity, usually shortened to MMN, could work as a physiological indicator of who will respond to this specific TMS protocol. Their mechanistic story is that MMN may reflect prediction-error processing and, more importantly, a kind of plasticity capacity that intermittent theta-burst stimulation may need in order to work well.

The dataset included forty-six adults with unipolar major depressive disorder and sixty-four healthy controls. The patients entered a two-week treatment study with active or sham stimulation over the dorsomedial prefrontal cortex. The intervention ran five days per week with two sessions per day, totaling twenty-four hundred pulses per day at ninety percent resting motor threshold. The MMN task itself was simple and clinically cheap: a one-channel frontal EEG recording during an auditory oddball task with short standard tones and slightly longer deviant tones.

Now the key findings. First, baseline MMN did not separate the depression group from healthy controls. Second, baseline MMN did not correlate with baseline symptom severity. Third, MMN amplitude did not significantly change after active treatment compared with sham. That already cuts against the lazy version of the biomarker story.

But there was one signal worth caring about. In the active treatment group, larger baseline MMN amplitude correlated with greater improvement in depressive symptoms, both on self-reported symptoms and on the clinician-rated affective subscale. In the sham group, that relationship was not there.

That is the paper’s strongest point, and also the place where it needs the most restraint. The active-arm correlation is interesting. But the authors could not show a statistically significant difference between the active and sham correlations themselves. So the correct read is not, we found a clinically deployable predictor. The correct read is, here is a plausible candidate that survives a more serious trial design than usual, but still needs a larger replication before anyone should trust it much.

What makes the paper genuinely useful is that it separates two different ideas that the literature often muddles together. A biomarker can be useful as a baseline predictor without being the thing that visibly changes after treatment. This study did not show that MMN normalizes or shifts with intermittent theta-burst stimulation. It showed, at most, that baseline MMN may tag whether the patient’s system is in a state where the stimulation can do useful plastic work.

The main limitations are important. This is a sub-analysis with limited statistical power. The EEG setup used only one channel, which is practical but methodologically thin. The treatment target was not individualized, and the targeting method even changed partway through the cohort. Follow-up timing varied, and patients stayed on heterogeneous pharmacotherapy. None of that destroys the result, but it does lower confidence and makes overclaiming ridiculous.

Why does this matter for Cabbageland? Because it pushes the depression TMS conversation away from target folklore and toward a more honest question. Not just, where should we stimulate, but also, does this patient have the physiological capacity that this stimulation protocol needs? That is a much better precision question.

The steal-worthy idea is to combine cheap physiological markers like MMN with structural route logic, individualized targeting, and real-world clinical heterogeneity instead of pretending any one layer is enough on its own.

Final decision. Preserve this note as a cautious biomarker paper. It does not validate MMN-guided TMS, but it is a more serious step toward that possibility than most papers in this lane.
