Today's pattern is simple. Stop grading addiction neuromodulation by whether craving looked better for a week.

The most relevant new paper is titled, Neural Response to Theta-Burst Stimulation Predicts Long-Term Relapse in Patients With Alcohol Use Disorder: A Pilot fMRI Study. I picked it because it forces one study to face three things this lane usually keeps separate: a blinded intervention, a measured post-treatment neural change, and a twelve-month relapse outcome. The quick verdict is highly relevant.

Here is the core result. Twenty-eight patients with alcohol use disorder were randomized to active or sham continuous theta-burst stimulation over the right dorsolateral prefrontal cortex for ten sessions across two weeks. The target was the simple F4 scalp coordinate, so this was not fancy personalized targeting. Even so, the clinical endpoint is more serious than usual. The active group showed lower relapse risk over the following year, with a reported hazard ratio of zero point two one relative to sham.

The mechanistic layer is narrower but still useful. Participants completed an alcohol-cue fMRI task before and after treatment. The key reported interaction was in the right superior frontal gyrus, where active stimulation prevented the cue-induced hyperactivity that showed up after sham. Then the paper goes one step further and trains a relapse model on intervention-induced whole-brain cue-reactivity changes. It reports seventy-eight point seven percent accuracy with an area under the curve of zero point nine zero three, and the feature carrying the strongest relapse signal was increased post-treatment cue reactivity in the left medial prefrontal cortex.

That does not mean the paper solved addiction TMS. The caveats matter. The sample is small. The target was a generic scalp landmark instead of individualized circuit guidance. And the active arm started with worse baseline AUDIT scores, which is an annoying imbalance in a pilot this size. But the paper is still useful because it asks the right question. Did stimulation move a relapse-relevant state strongly enough to matter later?

The second ranked note is titled, Target engagement in a head-to-head clinical trial of dysphoric versus anxiosomatic transcranial magnetic stimulation targets. That paper matters because it weakens an easy story. It suggests that what may matter most is not the prettiest pre-post connectivity change after treatment, but whether the chosen stimulation site was well positioned to influence the intended circuit before treatment even started.

The third ranked note is titled, Common Electrophysiology Biomarkers Collected at Home Robustly Track Depression Recovery With Deep Brain Stimulation. That one matters because it shows what serious longitudinal state tracking can look like. Dense at-home sensing makes slow recovery legible in a way weekly symptom guessing does not. That is the right context for thinking about relapse as a tracking problem rather than a single end-of-study label.

The fourth ranked note is titled, A first realization of reinforcement learning-based closed-loop EEG-TMS. I still treat it as adjacent, but it matters because it makes the stimulation state something the controller learns online from feedback instead of something the operator pretends to know in advance. That is exactly the right instinct if relapse-relevant brain states drift across time and patients.

There is also one additional adjacent note worth keeping in view, titled, Brain Functional Connectivity Signatures of Craving Across Substance Use Disorders: A Transdiagnostic Approach. I did not rank it higher today because it stays in the resting-state association lane rather than the intervention lane, but it is still useful as a reminder that craving may have a distributed cross-substance network signature.

The framing impact is straightforward. First, relapse is the right scoreboard for addiction neuromodulation, not just immediate cue or craving changes. Second, post-treatment neural change may be a better biomarker candidate than baseline description alone. Third, target selection should be taken more seriously, because convenience targets are not the same thing as competent targets. And fourth, if we want this field to become engineering rather than ritual, then target choice, online state estimation, and longitudinal monitoring all have to improve together.

The standing-interest lanes were real but weaker. In the hypnosis lane, the best resurfaced paper was Hypnotic safety suggestions reduce cortisol awakening response and morning heart rate in daily life. It is cleaner than average because it has physiology and a brief randomized design, but it is still a healthy-population stress-regulation result rather than a strong interventional-psychiatry keep. In the CBT plus interventional psychiatry lane, the CBT-ENDURE randomized trial of CBT after esketamine looks clinically useful and genuinely feasible, but from the accessible abstract it still reads more like relapse-prevention scaffolding after an acute intervention than a sharp mechanism or targeting paper. And the psilocybin-assisted CBT pilot remains interesting context without yet becoming a stronger mechanistic combination paper.

Final takeaway. Today's new AUD paper matters because it treats neuromodulation as something that should change later fate through a measurable circuit shift, not just through a short-lived symptom nudge. The target-engagement paper matters because baseline site suitability may be more important than prettier after-the-fact stories. The home DBS biomarker paper matters because recovery is a longitudinal state-tracking problem. And the RL EEG-TMS paper matters because the relevant stimulation state may need to be learned, not guessed. The shared lesson is that relapse-facing neuromodulation only gets serious when outcome, target logic, and state estimation all harden at once.
