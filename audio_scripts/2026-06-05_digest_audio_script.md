Today’s pattern is simple. Precision depression stimulation still does not deserve to call itself precise just because it has a favorite coordinate or a favorite resting-state connectivity slogan.

The most useful new paper is titled, Auditory mismatch-negativity predicts response to dorsomedial prefrontal intermittent theta-burst stimulation in major depressive disorder. The reason it matters is that it asks a real biomarker question inside a sham-controlled trial. The study does not show that mismatch negativity cleanly distinguishes depressed patients from healthy controls, and it does not show that mismatch negativity itself changes after treatment. What it does show is narrower and more interesting. In the active stimulation arm, larger baseline mismatch negativity predicted greater symptom improvement. That relationship did not appear in sham. So the paper gives a plausible cheap physiological marker of plasticity capacity. But the caution matters just as much as the signal. The active-versus-sham difference in those predictive correlations did not itself reach significance. So this is a candidate biomarker, not a validated one.

The second ranked note is titled, Predictive value of subgenual cingulate normative connectivity to TMS treatment site for antidepressant response in routine clinical practice: a prospective, multisite cohort study. This is the useful negative baseline. It pressures one of the field’s most repeated claims, namely that stronger negative connectivity between the delivered left dorsolateral prefrontal cortex site and subgenual cingulate should predict better antidepressant response. In a heterogeneous real-world clinical sample, that heuristic explained little by itself. That does not mean circuit targeting is fake. It means the easy static version of the story is weaker than the field likes to admit.

The third ranked note is titled, White matter pathways mediating dorsolateral prefrontal TMS therapy for depression. This is the structural counterpoint. Its value is not that it proves a final targeting solution. Its value is that it shifts the question from one-number connectivity folklore to route geometry. In other words, maybe the useful question is not only whether a cortical site anticorrelates with subgenual cingulate, but whether stimulation from that site has plausible multi-step paths into the deeper circuit you actually care about.

Put together, these three notes give a much sharper picture of what a serious precision stack might require. First, some dynamic or physiological readout of state or plasticity capacity. Second, a realism check against routine clinical heterogeneity. Third, a mechanistic propagation story instead of a static map fetish.

I also checked the standing interest lanes. On hypnosis and hypnotherapy, the most visible recent result was a large digital hypnotherapy plus aromatherapy relaxation trial. It may be perfectly fine if your question is calmness or wellness, but it does not really sharpen mechanism-centered psychiatry or intervention design. On CBT plus interventional psychiatry, the clearest adjacent item was a psilocybin-assisted cognitive behavioral therapy pilot in depression. It is interesting as feasibility, but it is a sixteen-patient single-arm study. That is not enough to say much about sequencing logic, mechanistic complementarity, or who should get what.

So the real takeaway for today is this. Precision depression stimulation should stop acting as if a single static heuristic already counts as personalization. The better path looks more demanding. Measure whether the patient has the physiological capacity the intervention needs. Test whether your favorite targeting rule survives real heterogeneity. And ask whether the stimulation site actually has plausible routes into the circuit you hope to move. That stack still is not finished, but it is much closer to honest intervention logic than the field’s usual self-congratulation.
