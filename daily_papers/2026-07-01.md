## Short overview

The fresh scout did surface real material, but not material that beat the archive. **BrainDyn: A Sheaf Neural ODE for Generative Brain Dynamics** is not stupid. It uses sheaf-based graph geometry plus a neural ODE to forecast resting-state fMRI, epilepsy EEG, and synthetic spiking-network perturbations. The problem is that the intervention rhetoric outruns the evidence. The real-data tasks are forecasting tasks, the prior graph is built from short-window Granger structure, and the perturbation generalization claim is shown only in NEST neuron-silencing simulations. That makes it a respectable computational note and a weak control note. It does not outrank the perturbation-based control paper already in the archive.

The standing-interest checks were more clinically grounded but still not keep-worthy enough to justify new notes. **Does mental health coaching improve efficacy of transcranial magnetic stimulation for major depression? A pilot randomized controlled trial and benchmarking study** is a small adjunct study where both coached digital arms improved over time and may have outperformed an external benchmark, but the randomized contrast between the two coaching conditions was null and the design still feels more like service-layer augmentation than circuit-level combination logic. **Efficacy of combined low-frequency rTMS and cognitive behavioral therapy for unmedicated panic disorder: A randomized controlled trial** is more interesting, because active rTMS plus CBT accelerated early panic and anxiety improvement relative to sham plus CBT. Even so, it is still a fifty-patient single-disorder adjunct study and does not beat the existing meta-analysis as the cleaner implementation guide for psychotherapy-plus-stimulation design. On the hypnosis side, a fresh PubMed route mostly degenerated into “hypnotics” noise rather than better mechanistic hypnosis papers, so the existing **Decoding hypnotic consciousness** note still stands as the better keep.

The useful July first pattern is therefore a negative one in the healthy sense. Architecture novelty is getting easier to manufacture than intervention logic. When that happens, the right move is not to mint another note just because something is new. The right move is to keep the archive anchored to papers that actually earn claims about control, targeting, measurement, and state change.

## Most relevant paper

**Quantifying State-Dependent Control Properties of Brain Dynamics from Perturbation Responses**

This stays at the top because it answers the question today’s fresh candidates keep sidestepping. If you want to talk about controllability, you should probably use perturbation data. The paper is not flashy, and it is not clinically large, but it upgrades control language from decorative mathematics to something closer to system identification. That matters more than another graph architecture that forecasts time series well and then gestures at intervention.

It also serves as the right benchmark for interpreting **BrainDyn**. BrainDyn may become useful as a virtual-brain modeling ingredient, but right now its strongest claim is better structured forecasting plus synthetic perturbation extrapolation. That is interesting. It is not yet the same thing as showing that the model identifies control-relevant directions in real neural systems or that it sharpens intervention timing or targeting in a falsifiable way.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful July first lesson is that new model architecture is not the same thing as new intervention logic. A sheaf neural ODE can be clever and still fail to change how we should think about stimulation. The archive notes that still matter most are the ones that earn their claims at the right layer: perturbation-grounded control, genuinely closed-loop human intervention, realistic stimulation geometry, and artifact-aware measurement. The fresh combo-treatment papers are clinically real but still too small and too adjunctive to outrank the stronger implementation note already on file.
