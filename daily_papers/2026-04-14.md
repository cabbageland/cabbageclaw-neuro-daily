## Short overview

The most relevant paper today is **Frequency-Dependent Whole-Brain Reconfiguration Following Left DLPFC rTMS in Older Adults: A 106-Channel fNIRS Study**. Thirty healthy older adults underwent both 10 hertz and 1 hertz left-dorsolateral-prefrontal repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation in a randomized single-blind crossover design, with whole-brain resting-state functional near-infrared spectroscopy before and immediately after stimulation. The clean result is not that one frequency was simply excitatory and the other inhibitory. At the network-summary level, 10 hertz tended toward more globally positive topology and distributed-connectivity changes, while 1 hertz tended the other way, but localized paradoxical effects also appeared, including bilateral primary-motor-cortex increases after 1 hertz and decreases after 10 hertz. That makes this paper useful less as a clinical efficacy claim and more as a reminder that stimulation effects are distributed, state-dependent, and not well described by a crude frequency slogan.

The second paper is **cuBNM: GPU-Accelerated Brain Network Modeling**. This is a methods infrastructure paper, but a respectable one. The authors build a GPU-based Python package for large-scale brain network model simulation and fitting, then show several-hundred-fold speedups over CPU baselines while supporting group-level and individualized optimization in low- and high-dimensional models. The useful read is not “faster is always better.” The useful read is that individualized network-model fitting may be becoming cheap enough to test as part of targeting, subject stratification, or intervention-design workflows instead of living mostly as boutique offline analysis.

The third paper is **Structured flexibility in recurrent neural networks via neuromodulation**. This is adjacent inspiration rather than a direct neuromodulation paper. In a low-rank recurrent-neural-network setup, a neuromodulatory subnetwork emits low-dimensional gain signals that dynamically scale recurrent weights, improving training and generalization on canonical tasks and yielding gating behavior that the authors relate to standard artificial recurrent-network mechanisms. The reason to keep it in view is not biological realism theater. It is that the paper offers a compact computational picture of how low-dimensional modulatory control could reorganize network computation without full structural rewiring.

One paper stayed below note threshold today despite being visible in scouting: **Proceedings of the 12th annual deep brain stimulation think tank: cutting edge technology meets novel applications**. It is useful citation material if you want a broad snapshot of where the DBS establishment thinks the field is headed, but it is still a proceedings overview, not a paper I would preserve as a primary mechanistic note.

## Most relevant paper

**Frequency-Dependent Whole-Brain Reconfiguration Following Left DLPFC rTMS in Older Adults: A 106-Channel fNIRS Study**

This is the top paper because it makes a basic but still under-enforced move: if you stimulate left dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, you should ask what happens to the broader network, not just repeat the stimulation recipe and gesture at prior dogma. The valuable part is the combination of whole-brain measurement and the willingness to report localized exceptions to the usual frequency story. That does not make the paper more clinically decisive, but it does make it more honest and more useful for thinking about individualized intervention logic.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For noninvasive neuromodulation framing:** the older-adult rTMS paper pushes against the stale “1 hertz inhibitory, 10 hertz excitatory” shorthand by showing network-level divergence plus region-specific exceptions.
- **For individualized intervention logic:** cuBNM matters because model-fitting cost has been a real reason whole-brain modeling stays ornamental. If the speedup is robust in practice, more personalized network modeling becomes operationally plausible.
- **For computational mechanism framing:** the structured-flexibility paper is useful because it treats neuromodulation as low-dimensional gain control over recurrent dynamics rather than vague context magic.
- **For field-status reading:** the DBS think-tank proceedings are worth knowing about, but they should not be confused with strong evidence.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The most useful papers today all punish oversimplification. The rTMS study matters because distributed network effects in aging brains do not collapse neatly into a single excitatory-versus-inhibitory rule. cuBNM matters because personalized network modeling only becomes consequential when it is cheap enough to run at real scale. The low-rank neuromodulated-RNN paper matters because it offers a compact computational story for how small modulatory control signals can reshape network behavior. Overall lesson: if the field wants better intervention logic, it needs both better whole-brain measurement and cheaper mechanistic modeling, not just louder target branding.
