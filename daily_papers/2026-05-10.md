## Short overview

The most relevant paper today is **Brain-heart interactions in late-onset major depressive disorder revealed by multimodal HRV-driven fMRI**. In twenty patients with late-onset major depressive disorder and twenty matched controls, the authors estimate time-varying autonomic regressors from simultaneous ECG and resting-state fMRI, then ask which brain regions track those autonomic fluctuations. The useful result is not just that depression touches the insula again. It is that right-insula, cingulate, and hippocampal coupling to autonomic dynamics appears altered, and right-insula response scales with symptom severity. That makes the paper more useful for interoceptive-state tracking and multimodal biomarker design than a standard resting-state paper.

The second paper is **Trough-phase coupling of 40 Hz oscillatory transcranial direct current stimulation with iTBS enhances corticospinal excitability and brain connectivity in healthy individuals**. In a randomized crossover study of eighteen healthy adults, the authors compare peak-phase and trough-phase coupling of oscillatory tDCS with intermittent theta burst stimulation, plus single-modality and sham controls. The main finding is that trough-phase coupling produces more sustained corticospinal facilitation and stronger late directed information flow in TMS-EEG than the alternatives. This is still a healthy-subject motor-cortex methods paper, but at least it tests a real phase-alignment hypothesis.

The third paper is **rTMS Modulates Static and Dynamic Brain Functional Networks in Children with Autism Spectrum Disorder: An EEG Microstate Study**. Thirty-two children with autism spectrum disorder were randomized to active or sham one-hertz dorsolateral prefrontal rTMS for nine weeks, with resting EEG and behavioral assessment before and after treatment. The paper did not show clear interaction effects in standard microstate timing parameters, but it did report stronger static connectivity and higher dynamic complexity across microstates after active stimulation. The mechanistic claim is modest, but the paper is still worth preserving because it treats network flexibility as a stimulation readout rather than just reporting symptom change.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful pattern today is that better papers expose timing or physiology instead of hiding inside broad symptom labels. The strongest paper treats autonomic fluctuations as part of the depressive state representation and finds altered insula-centered coupling. The stimulation-methods paper shows that combined stimulation only becomes interesting when timing is part of the hypothesis.
