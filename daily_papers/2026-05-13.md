## Short overview

The most relevant paper today is **White matter pathways mediating dorsolateral prefrontal TMS therapy for depression**. The paper argues that effective depression TMS sites are not just those with the right resting correlation to subgenual cingulate, but those with shorter and more plausible polysynaptic white-matter communication routes into that deeper circuit. According to the accessible abstract, modeled route length explained treatment response in two independent cohorts and also tracked the efficacy of functional-MRI-guided targeting. That is still a proxy story rather than direct propagation measurement, but it is a more serious mechanistic framing than anti-subgenual sloganism.

The second paper is **Phase-dependent stimulation response is shaped by the brain's dynamic functional connectivity**. This is a whole-brain modeling paper, not a clinical trial, but it is exactly the kind of computational-control work that helps intervention logic. The authors argue that the same pulse can have different effects not only because of local oscillatory phase but also because the brain is occupying different transient functional-connectivity states. Their machine-learning comparison suggests that network-aware state descriptors improve prediction of stimulation effects by up to about forty percent and that perturbations can trigger global state switching rather than merely local response changes.

The third paper is **Reliability and signal comparison of OPM-MEG, fMRI & iEEG in a repeated movie viewing paradigm**. This is a measurement paper, but a useful one. It asks whether optically pumped magnetometer MEG gives reproducible stimulus-driven signals and whether those signals line up with both functional MRI and intracranial EEG. The answer appears to be yes, especially in lower frequency bands and especially in visual and auditory regions. That does not prove OPM-MEG is ready to run psychiatric closed loops, but it does make it a more credible future state-estimation tool than a sensor stack that only agrees with itself.

## Most relevant paper

**White matter pathways mediating dorsolateral prefrontal TMS therapy for depression**

This gets top rank because it most directly changes intervention reasoning. The useful shift is from asking whether a surface target is anti-correlated with subgenual cingulate to asking how stimulation effects might actually travel through plausible structural routes. That does not yet prove route length is the right control variable, but it is a better mechanistic question than another round of target branding.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For depression TMS targeting:** the white-matter paper is a real framing upgrade. It suggests that propagation geometry through intermediate circuitry may be a better mechanistic baseline than simple site-to-subgenual functional connectivity.
- **For closed-loop stimulation logic:** the dynamic-functional-connectivity paper argues that local phase-only control is probably under-modeled. If true, many "state-dependent" systems are observing too small a slice of the system they claim to steer.
- **For future adaptive neurotechnology:** the OPM paper matters because measurement validation is upstream of control. A noninvasive modality that converges with both fMRI and iEEG is more worth building on than one that just produces pretty maps.
- **What did not make the cut:** there are clinically adjacent papers around personalized connectivity-guided stimulation and depression subtyping, but today the strongest transferable value came from a better pathway model, a better state model, and a better sensing benchmark.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The strongest papers today all attack a different weak point in neuromodulation logic. One improves the propagation story, one improves the state story, and one improves the sensing story. The white-matter paper is the most directly useful because it gives depression TMS a more explicit circuit-communication frame. The dynamic-functional-connectivity paper is the most conceptually important for closed-loop control because it treats network state as part of the controller state. And the OPM paper matters because without trustworthy noninvasive measurement, a lot of adaptive-stimulation rhetoric stays decorative.
