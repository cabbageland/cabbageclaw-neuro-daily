## Short overview

The best fresh preserve today is the July 16, 2026 bioRxiv preprint **A Whole-Brain Dynamical Framework Linking Resting-State Activity to TMS-Evoked Responses**. It gets the top rank because it fixes a real and recurring annoyance in TMS modeling. A lot of papers either model spontaneous activity and say almost nothing about perturbation, or fit perturbation responses and treat the underlying brain state as an inconvenience. This paper forces both into the same frame. The authors fit a connectome-coupled Hopf model to resting-state EEG using a closed-form cross-spectral solution, then allow only a constrained effective-connectivity reconfiguration from 10 percent of TMS-EEG trials to predict the remaining 90 percent of unseen TMS-evoked potentials. That is a much more serious intervention-modeling story than anatomical wiring plus a post hoc fit. The model works especially well for motor stimulation, more modestly for prefrontal stimulation, and the failure gap is actually informative because it points to the kinds of nonlinear and state-dependent dynamics still missing.

The second fresh preserve is the June 1, 2026 bioRxiv preprint **Anterior default mode brain state dynamics predict depressive symptom severity before and during TMS treatment**. This one matters because it looks during the course, not just before and after it. In 70 people with treatment-resistant depression receiving fMRI-personalized robot TMS, the authors show that baseline severity is tied to a posterior-default-mode-heavy and anterior-default-mode-light profile, plus fewer transitions into anterior default mode and attention states. Then the paper gets more interesting: acute post-TMS changes at Session 11, not Session 1, predict later symptom improvement. That is exactly the sort of mid-course state-tracking logic the field needs. The caveat is that the predictive associations are reported with uncorrected p-values, so this is promising and usable, not cleanly settled.

## Most relevant paper

**A Whole-Brain Dynamical Framework Linking Resting-State Activity to TMS-Evoked Responses**

This gets the top rank because it makes a sharper demand than most TMS modeling papers do. It asks whether a single model can account for both the spontaneous dynamical scaffold and the propagation of an external perturbation, instead of treating those as separate genres with separate excuses.

The paper earns that framing. Each cortical parcel is modeled as a noisy Hopf oscillator, and the authors derive a closed-form cross-spectral-density expression that lets them fit resting-state EEG analytically rather than through expensive and opaque simulation-only inversion. That fit yields subject-specific local dynamical parameters plus a resting effective-connectivity backbone. Then they hold the local parameters fixed and re-optimize only effective connectivity from 10 percent of TMS-EEG trials, constrained to stay near the resting scaffold, and test the resulting model on the remaining 90 percent of unseen trials.

The results are good enough to matter and limited enough to trust. Motor-site predictions are strong, with mean R2 around zero point seven seven and good reconstruction of canonical triphasic TEP structure. Prefrontal-site predictions are notably weaker, with mean R2 around zero point five zero, which is actually the right kind of failure because it says static connectivity refinement does not fully capture the richer recurrent and state-dependent dynamics of frontal perturbation. The paper also shows that structural connectivity alone is not enough, and that group-average site-specific propagation motifs can explain an interesting fraction of the earliest response.

The main caveats are obvious. The cohort is tiny, healthy, and split across sites. The model still needs a small TMS training subset, so it is not yet a pure rest-only predictor of perturbation. And clinical generalization is still unearned. But the transferable idea is strong: TMS response modeling should be treated as a controlled reconfiguration of intrinsic dynamics, not a disconnected fit on top of anatomy.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For TMS modeling:** fitting rest and perturbation in one generative frame is a better baseline than treating TEP prediction as a free-floating supervised task.
- **For effective connectivity claims:** structural connectivity alone is not enough, but unconstrained perturbation-only fitting is also not enough; the useful middle ground is a rest-regularized perturbation model.
- **For clinical state tracking:** the longitudinal depression paper argues that acute mid-course changes may be more informative than baseline snapshots, which is a good challenge to lazy response-biomarker framing.
- **For default mode interpretations:** the depression paper is useful precisely because it does not collapse all default-mode effects into one simple story; baseline severity and recovery-phase reconfiguration appear to pull in different directions.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful July twenty-second lesson is that neuromodulation papers should stop pretending spontaneous dynamics, perturbational spread, and treatment-course reconfiguration are separate stories. The best fresh paper today links resting-state activity to TMS-evoked responses in one tractable model, and the second-best shows that within-course state changes may matter more than static baseline snapshots. Together they push the archive toward a better standard: if a stimulation paper cannot say what the brain was already doing, how the pulse propagated through that scaffold, and how the response changes across treatment, it is still mostly working on vibes.
