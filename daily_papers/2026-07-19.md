## Short overview

The main fresh preserve today is the July 15, 2026 bioRxiv preprint **Low-latency neuromorphic closed-loop control of hippocampal ripples in vivo**. It gets the top rank because it asks the right humiliating question for adaptive-neuromodulation papers: what survives after the detector leaves the laptop and has to pass through actual acquisition, buffering, hardware inference, and feedback triggering in time to matter? The answer here is imperfect but real. The authors train a tiny spiking network, only forty-one neurons and five hundred thirty parameters, to detect hippocampal ripples from spike-encoded local field potentials, deploy it on SpiNNaker, integrate it with Open Ephys, and then show that the full optimized loop can hit about fifty milliseconds end-to-end latency and deliver stimulation before ripple termination in up to eighty percent of events. The most useful part is not that the spiking net beats everything. It does not. A tuned Buzsaki-style detector still wins the offline F1 race. The useful part is that the paper keeps measuring after that embarrassment and shows where neuromorphic hardware still buys something meaningful: real-time operation, large power savings, and a path to in-vivo intervention.

That lands well against the current archive. **A first realization of reinforcement learning-based closed-loop EEG-TMS** remains the best recent human proof that a stimulation target state can be learned online instead of hand-picked in advance. **Principles of Physiologic Closed-Loop Controllers in Neuromodulation** remains the best compact framework for judging whether a paper is controlling anything symptom-relevant or merely stabilizing a subsystem. **Neuromorphic Energy-Aware Learning for Adaptive Deep Brain Stimulation** still matters because it asks the complementary implant question: not just whether a loop works, but whether it optimizes the actuator energy budget the device actually pays. Today's new preserve adds the missing operational insult. A controller also has to survive the hardware path quickly enough to touch the event it claims to control.

## Most relevant paper

**Low-latency neuromorphic closed-loop control of hippocampal ripples in vivo**

This gets the top rank because it clears a stricter bar than most adaptive-neuromodulation papers ever face. It does not just ask whether a learned detector can classify neural events. It asks whether a compact detector can survive the whole loop fast enough to matter for a transient oscillation that lasts only about thirty to one hundred milliseconds.

The paper earns that framing. Hippocampal local field potentials are converted into sparse UP and DN spike streams, fed into a tiny spiking neural network, deployed on SpiNNaker, and integrated with Open Ephys for real-time feedback. The network is small, forty-one neurons and five hundred thirty parameters, and the offline detection results are only moderately flattering: median recall is high at about zero point eight nine, median precision is lower at about zero point five zero, and the best SNN reaches median F1 around zero point six one. The tuned classical Buzsaki-style detector still beats it at zero point seven zero. That honesty is part of why the paper is useful. It blocks the usual neuromorphic escape hatch where any hardware claim gets smuggled through on weak baseline discipline.

What makes the paper preserve-worthy is what happens after that. The authors measure the software-to-hardware hit, the online buffering tradeoff, the feedback-path penalty, and the eventual round-trip latency. In the optimized hardware-in-the-loop configuration, the system reaches about fifty milliseconds median latency and can stimulate before ripple termination in up to eighty percent of events. SpiNNaker runs in biological real time while using far less power than CPU or GPU simulation. Then, in awake head-fixed mice, the authors close the loop all the way through optogenetic inhibition and show that even a preliminary high-latency configuration still shifts ripple dynamics, with the strongest effect appearing as lower ripple energy in the longest detected events.

The limitations are real and worth keeping attached to the paper. The offline detector is not state of the art once the classical baseline is tuned properly. The in-vivo intervention experiments are only two mice, twelve sessions, and an optogenetic rather than clinically realistic actuator. Precision is still mediocre enough that unnecessary stimulation remains a live problem. But the transferable lesson is strong: if a loop claims to control fast brain events, then latency accounting, hardware deployment loss, and feedback-path design belong in the main result, not the appendix.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful July nineteenth lesson is that adaptive neuromodulation needs to stop flattering itself with offline success. A controller that cannot survive buffering, bridge overhead, hardware deployment, and feedback triggering quickly enough to touch the state it claims to regulate is not much of a controller. Today's preserve matters because it treats that as the real problem and measures the answer directly, even when the answer includes losing the offline accuracy contest to a tuned classical baseline.
