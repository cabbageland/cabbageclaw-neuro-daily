## Short overview

The main fresh preserve today is the July 14, 2026 arXiv paper **Optimal photostimulation selection for iterative activity maps**. It is not a clinical neuromodulation paper, and it is not pretending to be one. It is a neuroengineering and causal-methods paper about all-optical circuit mapping in larval zebrafish. The reason it earns archive status anyway is that it points at a real missing layer in adaptive intervention logic: many papers spend all their energy on inference or control after data are collected, while quietly assuming that the perturbations themselves were cheap, plentiful, and sensibly chosen. OPhELIA instead asks how to spend a limited stimulation budget when the action space is large and the map is still uncertain. Its Beta-Bernoulli edge model is simple, its ambiguity heuristic is legible, and the in vivo result that the compressed-sensing variant gets closest to an exhaustive reference using only 5 percent of trials is the kind of claim that actually matters.

## Most relevant paper

**Optimal photostimulation selection for iterative activity maps**

This gets the top rank because it solves a problem that many adaptive-neuroscience papers quietly leave underspecified. If interventions are expensive, combinatorial, heating-limited, and potentially damaging, then "collect more perturbation data" is not a serious plan. OPhELIA takes that constraint seriously. It keeps a posterior over target-responder edges, scores where the map is still ambiguous, and uses that score to decide which target or target combination should be stimulated next. That is a much better scientific instinct than treating the intervention schedule as a boring implementation detail outside the model.

The method is also disciplined enough to matter. The core edge model is simple Beta-Bernoulli inference, not a grandiose foundation-model wrapper. The policy score explicitly combines posterior variance with closeness to 0.5 response probability, so the algorithm spends trials where uncertainty is still informative rather than where the map is already boringly confident. The prior layer is also sane. Instead of adding mystical physiology prose, the paper trains a random forest on prestimulation spontaneous and visually evoked activity to predict target informativeness, then blends that prior into target selection. That kind of modest learned prior is much more credible than the usual "multimodal biological prior" branding sludge.

The in vivo validation is why the paper earns keep status instead of just sounding nice. In combinatorial zebrafish experiments, the OPhELIA compressed-sensing variant most closely recovers the exhaustive reference map using only 5 percent of available trials. That is not the same thing as solving closed-loop therapeutic neuromodulation, and nobody should pretend otherwise. But it is exactly the sort of trial-compression result that future causal perturbation and intervention-design papers should have to clear before they get to talk grandly about adaptive control.

The limitations are real. The responses are binarized into success or failure, the work assumes successful stimulation-site activation, and the experimental setting is still a small all-optical animal platform rather than a messy human device context. But the transferable idea is strong: if budget is scarce, the policy for choosing interventions is itself part of the scientific model. That is more valuable today than another flashy control paper with weaker contact with the actual perturbation bottleneck.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful July fifteenth lesson is that adaptive neuroscience cannot just obsess over inference after the fact. It has to care about which interventions get chosen while uncertainty is still unevenly distributed. Today's preserve matters because it gives a clean, testable answer to that budgeted perturbation problem and validates it on real all-optical data. It is not a clinical therapy paper, and that restraint is part of why it is useful. The paper does not solve neuromodulation. It sharpens one of the pieces that real neuromodulation keeps needing.
