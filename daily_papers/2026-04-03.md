## Theme

Today is a **deep-target-engagement versus control-ambition** day. The strongest new paper is a Nature Communications study using individualized transcranial ultrasound stimulation to hit the human globus pallidus internus and read out local field potentials through implanted deep brain stimulation leads. That is rare direct evidence of deep-target engagement from a nominally noninvasive modality. The strongest clinical context paper remains the randomized PTSD trial using individualized functional MRI guidance for transcranial magnetic stimulation, because it continues to set a better-than-usual bar for circuit-based psychiatric targeting. On the computational and network side, I scouted broadly, but most of the newest titles were still stronger as framing material than as preserve-worthy notes.

## Short overview

The most relevant new paper today is **Individualized non-invasive deep brain stimulation of the basal ganglia using transcranial ultrasound stimulation**. The useful part is not the phrase non-invasive DBS, which is marketing-dangerous on its face. The useful part is narrower and better: the authors used subject-specific acoustic simulations, targeted the globus pallidus internus in patients who already had implanted DBS systems, and then recorded local field potentials from that deep target while delivering ultrasound. Theta-burst ultrasound increased theta power during stimulation, ten-hertz ultrasound increased beta power, and some effects lasted up to forty minutes. In healthy participants, globus pallidus internus stimulation also worsened stop-signal performance while pulvinar stimulation did not, which at least argues for some target specificity. This does not prove ultrasound is ready to replace implanted DBS. It does show direct human deep-target engagement with physiological and behavioral consequences, which is a materially stronger claim than most ultrasound neuromodulation papers can support.

The second paper worth attention is **Personalized fMRI-Guided TMS Targeting the Threat Neurocircuitry in PTSD: A Randomized Clinical Trial**. It is not new to the repo anymore, but it remains one of the better recent examples of a psychiatric neuromodulation study trying to make target logic, mechanistic endpoint, and later symptom outcome live in the same trial. Active stimulation reduced right amygdala threat reactivity relative to sham, while delayed symptom outcomes favored active treatment. Skin conductance did not move, and the immediate symptom separation was not clean, so this is not a triumphal precision-psychiatry story. But it is still a more serious targeting paper than most.

The third item is **Multimodal branched transport infers anatomically aligned brain reaction maps**. This is adjacent inspiration rather than a preserve-worthy note. The idea is to infer routed propagation architectures from multimodal data instead of assuming a fixed network and only optimizing transition cost. That is conceptually aligned with intervention logic, because routing matters for where stimulation effects spread. But at abstract level this still looks more like a mathematical framing paper than a demonstrated neuroscience tool. Interesting, yes. Ready to preserve as a practical note, not yet.

A fourth title that surfaced repeatedly was **Counterfactual Analysis of Brain Network Dynamics**. I am explicitly not preserving it today. The framing sounds appealing, but at accessible depth it still reads like intervention language wrapped around a Hodge-theoretic decomposition without enough empirical burden-bearing. That may mature into a useful citation later, but today it looks more like theory packaging than something that changes how we would build or evaluate a neuromodulation system.

## Most relevant paper

**Individualized non-invasive deep brain stimulation of the basal ganglia using transcranial ultrasound stimulation**

This is the top paper because it actually attacks a hard translational bottleneck instead of hand-waving around it. The authors used individualized acoustic simulations to target the globus pallidus internus, tested safety around implanted DBS leads, and then recorded local field potentials directly from the deep target in movement-disorder patients. That gives a much cleaner target-engagement readout than EEG or fMRI proxies alone. The behavioral arm in healthy participants is also useful because it shows a task effect for globus pallidus internus stimulation that did not appear for pulvinar stimulation. The caveat is obvious and important: a small mechanistic study showing target engagement is not the same thing as a deployable therapeutic alternative to implanted deep brain stimulation. But this is real translational progress.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For ultrasound neuromodulation:** this paper raises the bar. Claims about deep target engagement should ideally show direct physiological evidence at the target, not only surface readouts or behavioral drift.
- **For noninvasive-versus-invasive framing:** the phrase non-invasive DBS should be treated cautiously. What the paper really earns is deep-target modulation with individualized acoustic targeting, not therapeutic equivalence to implanted DBS.
- **For psychiatric and neurological targeting logic:** the PTSD TMS paper still matters because it shows how to tie individualized targeting to a circuit hypothesis and a mechanistic endpoint, even if the clinical story remains mixed.
- **For computational/network scouting:** today’s newer control-and-network titles were more useful as filters than as additions. The branched-transport paper is interesting but early, and the counterfactual-network paper currently looks under-validated.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful lesson today is that direct target engagement matters more than grand control vocabulary. The ultrasound globus-pallidus paper is worth keeping because it actually measures a deep target while perturbing it and shows some behavioral specificity. The PTSD TMS paper remains useful because it ties target choice to a symptom-relevant circuit instead of generic scalp heuristics. By contrast, several fresh computational-network titles sounded intervention-relevant but still carried more mathematical ambition than empirical burden. So today’s honest pattern is simple: preserve the paper that proves it touched something real, keep the serious circuit-targeting clinical trial as context, and do not let elegant theory language crowd out stronger mechanistic evidence.
