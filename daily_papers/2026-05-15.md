## Short overview

The most relevant paper today is **Combinatorial effects of multi-site stimulation on depression-related brain regions: clinical data analysis and predictive modeling**. Three treatment-resistant depression participants received bilateral subcallosal cingulate and ventral capsule or ventral striatum stimulation while stereo-EEG sampled prefrontal depression networks. The important result is not clinical efficacy. It is that bilateral and cross-target combinations were usually additive or sub-additive, with region of interest contributing more to the interaction class than target name alone. That matters because multi-target depression DBS is often discussed as if more stimulation automatically means more network leverage, and this paper says the interaction structure is more constrained than that.

The second paper is **Invasive and Non-Invasive Neural Decoding of Motor Performance in Parkinson's Disease for Personalized Deep Brain Stimulation**. Across 19 Parkinson's patients and 35 sessions, the authors used patient-specific EEG or epidural ECoG features to decode drawing-task motor performance under DBS on and off conditions. The headline is not just that decoding works in many sessions. It is that the paper separates informative biomarkers from controllable ones and shows that some decodable signals would still be bad adaptive-DBS control variables. That is a healthier framing than the usual biomarker hunting.

The third paper is **A meta-analysis suggests that TMS targeting the hippocampal network selectively improves episodic memory**. Across 38 studies and 1,009 participants, hippocampal indirectly targeted stimulation produced a moderate positive effect on episodic memory, near-zero effects on non-memory cognition, stronger effects for recollection-heavy tasks, and stronger effects when stimulation came before the task instead of between encoding and retrieval. This does not magically validate every hippocampal-network TMS protocol, but it is one of the cleaner signs that network-guided targeting can produce functionally selective effects when the behavioral assay actually matches the circuit claim.

## Most relevant paper

**Combinatorial effects of multi-site stimulation on depression-related brain regions: clinical data analysis and predictive modeling**

This gets top rank because it addresses a quietly central question for multi-contact and multi-target neuromodulation: how do stimulation effects compose across a distributed therapeutic network? The study is tiny and not remotely decisive clinically, but it is still more useful than another average-effect depression DBS paper because it explicitly models additivity, super-additivity, and sub-additivity across targets, hemispheres, regions, and bands. The key caution is sample size. Three participants are enough to expose interaction structure and generate hypotheses, not to settle target strategy.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For depression DBS design:** the main update is that combined stimulation should be treated as a network interaction problem, not a monotonic dosing problem.
- **For adaptive DBS evaluation:** the Parkinson decoding paper is valuable because it asks whether a biomarker is both informative and controllable, rather than celebrating correlation alone.
- **For network-guided noninvasive stimulation:** the hippocampal-network meta-analysis strengthens the case that target definition and task structure matter together, especially for recollection-heavy episodic memory assays.
- **What did not make the cut:** an early sensing-enabled ventral-capsule OCD DBS preprint looked clinically interesting, but accessible text here was abstract-level only, so it was not a strong enough preserve note relative to the three better-inspected papers.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The strongest pattern today is that useful neuromodulation papers are getting better when they stop treating targets, biomarkers, and stimulation combinations as self-justifying objects. The depression DBS paper asks how effects compose across a network. The Parkinson paper asks whether a decoded feature is actually suitable for control. The hippocampal-network TMS meta-analysis asks whether a connectivity-guided intervention produces selective behavioral effects instead of generic uplift. Different modalities, same demand: intervention logic has to match network structure, state measurement, and task design.
