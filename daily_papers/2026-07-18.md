## Short overview

The main fresh preserve today is the July 9, 2026 bioRxiv preprint **Device-embedded accelerometry complements neural signals for tracking parkinsonian motor states**. It gets the top rank because it asks a more honest adaptive-DBS question than many decoder papers ask. Instead of assuming that canonical neural biomarkers remain equally useful once therapy is active, it tests which signals survive the intervention itself. Using more than 1,900 hours of chronic home recordings from sensing-enabled Parkinson's implants, the authors compare subthalamic and cortical field-potential features against the chest-implanted device accelerometer while patients move through everyday life before and during continuous stimulation. The useful result is not just that accelerometry works. The useful result is that several familiar neural features, especially periodic subthalamic beta, become less trustworthy once stimulation is on, while implanted accelerometry and some aperiodic or network-level neural features remain comparatively stable.

That lands well against the current archive. **Activity-dependent adaptive deep brain stimulation improves gait in Parkinson's disease** still gives the clearest recent case that adaptive control should switch policies across behavioral contexts instead of thresholding one generic marker. **Bayesian time-history modeling enhances Parkinsonian motor state classification for adaptive deep brain stimulation** remains a good reminder that temporal continuity matters for motor-state decoding. **Long-Term Personalized Adaptive Deep Brain Stimulation in Parkinson Disease: A Nonrandomized Clinical Trial** still matters because it proves that chronic sensing-driven DBS can actually run at home rather than only in short lab demos. Today's new preserve adds a different but equally important constraint: the marker itself has to stay legible under stimulation, or at least fail gracefully enough that the controller can compensate.

## Most relevant paper

**Device-embedded accelerometry complements neural signals for tracking parkinsonian motor states**

This gets the top rank because it solves a problem that adaptive-DBS papers often blur away. A closed-loop system does not need the prettiest biomarker in the off-stimulation state. It needs a signal that still means something once the therapy is already altering the circuit.

The paper's best move is to force that issue directly. The authors stream subthalamic, cortical, and device-embedded accelerometer signals from Parkinson's patients living through normal daily behavior, pair them with continuous PKG-derived bradykinesia and dyskinesia estimates, and then compare neural-only, accelerometry-only, and combined regressors. The key mechanistic result is that total subthalamic beta is too crude because it mixes periodic and aperiodic processes that point in opposite directions. Periodic beta behaves like a familiar bradykinetic marker off stimulation, but it becomes less reliable once stimulation is active. Aperiodic STN features, cortical periodic features, and cortico-subthalamic coherence survive better. The implanted accelerometer survives best of all.

That is not a glamorous result, and that is exactly why it matters. Adaptive neuromodulation keeps drifting toward decoder vanity: ever more spectral features, ever more predictive packaging, and not enough honesty about whether the putative marker remains usable once the loop is closed. This paper is useful because it says the controller may need a hierarchy. Let the implanted accelerometer carry stable behavioral-state tracking, and let neural features add mechanistic specificity where they remain trustworthy.

The caveats are real. The cohort is small at the patient level, only eight of eleven patients contributed concurrent implanted accelerometry, the wearable PKG target is itself a kinematic proxy rather than a latent disease-state oracle, and the decoding gains are respectable rather than transformative. But the transferable idea is strong: robustness under active therapy is part of the biomarker definition, not an afterthought.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For adaptive DBS design:** a control signal should be judged by whether it remains informative during active stimulation, not just by how well it correlates with symptoms off stimulation.
- **For Parkinson biomarker logic:** total beta power is a sloppy summary statistic when periodic and aperiodic components point in opposite behavioral directions.
- **For multimodal closed-loop systems:** a hierarchical behavioral-plus-neural controller looks more credible than a neural-only purity test.
- **For translational benchmarking:** today's paper, the activity-dependent gait note, and the Bayesian time-history note now make a useful trio: one asks which signal survives stimulation, one asks which policy should depend on behavior, and one asks how temporal continuity should shape decoding.
- **For standing-interest CBT combinations:** targeted PubMed passes still surfaced pilots, reviews, or broad treatment-overview papers rather than a stronger mechanistic keep than the existing stimulation-plus-psychotherapy anchor.
- **For standing-interest hypnosis:** targeted PubMed passes again surfaced suggestibility moderation, personality-disorder treatment, and protocol material rather than a better mechanism paper than the current hypnosis-consciousness anchor.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful July eighteenth lesson is that closed-loop neuromodulation should stop treating biomarker selection like a pre-therapy beauty contest. The question is not just which signal correlates with symptoms when the device is quiet. The question is which signal still helps once the device is already pushing on the system. Today's preserve matters because it treats that as the real engineering problem and comes back with a plausible answer: implanted accelerometry may be the stable behavioral backbone, while decomposed neural features help where circuit specificity is still worth the trouble.
