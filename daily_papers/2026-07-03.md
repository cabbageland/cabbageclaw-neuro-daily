## Short overview

The best new preserve today is the chronic-stress recurrent-network paper from Diaz, Beyer, and Hesse. It matters because it asks a much better question than the usual stress-circuit rhetoric. Instead of vaguely repeating that chronic stress shifts prefrontal excitation and inhibition, the paper compares eight candidate perturbations and gets a sharp answer: increasing inhibitory-to-excitatory synapses best matches the target pattern of inhibitory dominance, excitatory hypofunction, and impaired working-memory performance. The more interesting result comes after that. Networks trained under this stress operator become robust inside the trained regime, preserve baseline geometric and energetic structure, and keep performing. But they also generalize worse to longer untrained delays. That makes the paper useful not because it "solves" stress, but because it offers a sharper account of why chronic-stress adaptation can drift toward habit-like rigidity rather than simply toward uniform dysfunction.

The fresh clinical scout was mixed and mostly weaker than the existing archive anchors. **First-in-human low-intensity focused ultrasound targeting striatal circuits in schizophrenia: feasibility, safety, and effects on hallucinations and striatal-temporal functional connectivity** is mechanistically appealing on paper, but after full-text inspection the core issue is obvious: it is a three-patient within-subject feasibility preprint. That is interesting pilot material, not an archive-moving result. The better clinical benchmark remains the existing note on **Personalized fMRI-Guided TMS Targeting the Threat Neurocircuitry in PTSD: A Randomized Clinical Trial**, which already ties target personalization to a circuit hypothesis and actually moves an imaging endpoint in a fifty-person sham-controlled trial.

Standing-interest checks also stayed below preserve threshold today. On hypnosis and hypnotherapy, **Hypnosis reshapes multilevel stress response and enhances executive performance in stressed medical students** is a real full-text controlled study and the physiology is interesting, but it is still a stress-induction experiment in forty-nine medical students rather than a stronger mechanistic hypnosis paper than the existing ideomotor and agency note. **Hypnosis as a Mechanism of Emotion Regulation and Self-Integration** is a theory-heavy review with large-network language and uneven evidence quality, so it does not beat the current preserve either. On CBT plus interventional psychiatry, **Does mental health coaching improve efficacy of transcranial magnetic stimulation for major depression?** remained abstract-only here and does not show a specific iCBT win over the narrative control condition. The older combination-treatment meta-analysis still does a better job of organizing that design space.

The useful July third pattern is therefore not about another shiny intervention headline. It is that the middle layer matters. If a stress model cannot specify the perturbation, if a targeting paper cannot show circuit engagement, and if a combination-treatment paper cannot beat its own weak comparator logic, then the right move is to keep the note that sharpens mechanism and let the rest serve as calibration.

## Most relevant paper

**Modelling chronic stress as an excitatory-inhibitory perturbation in recurrent working-memory networks**

This gets top rank because it gives a concrete answer to a chronic problem in computational psychiatry: people love saying chronic stress disrupts E/I balance, but usually stop before specifying which perturbation actually matters. This paper does the tedious but useful comparison and finds that selectively strengthening inhibitory-to-excitatory synapses best reproduces the desired stress signature. That already makes it better than decorative "imbalance" language.

The more valuable result is the resilience trade-off. Networks trained under the perturbation preserve task performance and keep their internal geometry and energy budget near baseline, but they generalize worse to longer untrained delays. That is a much more interesting account of rigidity than a simple deficit story. It suggests that what looks like resilience on familiar demands may be the same specialization that makes the system brittle on novel ones. For cabbageland, that is a steal-worthy framing upgrade.

- **For chronic-stress modeling:** this paper raises the bar from "stress changes E/I balance" to "which perturbation reproduces the specific dysfunction pattern, and what trade-off does adaptation create?"

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful July third lesson is that the archive should reward mechanism that cashes out. The chronic-stress RNN paper earns preservation because it makes a falsifiable perturbation claim and then shows a meaningful resilience-versus-flexibility trade-off. The fresh schizophrenia ultrasound paper does not, because a three-person feasibility preprint is still mostly a scout signal. The fresh hypnosis and coaching-plus-rTMS papers also stay below threshold, because they do not beat the stronger existing notes in their own lanes. So the right move today is to preserve the computational paper that sharpens the argument and reuse the stronger existing clinical, control, combination-treatment, and hypnosis anchors around it.
