## Theme

Today is a **stop calling every durable psychedelic effect "brain entropy"** day. The strongest new keep is **Human brain changes after first psilocybin use**, because it does something more useful than slogan repetition. It ties acute EEG signal diversity to next-day insight, one-month well-being, and one-month structural changes in prefrontal-subcortical tracts, while also admitting that the long-term fMRI story is mostly weak. That honesty matters. The right read is not that psilocybin magically rewires everything. The right read is that acute state change, delayed psychological integration, and slower route-level plasticity may line up, but they are not the same object.

## Short overview

The new keep is a good mechanistic paper precisely because it resists flattening itself. In 28 psychedelic-naive healthy adults, a first 25 mg psilocybin session increased acute EEG signal diversity and reduced alpha power, while one-month follow-up showed decreased axial diffusivity in overlapping prefrontal-striatal and prefrontal-thalamic tracts plus improved well-being, psychological insight, and cognitive flexibility. The attractive part is that acute entropy predicted later well-being and that next-day insight mediated part of that relationship. The unattractive but important part is that most of the long-term fMRI results were weak or null. That combination makes the paper more believable, not less.

The context stack helps interpret it. **Deep brain stimulation induces white matter remodeling and functional changes to brain-wide networks** is the right comparator for the slower-plasticity claim, because it also argues that durable intervention effects may look more like route editing than instant functional fireworks. **Competitive interactions shape mammalian brain network dynamics and computation** is the right computational reminder that network modularity and global integration should not be read with a naive cooperative-brain prior. And **mPFC axons drive cognitive control enhancement during striatal stimulation** keeps the circuit-specificity lesson alive: when an intervention works, the recruited element matters more than the atlas label.

## Most relevant paper

**Human brain changes after first psilocybin use**

This gets top rank because it improves the language of psychedelic mechanism instead of merely extending the mythology. The main value is not the familiar claim that psilocybin increases brain entropy while people are on drug. The main value is the attempt to connect acute EEG entropy, next-day insight, one-month well-being, and one-month structural change without pretending the one-month fMRI results were stronger than they were.

The caveats are serious and visible. This is a fixed-order healthy-volunteer study, not a clinical trial. The DTI interpretation is biologically ambiguous, and the authors say so. The functional imaging results at one month are mostly modest. Even so, it is the strongest keep from today's scout because it is more disciplined than the average psychedelic biomarkers paper and more mechanistically useful than the average afterglow narrative.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful June twenty-seventh pattern is that durable intervention language gets better when it stops trying to force every effect through one acute-state slogan. Today's new psilocybin paper says acute EEG signal diversity may forecast later insight and well-being, while the one-month brain changes are more convincingly structural than functional. The SCC-DBS context note says slow route remodeling can be mechanistically real. The competitive-interactions note says network integration metrics need better dynamical priors. And the mPFC-axon note says recruited circuit elements matter more than target names. Put together, the lesson is simple: if a field wants to claim durable change, it should specify the timescale, the route, and the mediator instead of waving the word "plasticity" around.
