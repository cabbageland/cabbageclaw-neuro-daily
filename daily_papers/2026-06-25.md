## Short overview

The new OCD tDCS paper is the best thing from today’s scout because it asks a harder question than most noninvasive-psychiatry papers do. Instead of treating any suprathreshold electric field as equivalent, it looks at current direction relative to cortex in patient-specific head models from a treatment-resistant OCD cohort. The result is useful even though it is shaky: improvement on Y-BOCS was associated with greater depolarization in left anterior prefrontal cortex and right frontal eye field, and with greater hyperpolarization in right pars orbitalis, while simple field magnitude did not explain outcome. That is not a clinical biomarker yet. It is a much narrower and better claim: anatomy-shaped current geometry may matter more than peak-strength summaries.

The single-frame connectivity paper matters because it attacks a different version of the same average-brain mistake. Rather than clustering dynamic connectivity into shared group states, it asks whether one fMRI volume at a time already carries stable person-specific structure. Across young adults, adolescents, and older adults, the answer is yes, above chance, with better performance when the atlas is more fine-grained and the database has more scan data. More importantly, task decoding works better within subject than between subjects, which is exactly the kind of warning an adaptive-neuromodulation person should care about. If task-related state geometry is partly individual, then pretending there is one canonical group phase or network state to target becomes harder to defend.

## Most relevant paper

**Linking electric field directionality to treatment outcome in OCD: Insights from patient-specific tDCS modeling**

This gets top rank because it improves the object of explanation. A lot of tDCS papers ask whether a nominal montage helped. This one asks what field pattern each patient may actually have received and whether that geometry related to outcome. That is already a better framing for a modality whose main weakness is that the same scalp protocol can hide very different cortical delivery across heads.

The claim still needs discipline. This was a retrospective active-arm analysis in 42 patients, the responder count was thin, and none of the voxelwise effects survived FDR correction. So no, this is not a ready-made biomarker. But it is still more useful than decorative personalization talk, because it identifies a plausible mechanism-level variable to optimize next: directional field geometry in patient-specific anatomy rather than a one-number field-strength summary.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For OCD tDCS targeting:** current direction is a more interesting variable than electric-field magnitude. That should change what “better modeling” even means.
- **For biomarker rhetoric:** the paper is stronger as response-explanation than as response-prediction. Its finite-element model was significant at the model level, but single covariates were not clean standalone predictors and the imaging findings did not survive FDR.
- **For dynamic-state methodology:** the single-frame connectivity paper is the right warning that transient network patterns are not cleanly group-shareable. Personalization pressure starts before control, not only after it.
- **For adaptive-control logic:** the existing RL EEG-TMS note remains the best control counterpart to today’s keep. If relevant states vary by person and session, controllers should learn them online rather than inherit them from group priors.
- **For tDCS baseline choice:** the anatomy-informed montage paper remains the right older context note because it already showed that delivered field geometry varies heavily across people even before any outcome modeling enters.
- **For interventional psychiatry standing interests:** the new oral prolonged-release ketamine trials are real and clinically relevant, but they did not clear the preserve bar today because the primary day-21 efficacy endpoint missed and the main novelty is tolerability/home-use logic rather than sharper mechanism or targeting.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful June twenty-fifth pattern is that average-brain neuromodulation keeps breaking in the same place. Today’s OCD paper says that directional field geometry may matter more than scalar dose. Today’s single-frame connectivity paper says that moment-to-moment network structure is already person-specific enough to frustrate tidy group-state assumptions. The old RL EEG-TMS note says the control policy should therefore learn session-specific state rather than inherit it. And the anatomy-informed tDCS note says the actuator is partly the patient’s head. Put together, the lesson is simple: personalization in neuromodulation is not a branding layer added after the fact. It is the main engineering problem.
