## Short overview

The most relevant paper today is **Temporal interference stimulation (TIS) for major depressive disorder: right lingual gyrus as a predictor**. This is a multicenter randomized double-blind sham-controlled trial with seventy-six major-depression patients, framed as a secondary analysis of right-amygdala-targeted temporal interference stimulation. The useful part is not the usual deep-stimulation branding. It is the attempt to identify a baseline imaging feature, lower right lingual-gyrus ALFF, that predicts who benefits. That is still a modest biomarker story rather than a mature precision-therapy result, but it is better than pretending the target alone settles the problem.

The second paper is **Utilizing stimulation-evoked hemodynamic activity to predict antidepressant response to intermittent theta-burst stimulation in adults with major depression**. This is a smaller study, forty-four patients plus healthy controls, but the logic is more mechanistically appealing than many response-prediction papers. Instead of asking only who looks depressed at baseline, it measures prefrontal hemodynamic response during concurrent theta-burst stimulation and functional near-infrared spectroscopy, then uses that evoked response to predict four-week treatment outcome. The support-vector-machine result looks good on paper, but the reliability spread is wide enough to keep the paper in the promising-not-settled bucket.

The third paper is **Model-based analysis of stop-signal data reveals robust neural and clinical correlates of evidence accumulation but not inhibition**. This is not a neuromodulation trial, but it is exactly the kind of computational psychiatry paper that helps clean up intervention logic. Using a more mechanistic stop-signal model in the large IMAGEN cohort, the authors argue that substance-use vulnerability tracks evidence accumulation efficiency and related network features better than conventional inhibition metrics. If that holds up, then a lot of stimulation or cognitive-control framing built around “improving inhibition” is probably too blunt.

The fourth paper is **A Self-adaptive ultrasound patch for transcranial multimodal dynamic focusing**. I am keeping it as adjacent methods. The paper is mostly an engineering platform study, but it addresses a real bottleneck for wearable transcranial ultrasound: conformal fit plus dynamic focusing on irregular surfaces. It matters less as proof of neuromodulation efficacy than as a constraint-solving device paper that could make future adaptive ultrasound work less fake.

## Most relevant paper

**Temporal interference stimulation (TIS) for major depressive disorder: right lingual gyrus as a predictor**

This gets top rank because it combines a real sham-controlled clinical design with an explicit attempt to separate target selection from response prediction. The headline is not that right-amygdala temporal interference stimulation definitely works. The stronger contribution is that the paper tries to anchor treatment heterogeneity in a measurable baseline state variable, even if the current biomarker is still modest and not obviously robust enough for deployment.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For noninvasive deep-target stimulation:** today’s TIS paper is more useful as a heterogeneity paper than as a victory lap for temporal interference itself.
- **For depression biomarker work:** the iTBS plus functional-near-infrared-spectroscopy paper reinforces that stimulation-evoked response may be more interesting than static baseline scans, but reliability is still a ceiling.
- **For computational psychiatry framing:** the stop-signal modeling paper is a warning that “inhibitory control” may be a misleading intervention label when broader evidence-accumulation efficiency explains more of the signal.
- **For neuroengineering methods:** the ultrasound patch paper is a reminder that some of the most useful progress is still at the interface layer, where conformability and beam steering determine whether an adaptive story is even physically credible.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful pattern today is that target names are cheap, but state variables, evoked responses, and physically credible interfaces are harder and more valuable. The temporal interference depression paper matters because it at least tries to explain heterogeneity instead of treating deep targeting as self-justifying. The iTBS paper matters because it measures the brain’s response to stimulation rather than only cataloging baseline correlates. The stop-signal modeling paper matters because it suggests many psychiatric intervention stories may be aimed at the wrong latent construct. And the ultrasound hardware paper matters because adaptive neuromodulation remains partly a device-credibility problem, not just a modeling problem.
