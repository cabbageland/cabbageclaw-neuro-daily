## Short overview

The most relevant paper today is **Automating Subthalamic Deep Brain Stimulation Programming with Evoked Resonant Neural Activity in Parkinson's Disease**. In twelve Parkinson's patients with well-placed subthalamic leads, an automated programming rule based on intraoperative evoked resonant neural activity, or ERNA, produced acute motor benefit and side-effect thresholds similar to expert clinician programming and imaging-guided programming. The useful point is not that programming is solved. It is that a sub-minute physiological readout may be good enough to give clinicians an objective starting point instead of a slow heuristic search.

The second paper is **Cortical vs Subcortical Recordings to Drive Closed-Loop Deep Brain Stimulation in Essential Tremor**. This is a small four-patient implanted study, but it asks the right question. If you want movement-linked closed-loop control in essential tremor, should the biomarker come from the thalamus near stimulation, or from sensorimotor cortex via a separate cortical strip? The authors report that cortical sensing performs better and remains more stable over time, with months of home use. That is valuable because it treats adaptive stimulation as a measurement-design problem, not just a threshold-tuning problem.

The third paper is **TuneS: Patient-specific model-based optimization of contact configuration in deep brain stimulation**. This is a ten-patient Parkinson's disease methods paper rather than outcome proof, but it is strong on intervention logic. TuneS combines patient-specific stimulation-spread models with predefined targets and avoidance constraints, including streamline targets, to predict contact configurations that improve target coverage while reducing stimulation of regions associated with side effects. That is more useful than treating programming as a black box or claiming connectomic personalization without an explicit optimization objective.

The fourth paper is **Limitations of Variational Laplace-Based Dynamic Causal Modelling for Multistable Cortical Circuits**. It is not a neuromodulation trial, but it matters for the mechanistic layer underneath many network-targeting and state-estimation papers. In simulated cortical circuits with bistability, period-doubling, and chaos, dynamic causal modelling could often identify the correct architecture yet still recover wrong extrinsic parameters and wrong reconstructed dynamics. The warning is simple: a clean effective-connectivity story can still be dynamically false.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For DBS programming:** ERNA and TuneS both argue that programming should be formalized around measurable targets and constraints, not protected as tacit clinician craft.
- **For adaptive DBS:** the essential-tremor paper suggests that biomarker location matters a lot. Recording remote cortex may outperform recording near the stimulated subcortical target when the goal is stable movement-linked control.
- **For connectomic personalization claims:** TuneS is useful because it spells out the optimization target. A lot of "precision DBS" papers never do.
- **For network and control papers used in intervention logic:** the DCM paper is a caution that getting the model family right does not guarantee parameter or dynamics recovery when the system is multistable.
- **For repo framing:** today’s best set is programming physiology plus adaptive sensing plus explicit optimization plus computational skepticism. That is a healthier mix than another pile of small symptom papers.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful pattern today is that credible personalization needs an objective measurement or optimization layer. ERNA-guided programming matters because it gives DBS programming a fast physiological anchor. The essential-tremor closed-loop paper matters because it compares candidate sensing substrates in real use and finds that cortical signals may travel better over time than local subcortical ones. TuneS matters because it turns target coverage and avoidance into an explicit optimization problem. And the DCM paper matters because mechanistic inference can fail even when the model-selection output looks reassuring. If the sensing or inference layer is weak, the personalization story is usually theater.
