## Short overview

The most relevant paper today is **Spatial Effects of Electrode Configurations and Source Depth in EEG Source Localization: Implications for Concurrent Noninvasive Brain Stimulation with HD-EEG**. Using simultaneous stereoelectroencephalography and two-hundred-fifty-six-channel high-density EEG from seven epilepsy patients with single-pulse intracranial stimulation as ground truth, the authors test what happens when electrodes near or far from the source are removed or interpolated. The result is pleasantly concrete: degradation near the source hurts localization substantially, distal degradation barely matters, and sparse or parametric inverse methods like MxNE, dipole fitting, and RAP-MUSIC are more resilient than common distributed methods. That is exactly the kind of boring but high-leverage constraint that should shape any concurrent stimulation-plus-EEG protocol.

The second paper is **Dynamic causal modeling of effective connectivity generating a reduced auditory deviance detection in juvenile myoclonic epilepsy**. In sixty people with juvenile myoclonic epilepsy and thirty-nine controls, the authors combine a roving auditory oddball task, two-hundred-fifty-six-channel EEG, dynamic causal modeling, and Parametric Empirical Bayes. The headline is not just smaller mismatch-negativity and P3a responses. It is a more specific network claim: juvenile myoclonic epilepsy shows reduced right superior-temporal to right inferior-frontal feedforward connectivity during deviance processing, plus increased intrinsic excitability in left superior temporal gyrus. That is more useful than saying epilepsy patients have altered cognition, because it points toward a tractable circuit-level deficit model.

The third paper is **Inverting Foundation Models of Brain Function with Simulation-Based Inference**. This arXiv preprint uses the TRIBEv2 brain-activity foundation model plus large language model-generated stimuli to ask whether synthetic brain maps can be inverted back to latent stimulus properties like valence, arousal, and dominance. It is still proof-of-concept work and not a neuromodulation paper. But it matters because it reframes whole-brain models as controllable experimental objects: instead of only predicting responses to given stimuli, the model is used to infer what kind of stimulus would produce a desired neural pattern. The idea is strategically important even if the present demo is still toy-sized.

The fourth paper is **Piezoelectric neuromodulation of the subthalamic nucleus ameliorates motor and nonmotor symptoms of Parkinson's disease**. In a six-hydroxydopamine mouse model, ultrasound-responsive piezoelectric nanoparticles were injected into subthalamic nucleus and then driven wirelessly with ultrasound over several days. The authors report improvement in gait, pain, anxiety-like phenotypes, dopaminergic preservation, and reduced neuroinflammatory and mitochondrial pathology. The interesting part is not the disease-overclaim. It is the attempt to approximate deep-target stimulation without conventional implanted leads. Still, this remains invasive in a different way, biologically early, and far from proving a clinically credible replacement for deep brain stimulation.

## Most relevant paper

**Spatial Effects of Electrode Configurations and Source Depth in EEG Source Localization: Implications for Concurrent Noninvasive Brain Stimulation with HD-EEG**

This gets top rank because it addresses a hidden failure mode in many noninvasive control and targeting setups. If electrodes near the source are occluded by stimulation hardware, then downstream state estimation, localization, and target-engagement claims can become systematically distorted. The useful outcome here is not that everything breaks. It is that the paper maps where the breakage is strongest and which inverse methods are more robust under the exact kind of sensor loss that concurrent stimulation experiments create.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For concurrent stimulation and sensing:** the source-localization paper should tighten protocol expectations. Near-source electrode loss matters much more than distal loss, so montage convenience is not neutral.
- **For epilepsy network biomarkers:** the juvenile myoclonic epilepsy paper suggests that reduced auditory deviance detection may be better framed as weakened right-lateralized feedforward effective connectivity than as a generic ERP attenuation.
- **For computational modeling:** the foundation-model inversion preprint is worth tracking because it treats synthetic neural emulators as objects for inverse inference and controllable experiment design, not just representation learning.
- **For translational hype control:** the piezoelectric subthalamic paper is interesting hardware biology, but it should not be mistaken for a near-term deep-brain-stimulation replacement.
- **For repo framing:** today stays balanced across methods, network mechanism, computational modeling, and translational neuromodulation without letting the clinically shinier mouse paper dominate the ranking.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful pattern today is that intervention logic only gets stronger when the measurement layer, the mechanistic layer, and the generative-model layer all become less hand-wavy. The HD-EEG source-localization paper says practical sensor geometry matters more than people like to admit. The juvenile myoclonic epilepsy paper gives a cleaner directional-connectivity account of deviance-processing impairment. The foundation-model inversion preprint hints at a future where simulated brain models are used for inverse design, not just forward prediction. And the piezoelectric subthalamic paper is a reminder that new actuation hardware is only interesting if it eventually meets the same mechanistic and translational standards as old hardware.
