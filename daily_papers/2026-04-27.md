## Short overview

The most relevant paper today is **Personalized Virtual Brain Simulations and Vagus Nerve Stimulation Outcomes in Pediatric Epilepsy: A Multicenter Study From the CONNECTiVOS Collaboration**. The authors built individualized reduced Wong-Wang virtual brains from preimplant diffusion and functional MRI in thirty-eight children with drug-resistant epilepsy undergoing VNS. Model fit to empirical functional connectivity was worse in nonresponders, and responders showed stronger inferred inhibitory weights in parts of the vagal afferent network, including thalamic, cingulate, and frontal regions. The useful read is that VNS response may depend on whether a patient’s network can be captured by a relatively coherent mesoscopic dynamical model, not just on seizure labels or demographics. The caveat is that the sample is still small, outcome is responder status rather than richer longitudinal control, and the inferred inhibitory parameters are model-dependent.

The second paper is **Reduced pre-treatment isolated effective coherence between the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex and the subgenual anterior cingulate cortex as a potential predictive marker for remission following repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation in major depressive disorder**. In thirty adults with major depressive disorder treated with high-frequency left DLPFC rTMS, lower baseline alpha-band directed connectivity from left DLPFC to sgACC was associated with remission, with an area under the curve of 0.75 and an odds ratio of 0.25 in multivariable analysis. This is not enough to call it a deployable biomarker, but it is better than generic correlation worship because it tests a directional pre-treatment circuit quantity tied to a real treatment question.

The third paper is **Brain-computer interface-based neurofeedback training enables transferable control of cortical state switching in humans**. The main claim is that real-time neurofeedback can train people to deliberately modulate sensorimotor state transitions, and that the learned control generalizes beyond the explicit BCI setting. Data-driven latent-state analysis linked successful control to stronger interregional phase coupling and steeper broadband spectral slope in medial frontal cortex, alongside faster muscle contraction and relaxation reaction times. This matters because it treats brain-state control as something trainable and transferable, not just measurable after the fact.

The fourth paper is **Bilateral DLPFC Transcranial Direct Current Stimulation for Mood and Motor Symptoms in Parkinson's Disease: A Preliminary Study**. Patients with Parkinson’s disease and mild-to-moderate depressive symptoms received ten bilateral DLPFC tDCS sessions, and the authors report improvements in mood, anxiety, apathy, motor ratings, quality of life, and daily step count. The interesting part is the apathy and behavior angle, not the strength of the efficacy claim. Without a sham arm, this is still soft clinical evidence.

## Most relevant paper

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful pattern today is that neuromodulation gets more interesting when papers stop treating response as a black box. The pediatric VNS study tries to model response heterogeneity with patient-specific dynamical brains. The rTMS paper asks whether directional DLPFC to sgACC coupling can separate remitters from nonremitters before treatment. The neurofeedback paper shows that cortical state switching is not just a descriptive latent story but something humans may learn to control. And the Parkinson tDCS paper is worth keeping mainly because it hints that apathy-linked motivational circuitry may sit closer to functional benefit than another narrow motor-score narrative.
