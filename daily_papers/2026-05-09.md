## Short overview

The most relevant paper today is **Fronto-insular circuit mechanisms of accelerated intermittent theta burst stimulation**. In a mouse optogenetic model of prelimbic accelerated intermittent theta burst stimulation, the authors report synapse-related transcriptional changes, more dendritic spines, stronger excitatory currents in prefrontal intratelencephalic neurons, and a fronto-insular circuit that appears necessary and sufficient for antidepressant-like effects. They then add a human bridge using stereo-EEG and resting-state fMRI to argue that insula engagement is not just a rodent curiosity. The useful read is not that one circuit has been solved. It is that aiTBS mechanism can be framed as target-site plasticity plus downstream fronto-insular recruitment rather than as generic prefrontal stimulation magic.

The second paper is **Stable depression subtypes identified using functional connectome normative deviation models and their response to rTMS**. Using over twelve hundred patients with depression and more than sixteen hundred healthy controls, the authors build individual connectome-deviation maps relative to a normative model and cluster them into two reproducible subtypes. Only one subtype showed significant anhedonia improvement after dorsolateral prefrontal rTMS. This is still preliminary and not yet the kind of stratification result that should drive clinical deployment, but it is more useful than decorative subtype talk because the subtype definition is tied to individual deviation structure and then tested against treatment response.

The third paper is **Postapproval Study for Brain-Responsive Neurostimulation for Drug-Resistant Focal Epilepsy: Three-Year Efficacy and Interim Safety Results**. This is not the most novel item today, and it already has a repo note, but it remains worth keeping in circulation. Across three years, real-world responsive neurostimulation outcomes were strong and broadly consistent across major onset-group splits. The paper does not solve detection-policy design or adaptive-control theory, but it is the sort of durable benchmark new closed-loop claims should have to face.
