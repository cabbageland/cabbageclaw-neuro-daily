## Short overview

The most relevant paper today is **Differential effects of individualized vs. fixed-frequency tACS on clinical and EEG outcomes in major depressive disorder**. In a randomized double-blind trial, seventy-two outpatients with major depressive disorder were assigned to fixed ten-hertz versus individualized-alpha-frequency transcranial alternating current stimulation, each delivered either once or twice daily over twenty sessions. The clinically important result is not a personalization win. There was no significant efficacy advantage for individualized-alpha stimulation over fixed-frequency stimulation, and no clean administration-frequency effect either. The useful residue is narrower: fixed-frequency and higher-administration arms showed trend-level symptom improvement, while EEG measures suggested protocol-dependent modulation of frontal connectivity and parietal alpha power. That makes this more useful as a brake on casual IAF personalization rhetoric than as a success story.

The second paper is **Hippocampal-parietal directed connectivity mediates brain network reconfiguration between internal and external attention: An intracranial EEG study**. Using stereo-EEG from seventeen patients with refractory epilepsy performing a sustained-attention task, the authors built directed effective-connectivity graphs with high-frequency broadband activity and Granger causality. External attention was associated with stronger global causal connectivity and more connector-hub structure, while internal attention showed higher modularity and more peripheral-node organization. The most interesting mechanistic point is that hippocampal self-dynamics and hippocampal outflow to middle temporal gyrus were among the most discriminative features, which is a more memory-linked account of attentional state switching than generic frontoparietal slogans.

The third paper is **MapperEEG: A topological approach to brain state clustering in EEG recordings**. This is not a clinical neuromodulation paper, but it matters because a lot of adaptive-stimulation and biomarker work quietly assumes that brain states can be clustered cleanly. MapperEEG combines classical EEG features with topological Mapper graphs to cluster states in spectral space and represent their connectivity, then compares against six other clustering approaches including hierarchical clustering, hidden Markov models, and basic autoencoders. The authors report better state identification in a tapping task and useful structural readouts even when clustering is otherwise messy. If that holds up, it is a potentially practical upgrade for state discovery rather than another vague topology-for-brains performance piece.

The fourth paper is **Micro-Stimulation Timing Framed Around an Averaged Theta Period of Stimulation Determines Hippocampal Recruitment in Cued Fear Conditioning**. In mice, the authors delivered six-pulse microstimulation patterns across a one-hundred-forty-millisecond theta cycle in CA3 during auditory fear conditioning, comparing a fixed temporal pattern against randomized timing and sham. Only the fixed-pattern stimulation increased freezing relative to sham and produced stronger hippocampal c-Fos recruitment after retrieval. The useful lesson is not that this is ready for translation. It is that temporal packaging itself can act like a control variable, which matters for any stimulation program that claims phase sensitivity without specifying what temporal code is actually being imposed.

## Most relevant paper

**Differential effects of individualized vs. fixed-frequency tACS on clinical and EEG outcomes in major depressive disorder**

This gets top rank because it tests a personalization claim that is highly relevant and often weakly interrogated. If individualized alpha frequency is supposed to matter, then a randomized comparison against a fixed ten-hertz protocol is exactly the kind of test that should be done. The result is usefully awkward: the personalized version did not beat fixed frequency on clinical efficacy, and the EEG shifts do not rescue a weak behavioral result. That is still valuable because it narrows what future tACS personalization work has to prove.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For noninvasive stimulation personalization:** the tACS paper is a useful negative or at least nonconfirmatory result. Individualized alpha frequency does not automatically outperform a fixed ten-hertz protocol.
- **For network-state framing:** the SEEG attention paper supports a directed, state-switching view in which hippocampal-cortical coupling helps mediate internal versus external attention.
- **For methods:** MapperEEG is worth watching because adaptive control stories depend on state discovery, and this paper at least compares against recognizable clustering baselines instead of only announcing a fancy representation.
- **For temporally coded neuromodulation:** the hippocampal microstimulation paper suggests that within-cycle timing pattern can matter independently of gross stimulation dose.
- **For repo framing:** today stays balanced across clinical neuromodulation, network neuroscience, methods, and preclinical timing/control without letting the clinical paper crowd out the mechanistic ones.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful pattern today is that better intervention logic needs stronger discipline about what counts as personalization, what counts as a brain state, and what counts as timing. The tACS trial says individualized alpha frequency is not yet an earned antidepressant targeting principle. The intracranial attention paper sharpens the state-transition story with directed hippocampal-temporal-parietal interactions rather than just broad resting-state labels. MapperEEG matters because if state clustering is weak, then closed-loop rhetoric built on those states is fragile. And the hippocampal microstimulation paper is a reminder that temporal structure itself may be part of the intervention, not just the carrier for it.
