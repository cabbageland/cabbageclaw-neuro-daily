## Short overview

The most relevant paper today is **Digital twin brain reveals state-specific stimulation targets for abnormal brain dynamics in tinnitus**. The authors built a tinnitus-specific digital twin brain from multimodal imaging and used more than 1.64 million virtual stimulations to map how different targets shift two abnormal whole-brain states, one more sensory-weighted and one more cognitive-default-mode weighted. The useful claim is not that tinnitus targeting is solved. The useful claim is that state-specific target choice may be the right abstraction, and the authors do at least validate their response maps against an independent repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation dataset.

The second paper is **Controlling the human connectome with spatially diffuse input signals**. This is not a clinical trial. It is a network-control paper arguing that the usual node-by-node control assumption is physically silly for cortex and for real stimulation devices. When the authors allow inputs to spread spatially with exponential decay, state transitions require much less energy and can often be approximated with far fewer input sites. That matters because intervention logic should respect the fact that stimulation is not a perfectly local delta function.

The third paper is **Circadian limbic and thalamic beta oscillations drive slow-adapting dual-threshold adaptive deep brain stimulation in Tourette syndrome**. In one patient with refractory Tourette syndrome and obsessive-compulsive disorder, chronic sensing from ventral capsule or ventral striatum and centromedian-parafascicular thalamus showed circadian beta rhythms that were then used to reduce stimulation automatically during sleep. The result is still fragile single-patient evidence, but it is a respectable proof of concept for slow adaptive control driven by a clinically usable day-night signal rather than by fragile moment-to-moment detection.

The fourth paper is **Anatomy-informed recommendations for electrode montage and shape in electrical stimulation methods: a tDCS case study**. Using finite-element simulations in 590 Human Connectome Project-Aging participants, the authors show that montage, skull thickness, cerebrospinal-fluid thickness, cortical thickness, local gyrification, and sulcal depth all materially shift where current actually goes. The important read is not that one montage wins universally. It is that standardized dosing still produces highly nonstandardized electric fields.

## Most relevant paper

**Digital twin brain reveals state-specific stimulation targets for abnormal brain dynamics in tinnitus**

This gets top rank because it is one of the cleaner recent attempts to turn target selection into an explicit dynamical-response question rather than a static lesion-or-connectivity slogan. The useful move is that it separates sensory and cognitive abnormal states, derives different causal response maps for each, and then validates those maps against real rTMS outcome variation. It is still a disease-specific paper and still model-dependent, but the framing transfers well to psychiatric and neuromodulation work more broadly.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For digital-twin targeting:** the tinnitus paper is a real framing upgrade because it asks which target perturbs which pathological state, not just which site correlates with symptoms.
- **For network control logic:** the diffuse-input paper weakens the default habit of treating brain control as independent node poking and gives a better physical prior for stimulation design.
- **For adaptive DBS:** the Tourette case is small, but it is a sensible reminder that slow circadian adaptation may be easier to deploy robustly than hyper-reactive closed-loop fantasies.
- **For tDCS targeting:** the anatomy-informed montage paper is mostly a methods result, but it should make anyone suspicious of one-size-fits-all dosing claims.
- **For repo framing:** today’s set is balanced the right way, with one translational digital-twin paper, one control-theory paper, one adaptive clinical proof of concept, and one large-sample anatomy and targeting methods paper.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful pattern today is that neuromodulation gets better when papers stop pretending stimulation is either perfectly local or perfectly generic. The tinnitus digital-twin study asks which target changes which abnormal state. The diffuse-input control paper shows that realistic spatial spread changes the control problem itself. The Tourette adaptive DBS case uses a slow, interpretable biomarker tied to actual sleep-wake needs instead of chasing flashy millisecond adaptivity. And the tDCS montage paper reminds us that anatomy still decides a lot of what the protocol really is. Taken together, this is a good day for state-aware and geometry-aware intervention logic.
