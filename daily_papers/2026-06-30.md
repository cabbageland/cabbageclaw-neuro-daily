## Short overview

The top paper matters because it asks a question more neuromodulation papers should ask: can you stimulate and still trust what you record? The answer here is partial but useful. By delivering premodulated amplitude-modulated kilohertz waveforms directly through implanted electrodes, the authors argue that hardware-generated intermodulation artifacts can be reduced substantially with parallel stimulators and separate electrodes, while amplifier-side intermodulation still has to be taken seriously. In one tested pathway, the waveform produced neural responses similar to square pulses, and the NEURON-side MRG axon simulation mirrored the threshold pattern. That is not a clinical endpoint. It is better than that for today’s purpose. It is a measurement note that could make future closed-loop work less fraudulent.

The comparison stack is what makes the day cohere. **Closing the loop between brain and electrical stimulation: A proof-of-concept randomized trial of real-time fMRI-guided tACS optimization** remains the cleaner personalization baseline when someone wants adaptive stimulation to mean more than fixed-parameter repetition. **Quantifying State-Dependent Control Properties of Brain Dynamics from Perturbation Responses** remains the better control-theory warning that controllability claims should come from perturbation data rather than passive speculation. And **Frequency-dependent modulation of human reward circuitry: A comparative study of theta, gamma, and high-frequency temporal interference** remains the better recent human reminder that kilohertz or temporal-interference work only becomes interesting when parameter changes show circuit consequences.

I also checked the standing-interest lanes. **Hypnosis reduces decoding accuracy of visual and auditory representations** is an interesting recent perception-and-suggestion result, but from the accessible material it still looks more like a specific oddball-decoding effect than a better hypnosis mechanism keep than **Decoding hypnotic consciousness: neural and experiential insights into induced and ideomotor suggestions**. ## Most relevant paper

**Direct delivery of modulated kilohertz frequency waveforms enable simultaneous electrical stimulation and recording with minimal-artifact**

This gets top rank because it starts at the layer where a lot of closed-loop neuromodulation talk quietly cheats. If the stimulation waveform corrupts the recording so badly that the response estimate is mostly artifact management, then the downstream controller is built on theater. The paper’s core claim is that directly delivered premodulated kilohertz waveforms can reduce hardware-side intermodulation enough to make simultaneous recording more credible, while also exposing that the amplifier itself can still generate phantom structure. That is exactly the kind of unglamorous but high-leverage note worth preserving.

The caveat is not hidden. This preserve is abstract-only after 10 full-text acquisition attempts because the publisher’s supposedly open route was blocked by bot protection in this environment. So confidence is deliberately moderate on the framing and headline result, not high on the quantitative engineering margins. Still, even with that limit, the paper is more useful than a generic waveform paper because it treats observability as part of intervention design rather than as post hoc cleanup.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful June thirtieth pattern is that control gets more serious when measurement gets less fake. The new kilohertz-waveform paper says you do not get to talk cleanly about adaptive stimulation if the sensing stack is still artifact soup. The closed-loop tACS optimization note says personalization should be explicit. The perturbation-control note says controllability should be estimated from actual responses. And the temporal-interference reward-circuit note says parameter changes only matter when they show circuit consequences. The common lesson is simple: before asking how to steer the brain, make sure you can tell what moved.
