## Short overview

The most relevant paper today is **Canonical Hidden Markov Model Networks for studying M/EEG**. This is worth keeping because it addresses a real bottleneck in network neuroscience and intervention-adjacent biomarker work: dynamic state models are often trained on tiny one-off datasets and then treated as if their latent states mean the same thing across studies. By pretraining canonical hidden Markov models on 1,849 MEG sessions from Cam-CAN and releasing reusable models across multiple state counts, the authors offer a shared dynamic-network basis for later boutique MEG and EEG studies. That does not by itself solve causal inference or adaptive control, but it improves the substrate on which those claims are often built.

## Most relevant paper

**Canonical Hidden Markov Model Networks for studying M/EEG**

This gets top rank because it improves the comparability problem that keeps showing up whenever people want to build biomarkers, latent-state models, or adaptive intervention logic from electrophysiology. If every small dataset trains its own state model, then downstream claims about state tracking, disease signatures, or stimulation timing live on shaky semantic ground. A canonical state basis will not magically make those claims true, but it is a better starting point than endless bespoke latent-state reinvention.
