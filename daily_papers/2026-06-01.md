## Short overview

The best new keep today is **Skull-Conforming Acoustic Holographic Lenses for Transcranial Targeting**, currently on arXiv. It is not a clinical outcome paper, but it is exactly the kind of methods paper that can make later neuromodulation claims either believable or fake. The useful contribution is not merely another hologram buzzword. It is the combination of subject-specific phase correction, conformal coupling geometry, volumetric target optimization, and ex vivo skull validation. If deep ultrasound targeting is going to mature, this is much closer to the right problem formulation than generic “focused” marketing.

The most clinically provocative fresh paper is **Accelerated intermittent theta burst stimulation targeting personalized fronto-parietal control network improves core symptoms of autism spectrum disorder: a double-blind, randomized controlled trial** in *Molecular Psychiatry*. The attractive part is the attempt to personalize fronto-parietal targeting in a population where circuit heterogeneity matters and where core-symptom treatment options are weak. The caution is that the design combines active or sham stimulation with standard behavioral therapy, the primary endpoint is modest, exploratory measures are mostly null, and the strongest-looking effect lands in the more impaired module-one subgroup. That makes it worth tracking, but not worth overclaiming.

For computational and systems framing, I re-inspected **Controlling the human connectome with spatially diffuse input signals** in *Communications Biology*. It is not new to the repo, but it still matters because it fixes a physically silly assumption common in network-control papers, namely that inputs act on isolated nodes instead of spatially spreading through nearby cortex. That is the sort of modeling correction that can quietly improve how we think about stimulation, targeting blur, and state-transition feasibility.

I also explicitly scouted hypnosis, hypnotherapy, and CBT-plus-interventional-psychiatry combinations using Brave first. I found material, including the older SHIFT line and hypnotherapy-versus-CBT imaging work, but nothing new and strong enough today to displace the three papers above.

## Most relevant paper

**Skull-Conforming Acoustic Holographic Lenses for Transcranial Targeting**

This gets top rank because a surprising amount of ultrasound neuromodulation rhetoric still floats on top of weak delivery assumptions. The paper’s real value is not a headline about precision. It is that the authors explicitly combine CT and MRI-derived subject geometry, target-specific optimization, a conformal coupling layer, and ex vivo human-skull validation. That does not prove clinical benefit, but it does move the field toward a more honest physical basis for later targeting claims.

- **For computational control:** the spatially diffuse control paper remains a useful baseline correction for anyone talking about brain-state steering under realistic stimulation spread.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The honest pattern today is that better intervention science starts earlier than the symptom chart. The strongest paper improves the physical plausibility of deep ultrasound targeting. The ASD trial is worth watching because it tries individualized circuit targeting in a clinically hard setting, but it is not decisive evidence that personalized stimulation has solved core symptoms. And the control-theory paper is a reminder that stimulation logic should respect spatial spread from the beginning. Put together, the right read is: better physics, better targeting assumptions, and more restraint about what personalization has really earned.
