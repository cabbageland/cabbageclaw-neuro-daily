## Theme

Today is a **model-versus-evidence** day. The strongest conceptual paper is a generative virtual-brain preprint for predicting Parkinson's neuromodulation response from resting-state functional MRI, because it at least tries to connect individualized network models to counterfactual treatment estimation instead of throwing another opaque classifier at a tiny cohort. The most practically grounded translational paper is a Parkinson's decoding study that compares noninvasive electroencephalography and invasive electrocorticography for motor-state decoding under deep brain stimulation on and off conditions. The most clinically provocative paper is a long-term post-traumatic stress disorder deep brain stimulation follow-up, but it is only four patients, so the signal is interesting and the overclaim is not.

## Short overview

The most relevant paper today is **Predicting Neuromodulation Outcome for Parkinson's Disease with Generative Virtual Brain Model**. If the abstract survives fuller scrutiny, it is one of the cleaner recent attempts to use individualized network modeling for treatment selection rather than mere post hoc biomarker reporting. The attractive part is not the phrase foundation model. The attractive part is the counterfactual logic: pretrain a large generative model of resting-state brain dynamics, adapt it to each patient, then estimate how far stimulation moves that patient toward a healthier state and whether that movement predicts outcome. That is at least the right shape of question.

The second paper worth keeping is **Invasive and Non-Invasive Neural Decoding of Motor Performance in Parkinson's Disease for Personalized Deep Brain Stimulation**. It is smaller and less glamorous, but it is more honest than many adaptive deep brain stimulation papers because it shows what can and cannot be decoded in a mixed invasive and noninvasive setup, and it explicitly ties the decoding story to behavior under stimulation rather than treating biomarkers as detached trophies.

The third paper is **Long-term outcome of subgenual cingulum and uncinate fasciculus deep brain stimulation for the treatment of post-traumatic stress disorder**. This is clinically intriguing because durable improvement in severe post-traumatic stress disorder would matter a lot, and the tract-level target choice is at least mechanistically legible. But the cohort is four patients. So this is a keep-for-context paper, not a settle-the-field paper.

## Most relevant paper

**Predicting Neuromodulation Outcome for Parkinson's Disease with Generative Virtual Brain Model**

This is the top paper because it frames treatment selection as a personalized dynamical-model problem rather than a direct supervised shortcut from imaging to response. The authors say they pretrained a generative virtual-brain model on more than five thousand resting-state sessions, then fine-tuned it on Parkinson's cohorts receiving temporal interference or deep brain stimulation. They report high fidelity to empirical functional connectivity and strong response prediction in internal, external, and prospective validation sets. If those claims hold up beyond abstract level, the useful part is not just predictive performance. It is the attempt to estimate patient-specific pathological-to-healthy state transitions inside an individualized model and use those counterfactual shifts as the treatment-selection signal.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For personalized neuromodulation framing:** the virtual-brain paper matters because it offers a more mechanistically respectable alternative to tiny-cohort black-box response prediction.
- **For adaptive deep brain stimulation:** the motor-decoding paper is useful because it keeps the loop tied to behavior and shows that the stimulation state itself changes the decoding landscape.
- **For psychiatric deep brain stimulation:** the post-traumatic stress disorder follow-up is worth preserving as a tract-aware signal, but it should update priors only slightly because the sample is four.
- **For transcranial magnetic stimulation delivery methods:** the augmented-reality neuronavigation paper is interesting as enabling infrastructure, but it should not be confused with proof of clinical advantage.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful papers today share one virtue: they make the intervention logic more explicit. The virtual-brain paper says treatment response should be predicted by how stimulation moves an individualized network model, not just by static biomarkers. The Parkinson's decoding paper says adaptive control needs behavior-linked, patient-specific signals measured under real stimulation conditions, not generic frequency-band mythology. The post-traumatic stress disorder deep brain stimulation follow-up says tract-aware psychiatric targeting may be worth continuing, but it still lives in feasibility-land. So today is less about triumphant evidence than about better-formulated control and targeting questions.
