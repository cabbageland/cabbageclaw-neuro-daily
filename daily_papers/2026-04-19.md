## Short overview

The most relevant paper today is **Multimodal Image Guidance in Subthalamic Deep Brain Stimulation for Parkinson's Disease**. A lot of imaging-guided DBS work is decorative because it predicts between-patient outcomes while clinicians still have to test contacts manually. This paper at least aims at the harder, more clinically meaningful problem: discriminating among candidate contacts within patients. Using coordinates, electric fields, tract activations, and structural plus functional network features inside a combined ridge-regression framework, the authors report modest group-level variance explained in unseen data but substantially better contact-ranking performance at the individual level, identifying the optimal contact or a neighboring contact in all but one case. That is not magical precision programming, but it is enough to matter.

The second paper is **Non-invasive Neuromodulation Targeting Approach by Mapping Stimulations and Lesions That Modify Visual Memory**. This is not a trial, but it is a good example of network-guided target discovery that does more than gesture vaguely at "memory circuits." Across TMS, penetrating head trauma, ischemic stroke, and an independent Alzheimer's-related replication dataset, the authors claim that lesions and stimulation sites associated with visual-memory change converge onto a specific network, with effect direction flipping by age at stimulation or injury. The attractive part is not that it nominates one perfect site. It is that it uses convergent perturbation logic and then translates that into candidate TMS target maps.

The third paper is **Transcranial temporal interference stimulation targeting the subthalamic region for motor symptoms in Parkinson's disease: a pilot, randomised, double-blind, sham-controlled crossover study**. This is exactly the kind of result that invites overclaiming, so restraint matters. The good news is that a single individualized twenty-minute session in thirty medication-off patients beat sham on short-term MDS-UPDRS-III change and responder rate without obvious safety problems. The bad news is that it is still one small acute-effect study, and temporal-interference targeting claims remain anatomically and mechanistically contentious.

A fourth paper stayed below full-note threshold but was worth scanning: **Higher-order intrinsic routes support flexible task-evoked communication**. It is a strong network-neuroscience idea — task-specific communication as selective recruitment of higher-order resting-state routes rather than wholesale rewiring — but it is still some distance from direct intervention logic, so I kept it as adjacent inspiration instead of a preserved note.

## Most relevant paper

**Multimodal Image Guidance in Subthalamic Deep Brain Stimulation for Parkinson's Disease**

This gets top rank because it targets a real bottleneck in precision neuromodulation: postoperative programming remains laborious, and many imaging papers help less than advertised because they operate at the wrong level of decision-making. Here, the key claim is that combining contact location, electric-field estimates, tract engagement, and structural plus functional network features can help identify the best contact within patients, not just correlate with cohort outcomes. The reported out-of-sample group-level R-squared is only about 0.12, which is not impressive if spun as prediction, but the individual-level contact-ranking result is much more clinically meaningful. The paper is still constrained by moderate explanatory power and by the limits of imaging-derived proxies, yet it looks like real progress rather than packaging.

- **For network-guided targeting:** the visual-memory mapping paper is a reminder that lesion-network and stimulation-network convergence can generate candidate targets more honestly than one-modality correlational fishing.
- **For noninvasive deep stimulation:** the temporal-interference Parkinson's pilot is interesting enough to track, but not enough to treat as settled evidence for selective subthalamic engagement.
- **For repo framing:** today reinforces that useful precision-neuromodulation work usually earns its keep through targeting logic, not just by reporting another mean clinical effect.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful pattern today is that targeting papers are worthwhile only when they help an actual intervention decision. The image-guided STN DBS paper clears that bar because it tries to rank contacts within patients and gets plausible if still modest results. The visual-memory mapping paper matters because it uses perturbation convergence across lesions and stimulation to derive candidate noninvasive targets, with an important age interaction rather than a fake universal map. The temporal-interference Parkinson's study is promising but still pilot-scale; it should update curiosity, not confidence.
