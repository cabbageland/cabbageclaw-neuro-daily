## Short overview

The most relevant paper today is **Multimodal network localization of brain abnormalities and neuromodulation target identification in obsessive-compulsive disorder**. The authors use coordinate-based network mapping to integrate structural abnormalities, executive-function findings, and emotion-processing findings in OCD, then compare those convergent maps against candidate neuromodulation targets. The useful part is that they do not pretend OCD is one monolithic abnormality blob. They recover partially dissociable networks that still converge on basal-ganglia circuitry, and they show that dorsolateral prefrontal cortex and supplementary motor area targets line up better with executive-network abnormalities than orbitofrontal cortex does. That is still a normative mapping exercise, not proof that these are the right intervention targets in any given patient.

The second paper is **Linking human brain functional connectivity to underlying neurotransmission**. Across six healthy cohorts using resting-state functional MRI and magnetoencephalography, the authors report that regional functional-connectivity variation aligns reproducibly with neurotransmitter receptor and transporter distributions, with a particularly prominent noradrenergic signature in a sensorimotor and posterior-insular network linked to autonomic arousal. This is not a stimulation paper, but it is intervention-relevant because it offers a cleaner biological bridge between systems-level connectivity claims and putative chemical levers.

The third paper is **How Statistical Methods, Hemispheric Data and Masking Approaches Shape Probabilistic Sweet Spots in Deep Brain Stimulation**. This is a useful anti-folklore paper. Using intraoperative stimulation-test data in Parkinson disease, the authors show that the shape and stability of probabilistic sweet spots depend heavily on the statistical test, cohort size, and whether hemispheres are merged. The main practical claim is that Bayesian t-tests stabilize sooner than the common Wilcoxon-based approaches, and that combining hemispheres can hide clinically relevant asymmetries rather than improve robustness.

The fourth paper is **Intermittent Theta-Burst Stimulation (iTBS) Modulates Abnormal Brain Activity During Emotional Arousal in Adolescent Depression: A Pilot Study**. This is the weakest note today, but still worth preserving. In adolescents with depression, the paper reports reduced superior and middle frontal responses during emotionally arousing video viewing, some symptom improvement after accelerated iTBS, and an exploratory association between target proximity to superior frontal abnormality and anxiety-score reduction. The right read is not precision-biomarker triumph. The right read is that adolescent TMS targeting remains suggestive and preliminary.

## Most relevant paper

**Multimodal network localization of brain abnormalities and neuromodulation target identification in obsessive-compulsive disorder**

This gets top rank because it does a better job than most psychiatric-network papers of separating symptom-relevant systems instead of flattening them. The paper is still based on normative network mapping and meta-analytic coordinates, so it is not individualized targeting evidence. But it gives a sharper intervention frame than generic cortico-striato-thalamo-cortical slogans by asking which candidate targets align with which dysfunction network.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For OCD targeting:** the multimodal network-localization paper supports a more differentiated target story in which dorsolateral prefrontal cortex and supplementary motor area may make more sense for executive-network disruption than orbitofrontal cortex does.
- **For mechanistic interpretation of connectomics:** the neurotransmission paper is a strong reminder that large-scale connectivity structure may reflect distributed receptor and transporter architecture, not just abstract graph geometry.
- **For DBS mapping practice:** the probabilistic-sweet-spots paper should lower confidence in any target map that does not report cohort size sensitivity, hemispheric handling, and statistical-method dependence.
- **For adolescent depression neuromodulation:** the iTBS pilot is not strong enough to change clinical practice, but it does suggest that target-distance-to-abnormality may be a better framing than generic scalp-rule placement.
- **For repo balance:** today intentionally keeps one clinical paper in the mix, but the stronger value comes from network and methods work that clarifies how targets and biomarkers should be interpreted.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The common thread today is that target claims need better grounding at three levels. First, psychiatric abnormalities should be localized as networks with partially distinct functional roles, not as one big disease blob. Second, systems-level connectivity claims become more interesting when they can be related to underlying neurotransmitter architecture. Third, any map of “optimal stimulation sites” is only as trustworthy as the statistics and symmetry assumptions used to build it. The clinical pilot in adolescent depression is a decent reminder of what happens when those layers are still weak: you get a plausible target story with only modest evidence underneath it.
