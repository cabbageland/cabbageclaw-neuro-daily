# 2026-03-29

## Theme

Today is mostly a **targeting-and-evidence-quality** day rather than a breakthrough-mechanism day. The strongest paper is a large cross-target DBS mapping study in Tourette syndrome that tries to move beyond single-target folklore toward fiber-informed response landscapes. The other notable hits are mostly useful because they trim hype: a naturalistic personalized accelerated TMS report that is directionally interesting but far too uncontrolled to cash its precision-psychiatry rhetoric, a tiny randomized coil-comparison trial that mostly says both active depression protocols can move symptoms without separating cleanly, and a temporal-interference schizophrenia pilot that is mechanically provocative but clinically much too small and open-label to trust.

## Short overview

The most worth-preserving paper today is **Optimal Deep Brain Stimulation Locations for Gilles de la Tourette Syndrome** because it is the only one that really tries to unify multiple DBS targets using anatomy and outcome maps rather than treating each target as its own isolated doctrine. **Efficacy of Personalized Connectivity-Guided Accelerated Brain Stimulation in a Naturalistic Treatment-Resistant Depression Population** is worth keeping as a citation and caution note, not as decisive evidence: the targeting logic is interesting, but the design is uncontrolled, small, and mixed together with historical comparisons and post-hoc modeling. **Pilot Randomized Trial of Intermittent Theta-Burst Stimulation versus H-Coil Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation for Treatment-Resistant Depression** is a modestly useful practical null. **Efficacy and Safety of Transcranial Temporal Interference Stimulation for Improving Negative Symptoms and Cognition in Schizophrenia** sounds exciting on first read, but right now it is still eight patients, single arm, five sessions, and too fragile to treat as more than a feasibility whisper.

## Most relevant paper

**Optimal Deep Brain Stimulation Locations for Gilles de la Tourette Syndrome**

This is the top paper because it tries to solve an actual neuromodulation problem: different Tourette DBS targets have accumulated partial evidence, but the field still lacks a coherent account of whether they are engaging distinct mechanisms or different access points into overlapping therapeutic circuitry. The authors analyze 115 patients across 12 centers spanning thalamic, pallidal, and STN DBS, use voxel-wise sweetspot mapping, and interpret the resulting response landscapes against specific pallidothalamic and thalamostriatal fiber bundles. The useful claim is not that they found a magical coordinate, but that tic-response peaks across targets appear to align along a structural pathway logic, with some out-of-sample support. It is still retrospective, variance explained is limited, and obsessive-compulsive symptom effects look less clean than tic effects, but this is much closer to transferable targeting knowledge than another single-target case series.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For DBS targeting:** this supports a cross-target network view for Tourette DBS rather than nucleus-by-nucleus turf wars.
- **For psychiatric neuromodulation generally:** it strengthens the case for fiber-informed response mapping, but also shows how far the field still is from high-variance-explained prediction.
- **For personalized TMS framing:** the TAO-TMS paper should not be used as strong efficacy evidence. It is better treated as a hypothesis-generating translational report with some modeling support and a lot of design weakness.
- **For noninvasive deep stimulation claims:** the temporal-interference schizophrenia pilot is nowhere near enough to settle whether the target was really engaged, whether the gains are durable, or whether the apparent cognitive improvements outrun expectancy and repeated testing effects.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The honest read today is that the strongest neuromodulation work is still the work that exposes targeting structure rather than proclaiming outcome wins from small uncontrolled cohorts. The Tourette DBS paper is worth keeping because it tries to unify multiple targets under a fiber-based response logic and gives the field something more reusable than target tribalism. The personalized accelerated TMS paper is interesting as translational drift from idealized precision-TMS protocols into a comorbid real-world population, but it is not a clean demonstration. The coil-comparison depression trial and the temporal-interference schizophrenia pilot are both reminders that clinically exciting stimulation ideas often shrink back to feasibility-level evidence once you inspect the design.
