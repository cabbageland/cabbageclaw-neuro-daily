The strongest computational paper is **JEDI: Jointly Embedded Inference of Neural Dynamics**, not because it is immediately intervention-ready, but because it tries to infer shared and condition-specific dynamical structure across tasks instead of fitting one recurrent network per silo. ## Short overview

The most relevant new paper today is **Personalized fMRI-Guided TMS Targeting the Threat Neurocircuitry in PTSD: A Randomized Clinical Trial**. The useful part is not the phrase personalized. The useful part is that the personalization logic is actually tied to a circuit hypothesis: find the right dorsolateral prefrontal cortex site with the strongest functional connection to the right amygdala, then test whether low-frequency stimulation changes amygdala threat reactivity and symptoms. The result is not clean triumph. The mechanistic endpoint moved, the immediate autonomic endpoint did not, and the clearest symptom separation appears at follow-up rather than right after treatment. But this is still a better-formulated targeting paper than most psychiatric TMS work.

The second new paper worth preserving is **Jointly Embedded Inference of Neural Dynamics**. It is adjacent rather than clinical, but it matters because it attacks a real bottleneck in mechanistic modeling: most data-constrained recurrent models are trained condition by condition, which makes cross-task comparison awkward and generalization weak. JEDI proposes a hierarchical embedding over recurrent weights so that condition-specific dynamics live in a shared space. If that framework holds up beyond the abstract, it could be useful for state-transition framing, subject-condition comparison, and eventually for control-oriented modeling.

The third paper worth calling out is **Time-Varying Directed Interactions in Functional Brain Networks: Modeling and Validation**. This is more methods than intervention, but it is a respectable methods paper because it tries to estimate time-varying directed interactions rather than recycling sliding-window correlation. The multimodal validation, task sensitivity, and clinical application to post-concussion vestibular dysfunction make it more credible than a purely synthetic benchmark paper. Still, it is a directed-connectivity method paper, not a direct neuromodulation result, so it belongs in the useful-but-adjacent bucket.

A fourth paper remains important context rather than a new note: **Association of Brain Network Perturbations With Response to Vagus Nerve Stimulation in Children With Drug-Resistant Focal Epilepsy**. It already has a note in the repo, but it still deserves mention because it reinforces the same theme as the PTSD paper and JEDI paper: treatment heterogeneity and intervention logic become more legible when you model networks as dynamic and perturbable rather than as static correlation maps.

## Most relevant paper

**Personalized fMRI-Guided TMS Targeting the Threat Neurocircuitry in PTSD: A Randomized Clinical Trial**

This is the top paper because it at least tries to make psychiatric TMS targeting earn its circuitry rhetoric. The authors randomized fifty adults with PTSD to active or sham low-frequency TMS, using functional MRI to pick a right dorsolateral prefrontal cortex target with the strongest functional connection to the right amygdala. Active stimulation reduced right amygdala threat reactivity relative to sham, which is the cleanest part of the paper. The less clean part is that skin-conductance reactivity did not move and immediate post-treatment symptom differences were not significant between groups, although follow-up symptom differences favored active treatment. So the take is not that personalized threat-circuit TMS is settled. The take is that this is a relatively serious attempt to connect target selection, neural mechanism, and delayed clinical outcome in one trial.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For psychiatric TMS framing:** the PTSD trial matters because it uses a target defined by individual functional connectivity to a symptom-relevant subcortical region and then checks whether the hypothesized circuit endpoint actually changes.
- **For mechanistic psychiatry:** the paper supports a stronger standard than generic symptom-only trials. If a study claims circuit-guided personalization, it should measure circuit engagement, not just symptom averages.
- **For computational and control framing:** JEDI is useful because it treats neural dynamics inference as a shared latent-embedding problem across conditions, which is closer to the representation problem we actually care about than one-off decoder contests.
- **For network methods:** the directed-interaction paper is worth citing when we need a cleaner story about dynamic information flow than ordinary sliding-window correlation allows, but it does not by itself solve causal identification.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful papers today all refuse static thinking. The PTSD trial treats TMS targeting as a circuit-selection problem tied to amygdala reactivity. JEDI treats neural dynamics as something that should be modeled jointly across tasks and contexts rather than separately memorized. The directed-connectivity paper treats brain networks as time-varying and directional rather than as undirected similarity blobs. Even the already-preserved pediatric VNS paper fits the same pattern: response depends on network resilience to perturbation, not just on baseline labels. So the broader lesson is simple. Better neuromodulation work is moving toward explicit state, circuit, and perturbation logic. The field is still far from clean control theory in humans, but at least the better papers are asking questions in that direction.
