## Theme

Today is a **network-targeted stimulation gets more credible when the brain moves before the headline does** day. The strongest new keep is **Targeting cortico-striatal-amygdalar networks via theta-band frontoparietal synchronization in opioid use disorder: a randomized tACS-fMRI Trial**, because it does the rare grown-up thing in addiction neuromodulation: it shows a preregistered neural effect, ties it to electric-field dose and network engagement, and leaves the craving null visible. The second keep, **The physiological foundation of extinction improvement by tDCS over the ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC) in healthy humans: an fMRI study**, pushes the same honesty in a different register. It gives exposure-augmentation logic a more specific physiological story instead of pretending a frontal electrode automatically equals better therapy.

## Short overview

The top paper is worth keeping because it treats network-targeted interventional psychiatry like an engineering problem instead of a branding exercise. In sixty men with opioid use disorder, a single session of right frontoparietal six-hertz HD-tACS reduced opioid cue reactivity in ventral-striatal, amygdalar, ventral-tegmental, and posterior-cingulate territory, increased frontoparietal task coupling, and showed an electric-field-to-engagement relationship. What it did **not** do was beat sham on subjective craving. That is not failure theater. That is the useful result: the targeted network moved, the self-report endpoint did not, and the paper says so plainly.

The second keep matters because anxiety/exposure augmentation papers often wave at vmPFC and extinction without showing what changed. Here, anodal vmPFC tDCS during extinction improved early recall in skin conductance terms, increased vmPFC activation, recruited safety-network regions earlier, and increased vmPFC decoupling from fear-network regions. It is still a healthy-volunteer paradigm with exclusions and no clinical endpoint, but it gives the combination-treatment story a more defensible physiological bridge.

The context stack stays important. **Closing the loop between brain and electrical stimulation: A proof-of-concept randomized trial of real-time fMRI-guided tACS optimization** remains the cleaner personalization baseline for noninvasive network targeting. **Quantifying State-Dependent Control Properties of Brain Dynamics from Perturbation Responses** remains the better control-theory reminder that stimulation language should be anchored in perturbation responses rather than elegant passivity. For standing interests, no new hypnosis paper beat **Decoding hypnotic consciousness: neural and experiential insights into induced and ideomotor suggestions**, and no new CBT-plus-interventional paper beat the broader implementation framing already preserved in **Noninvasive brain stimulation combined with evidence-based psychotherapy for psychiatric disorders: A meta-analysis of optimal implementation parameters**.

## Most relevant paper

**Targeting cortico-striatal-amygdalar networks via theta-band frontoparietal synchronization in opioid use disorder: a randomized tACS-fMRI Trial**

This gets top rank because it does not confuse a tractable intermediate result with a completed treatment story. The paper names a control-network target, stimulates it with a clear six-hertz frontoparietal montage, shows reduced cue reactivity in striatal and amygdalar reward circuitry, shows increased frontoparietal task engagement, and shows that stronger modeled electric field predicts stronger engagement. That is the right kind of interventional psychiatry note to preserve.

The caveat is the point. This was a single-session study in sixty men in early abstinence, and subjective craving did not significantly separate from sham. Good. That means the paper is more useful as a network-engagement and biomarker note than as a clinical efficacy claim. The field needs more of that and less symptom-overclaiming glued onto underpowered neural stories.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For interventional psychiatry framing:** the OUD tACS paper argues that neural engagement can be a real win even when self-report endpoints stay null, which is a healthier standard than pretending every brain shift is already a treatment effect.
- **For exposure-augmentation framing:** the vmPFC tDCS paper suggests that recall-phase physiology, early safety-network recruitment, and vmPFC decoupling are better mechanistic endpoints than a generic “extinction improved” slogan.
- **For personalization framing:** the new OUD trial pairs naturally with **Closing the loop between brain and electrical stimulation: A proof-of-concept randomized trial of real-time fMRI-guided tACS optimization**, because the obvious next step is not louder fixed-parameter stimulation but better state- and person-specific targeting.
- **For computational-control framing:** **Quantifying State-Dependent Control Properties of Brain Dynamics from Perturbation Responses** still says the right thing here: control language should grow out of actual perturbation data, not only structural priors or post hoc metaphors.
- **For standing-interest hypnosis:** no fresher paper from this scout beat **Decoding hypnotic consciousness: neural and experiential insights into induced and ideomotor suggestions** as a preserve-worthy mechanism note.
- **For standing-interest CBT-plus-interventional psychiatry:** **Efficacy of combined low-frequency rTMS and cognitive behavioral therapy for unmedicated panic disorder: A randomized controlled trial** is clinically interesting, but from the accessible abstract it still looks more like early symptom acceleration than a sharper mechanism or targeting keep.
- **For computational/modeling scouting:** **BrainDyn: A Sheaf Neural ODE for Generative Brain Dynamics** is clever and full-text accessible, but the intervention-relevant gain over the strongest neural-ODE baseline is modest and demonstrated mainly on synthetic NEST perturbations rather than biological perturbation data.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful June twenty-ninth pattern is that a stimulation paper becomes more believable when it is willing to stop at the level the data actually earned. The OUD trial says a targeted frontoparietal network can move reward-circuit cue reactivity before subjective craving clearly follows. The vmPFC extinction paper says exposure augmentation may live in recall, safety-network recruitment, and vmPFC-to-fear-network reconfiguration rather than in a broad immediate effect. The closed-loop tACS note says personalization should be explicit, and the perturbation-control note says control claims should come from real response dynamics. The common lesson is simple: if the brain moved but the headline did not, keep the neural result and do not counterfeit the rest.
