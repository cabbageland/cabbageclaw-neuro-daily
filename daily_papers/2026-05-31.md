## Short overview

The top preserve-worthy paper today is **Distinct antidepressant therapies act on common brain network mechanisms** in *Nature Mental Health*. I kept it because it tries to answer a real systems question: when psychedelic treatment, transcranial magnetic stimulation, electroconvulsive therapy, and depression itself all appear to perturb large-scale functional connectivity, do they do so through unrelated signatures or through a shared network backbone? The useful answer here is not that every treatment is the same. It is that a common network geometry may underlie several antidepressant effects, which is much more helpful for intervention logic than another siloed treatment-specific story.

The sharpest fresh clinical signal is **Placebo effects on mood and brain activity after accelerated intermittent theta burst stimulation treatment in depression** in *JAMA Psychiatry*. I am not preserving a full note because I could inspect only the abstract in this environment, not the full text. But even at abstract level, the result matters: expectation and sham-associated improvement were large enough to alter both mood outcomes and resting-state neural measures after accelerated intermittent theta burst stimulation. That does not mean the active treatment is fake. It means the field still needs to model expectancy as part of the intervention, not as statistical trash to be swept away.

The best computational and personalization paper today is **Personalized neural treatment sensitivity in depression**, a medRxiv preprint with accessible full text. The core move is to estimate brain-wide sensitivity maps for different treatment targets and modalities from pretreatment resting-state functional connectivity, then use those maps to stratify likely responders. This is still preprint terrain and not decisive evidence, but it is a better use of connectomic personalization than generic response-prediction marketing because it at least tries to represent *which* treatment perturbations a person is sensitive to.

I also explicitly scouted hypnosis, hypnotherapy, and CBT-plus-interventional-psychiatry combinations today. I found some adjacency and trial protocol material, but nothing strong enough to displace the three papers above.

## Most relevant paper

**Distinct antidepressant therapies act on common brain network mechanisms**

This gets top rank because it is both broader and more durable than a one-modality result. If several antidepressant interventions converge on a common functional-network scaffold, then network perturbation may be the thing we actually want to model, measure, and eventually control. That matters for stimulation targeting, psychedelic mechanism, biomarker transfer, and future combination logic. It is also much more intellectually honest than pretending each treatment has a wholly separate magical pathway.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For network neuroscience:** the Nature Mental Health paper pushes a common-network framing across otherwise separate antidepressant modalities.
- **For clinical neuromodulation:** the JAMA Psychiatry placebo paper is a warning that accelerated TMS outcomes and neural readouts can be heavily shaped by expectancy, so sham design and interpretation still matter a lot.
- **For computational personalization:** the medRxiv paper is useful because it tries to infer patient-by-treatment sensitivity structure rather than just train another coarse responder classifier.
- **For intervention logic:** today’s scout reinforces that treatment effects should be framed as interactions among network state, perturbation sensitivity, and expectancy, not as device branding plus average symptom change.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The honest pattern today is that the best papers converge on a more adult view of intervention. The network paper says apparently different antidepressant treatments may share a circuit backbone. The placebo paper says expectation can materially shape both symptoms and neural outcomes in accelerated TMS. The treatment-sensitivity preprint says personalization should be about how a given brain is likely to move under particular perturbations, not only whether it will improve on average. Put together, that is a better research agenda: shared network mechanism, explicit sensitivity structure, and less denial about expectancy.
