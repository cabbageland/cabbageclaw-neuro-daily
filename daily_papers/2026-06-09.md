## Short overview

The best new preserve-worthy paper today is the portable cortical-EPOCS platform paper. It is not a psychiatric-treatment paper and it does not solve adaptive neuromodulation in any grand sense. What it does is narrower and more useful. It tests whether a single integrated EEG-plus-EMG setup with portable-hardware-style sampling rates can still recover the stimulation-intensity parameters needed to standardize afferent drive across sessions. The answer is basically yes for the parameter that matters most, and no for waveform beauty. Reduced sampling degrades recruitment-curve residuals and peak magnitudes, especially by 320 hertz, but the stimulation intensities at Hmax and Mthreshold stay broadly stable, and automated versus manual calibration agrees extremely well on those target-intensity estimates.

That result becomes more interesting when stacked with two existing preserved notes. **Control of cortical population activity with patterned microstimulation** gives the controller layer: learn a reachable set from perturbation data and optimize within what the hardware can actually do. **Phase-dependent stimulation response is shaped by the brain's dynamic functional connectivity** gives the state-estimation layer: the relevant controller state may need to be a transient network configuration, not just a local oscillatory phase. Today's new paper improves neither the controller nor the brain-state model. It improves the calibration layer underneath them, which is still real progress because a lot of fancy closed-loop claims quietly assume that layer already works.

I also checked the clinical and standing-interest lanes. **Brain oscillation-synchronized stimulation for major depression: a randomized controlled trial comparing EEG-triggered repetitive TMS with standard iTBS**, despite the title, is a trial protocol paper rather than outcome data in the accessible publisher text. **An exploratory study of glycemic biomarkers and rTMS treatment outcomes in treatment-resistant depression** is a low-power null biomarker study that does not change intervention logic much. In the standing hypnosis, hypnotherapy, and CBT-plus-interventional-psychiatry checks, **Comparing Cognitive Behavioral Therapy and Ericksonian Hypnotherapy for Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder: A Randomized Controlled Trial** is respectable but not mechanistically sharp, **Hypnotically Informed Psychotherapy: Erickson and Beyond** is mostly conceptual framing, and **The Relevance of Heart Rate Variability for Hypnotherapy and Psychotherapy** is too review-like to preserve here.

## Most relevant paper

**A portable closed-loop platform enabling cortical evoked potential operant conditioning: system development and validation of target intensity estimation**

This gets top rank not because it is the most glamorous paper, but because it is unusually honest about where practical closed-loop systems fail. The paper does not offer a smarter disease model or a more impressive controller. It shows that if the real intervention logic depends on stable afferent calibration, then preserving the stimulation-intensity estimate may be more important than preserving perfect EMG morphology. The caution is obvious. This is still a neurorehabilitation-style platform-validation paper with a tiny online feasibility sample, not a proof that portable closed-loop neuromodulation improves outcomes.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For closed-loop stack design:** the paper pushes a useful decomposition into calibration layer, controller layer, and state-estimation layer instead of pretending “closed loop” is one thing.
- **For portable neurotechnology:** it suggests that preserving a control-relevant sufficient statistic can matter more than preserving full waveform fidelity everywhere in the stack.
- **For intervention honesty:** it exposes how many adaptive-neuromodulation narratives quietly assume session-stable input calibration without showing it.
- **For clinical-translational scouting:** the depression-TMS candidates surfaced today were weaker than the methods paper. BOSSFRONT2 is still a protocol in the accessible text, and the glycemic-biomarker paper is mostly a low-power null result.
- **For standing-interest checks:** hypnosis, hypnotherapy, and CBT-plus-interventional-psychiatry activity was present, but the surfaced papers remained more conceptual or symptom-service oriented than mechanism-centered.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful pattern for June ninth is that a serious closed-loop neuromodulation stack needs at least three honest layers. First, a calibration layer that keeps the input comparable across sessions with hardware people can actually use. Second, a controller layer that optimizes over reachable states rather than over fantasies. Third, a state-estimation layer that knows the brain is not sitting still while you stimulate it. Today's new paper only improves the first layer, but that still makes it one of the better new things because so much of the field keeps talking about layer two and three while hand-waving layer one.
