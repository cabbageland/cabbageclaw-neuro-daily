## Short overview

The main fresh preserve today is the July 17, 2026 **Translational Psychiatry** paper **Gamma and beta power and the 1/f slope vary across a spectrum of depression severity**. It gets the top rank because it asks a better biomarker question than most depression EEG papers ask. Instead of doing one more blunt case-versus-control comparison, it tests whether high-frequency and aperiodic EEG features change across mild, moderate, and severe symptom burden, and then stress-tests the result in a second independent dataset.

That does not make it a triumph. The paper is useful precisely because the answer comes back mixed. In the larger heterogeneous TDBRAIN cohort, worse symptoms track higher gamma power and higher 1/f offset, especially when severe cases are compared against moderate cases. In the cleaner Cavanagh cohort, the more stable replicated effect shifts toward beta power rather than gamma, and the 1/f result weakens. The right read is not that the field now has a settled EEG severity biomarker. The right read is that severity-continuum analysis is a much better filter for candidate biomarkers than diagnostic binning, and that cohort shifts quickly expose how provisional many "depression EEG signature" claims really are.

The fresh surrounding field was thinner. **Acute neuromodulatory effects of 1 Hz rTMS on the central autonomic network in depression: A concurrent TMS-fMRI study** is a respectable acute mechanism paper, but from the accessible abstract it still looks like a transient CAN-coupling result rather than a preserve-level shift in intervention logic. **Functional segregation in Parkinson's disease** looks like a strong fresh systems paper adjacent to today's search, but in this environment the direct Science Advances full-text routes were blocked and the accessible abstract makes it more of a Parkinson's circuit-organization note than a near-term psychiatry or neuromodulation keep. ## Most relevant paper

**Gamma and beta power and the 1/f slope vary across a spectrum of depression severity**

This gets the top rank because it is the freshest paper today that both had accessible full text and made a reusable methodological move. The move is simple but important: stop asking whether depressed participants differ from controls in some average spectral feature, and ask whether candidate signals scale across real symptom burden and survive a cohort shift.

The useful part is that the answer is not cosmetically neat. In the larger adult archive, gamma power and 1/f offset carry the stronger severity signal. In the cleaner young-adult cohort, beta carries more of the replicated effect and the aperiodic story weakens. That mismatch is not a reason to throw the paper away. It is the reason to keep it. It shows how quickly a "signature" can change shape when the sample, electrode density, medication exposure, and age profile change.

The paper still has real limits. It is cross-sectional, symptom based, and treatment agnostic. The primary cohort is diagnostically messy and includes recent medication exposure. The secondary cohort is cleaner but narrower. So this is not a treatment-ready biomarker. But it is a useful calibration object for cabbageland because it says severity-aware, cohort-aware biomarker testing is the minimum standard, not an optional extra.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful July seventeenth lesson is that depression EEG probably will not become clinically interesting through one neat case-versus-control signature. It will become interesting, if it does at all, by showing that a small set of periodic and aperiodic features tracks severity, heterogeneity, or treatment sensitivity across messy real cohorts. Today's preserve matters because it pushes the field toward that harder question and exposes how unstable the answers still are.
