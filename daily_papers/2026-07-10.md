## Short overview

The main fresh preserve today is the open-access **Translational Psychiatry** paper **Reward pursuit during a translational reward task correlates with anhedonia reductions following rTMS in patients with major depressive disorder**, published on **July 10, 2026**. The useful move is not "we found a biomarker for rTMS." The paper does not earn that line. The useful move is narrower and better. It puts a rodent-to-human foraging task, Web-Surf, inside a naturalistic clinical rTMS workflow and asks whether reward pursuit is specifically tied to anhedonia improvement rather than to undifferentiated depression change. In 32 patients with treatment-resistant major depressive disorder, baseline capture of preferred rewards tracks pleasurable task experience, and the patients whose anhedonia improves also become better at harvesting preferred rewards over the treatment course.

That matters because interventional psychiatry keeps saying it wants symptom-specific prediction while mostly feeding on broad rating scales and retrospective stories. This paper is still small, observational, non-randomized, clinically heterogeneous, and stuck with a weak anhedonia endpoint, essentially a single PHQ-9 item. But it is still a cleaner archive object than another generic "reward circuitry" paper because it uses an actual translational task and because it is willing to stop at a narrower claim.

The other fresh lanes mostly tightened boundaries. A **JAMA Network Open** randomized trial, **Prefrontal Transcranial Pulse Stimulation for Major Depressive Disorder: A Randomized Clinical Trial**, reports a statistically significant antidepressant effect against sham with a modest effect size and left-DLPFC-seeded connectivity changes. That is clinically useful, but not yet enough to outrank today's preserve as an archive note. The fresh CBT-plus-interventional lane again came in thin: the accessible new rTMS-plus-CBT paper is a gambling-disorder pilot, not a mechanism-rich psychiatry result, and the best fresh hypnosis or hypnotherapy material remained broad clinical-effectiveness territory rather than state-change or biomarker leverage. A recent **Molecular Psychiatry** paper on connectome-based growth models in subthreshold depression remains useful as a heterogeneity reminder, but it still sits much closer to stratification than to intervention logic you can steal tomorrow.

## Most relevant paper

**Reward pursuit during a translational reward task correlates with anhedonia reductions following rTMS in patients with major depressive disorder**

This gets top rank because today's other fresh papers were either broader but less sharp or sharper but less intervention-relevant. The new JAMA transcranial pulse stimulation trial is a real result, but its core message is still "novel hardware produced a modest sham-beating effect from a broad left-DLPFC target." That is useful, but familiar. Today's reward-pursuit paper asks a more interesting question: if rTMS helps some depressed patients specifically on anhedonia, can we detect a behaviorally legible, cross-species phenotype that tracks that improvement?

The best part of the paper is that it does not pretend total depression improvement and anhedonia improvement are the same object. Patients with major depressive disorder completed the Web-Surf task weekly during treatment. The task comes from the rodent Restaurant Row framework, so reward pursuit is operationalized through serial accept-or-reject decisions under a time budget rather than through abstract self-report alone. Baseline capture of preferred rewards tracks in-task pleasurable experience, and the longitudinal part of the study shows that patients whose anhedonia improves increasingly concentrate their earnings in their preferred reward categories over time.

The paper also earns points for restraint. The authors do not have a randomized biomarker trial. They have a naturalistic observational clinic sample with mixed coil configurations, no non-treatment comparator, and only the PHQ-9 to work with clinically. The main baseline predictor story is also not perfectly clean after multiple-comparison correction. That is exactly why this is useful. It is a real translational phenotype paper that says less than the average biomarker paper, which in this lane usually means it says something more trustworthy.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For interventional psychiatry framing:** today's paper argues that symptom-specific response heterogeneity should be studied with repeated behavior, not only with broad symptom totals or one-shot baseline questionnaires.
- **For rTMS biomarker rhetoric:** the useful signal is narrower than "predicts rTMS response." What the paper really supports is a candidate reward-pursuit phenotype for tracking or stratifying anhedonia-relevant change.
- **For novel-stimulation-device hype:** the fresh JAMA transcranial pulse stimulation trial is clinically worth reading, but its modest effect size and broad left-DLPFC framing do not automatically make it the more valuable archive object.
- **For CBT-plus-interventional framing:** the fresh rTMS-plus-CBT paper again fails to establish testable mechanistic complementarity in a psychiatry setting that matters here.
- **For hypnosis and hypnotherapy:** the fresh material I checked stayed clinically broad and outcome-focused rather than mechanism-rich, so the older hypnosis archive anchor still wins on state-change leverage.
- **For network-heterogeneity work:** the fresh connectome-growth paper still supports the claim that psychiatric subtypes matter, but it remains closer to stratification support than to immediately steal-worthy intervention logic.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful July tenth lesson is that reward-response papers become worth keeping when they stop pretending symptom totals are enough. Today's preserve matters because it gives interventional psychiatry a concrete translational task for asking who actually gets less anhedonic during rTMS and how that change shows up behaviorally over time. It is still far from a validated biomarker, and the paper is honest enough about that. The surrounding scout mostly sharpened boundaries: the new JAMA transcranial pulse stimulation trial looks real but still broad, the connectome-growth paper strengthens heterogeneity framing without yet changing intervention logic, and the standing CBT and hypnosis lanes again produced calibration more than mechanism.
