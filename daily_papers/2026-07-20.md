## Short overview

The main fresh preserve today is the July 5, 2026 arXiv paper **Neuromorphic Silicon Neuron Controller for Adaptive Deep Brain Stimulation in Parkinson's Disease**. It gets the top rank not because it wins every controller benchmark. It does not. In the paper's own comparison table, the simple on-off controller still has the best suppression-efficiency number. What makes this one worth keeping is a different question: if a closed-loop DBS controller is supposed to matter for an implant, does the controller ever become a physically realizable low-power object, or does it stay a software sketch forever? This paper actually builds the controller primitive as a refractory-enabled silicon neuron in CMOS, derives a matched computational surrogate, and validates that surrogate inside a Parkinsonian closed loop driven by beta-band STN local field potentials and beta average rectified value. In that loop, the controller uses 25 percent of open-loop DBS power and reaches 5.85 percent suppression efficiency per microwatt. That does not settle adaptive DBS. It does make the hardware claim less fake.

That lands cleanly against the current archive. **Neuromorphic Energy-Aware Learning for Adaptive Deep Brain Stimulation** remains the best recent note on making actuator energy part of the learning objective rather than a benchmark appendix. **Low-latency neuromorphic closed-loop control of hippocampal ripples in vivo** remains the better note on whether the loop survives real timing constraints once it leaves offline evaluation. **A first realization of reinforcement learning-based closed-loop EEG-TMS** still matters because it shows online phase learning in humans instead of hard-coded stimulation timing. ## Most relevant paper

**Neuromorphic Silicon Neuron Controller for Adaptive Deep Brain Stimulation in Parkinson's Disease**

This gets the top rank because it attacks a neglected failure mode in adaptive DBS papers. Many papers want implant relevance, but the controller itself never becomes a plausible implant controller. It remains a software object with an eventual-hardware promise stapled on later. This paper does something more useful. It instantiates the controller as a refractory-enabled silicon neuron, derives a matched computational surrogate, and then embeds that surrogate inside a Parkinsonian closed loop.

The control logic is conservative but legible. Beta-band filtered subthalamic local field potentials are converted into beta average rectified value, and that biomarker drives the controller. The controller's spike-rate estimate is then mapped into bounded DBS amplitude commands. The point is not fancy learning. The point is that membrane, leak, threshold, reset, and refractory dynamics all remain real circuit-level objects, so the adaptive behavior stays tied to a plausible low-power implementation instead of floating above it.

The paper's numbers should be read without flattery. Relative to continuous open-loop DBS, the controller uses 25 percent of the power and reaches 5.85 percent suppression efficiency per microwatt. That is better than open-loop DBS and slightly better than the dual-threshold controller on the paper's table, but it does not beat the simple on-off controller, which reaches 8.10. That matters. The preserve-worthy part is therefore not a leaderboard win. It is the insistence that implant relevance requires controller realizability and interpretable hardware-software co-design, not just a control curve inside simulation.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For adaptive DBS papers:** a controller is more believable when it becomes a physically realizable controller primitive, not just a software policy waiting for a future chip team.
- **For low-power neuromodulation claims:** beating open-loop power is not enough; papers should also show whether they beat simple cheap heuristics like on-off or dual-threshold control.
- **For current methods scouting:** the fresh dynamic-manifolds-on-SpiNNaker paper is interesting adjacent inspiration, but the manually planned robotic-maze framing keeps it below preserve level for this repo.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful July twentieth lesson is that adaptive-neuromodulation papers should stop treating hardware reality as somebody else's future problem. If the controller never becomes a plausible physical controller, the implant story is still mostly vibes. Today's preserve matters because it makes the controller itself a real object, even while leaving the clinical and translational hard part very much unfinished.
