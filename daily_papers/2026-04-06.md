## Short overview

The most relevant paper today is **Predicting monopolar local field potential power from bipolar recordings in deep brain stimulation**. The reason is simple: a lot of so-called precision neuromodulation quietly rests on a measurement mismatch. Chronic devices prefer bipolar recordings because they are cleaner, but the field still wants to reason about contact-specific physiology as if it had monopolar access for free. This paper reframes that mismatch as a stable inverse problem. Using intraoperative recordings from sixty-four Parkinson patients with subthalamic or pallidal implants, the authors use selected bipolar montages and robust linear regression to estimate monopolar band power. The reported performance is strong enough to take seriously, with adjusted R-squared values near zero point nine. That does not prove better outcomes, but it directly sharpens the representational layer underneath adaptive programming.

The second keep-worthy paper is **Untangling neurophysiology of Tourette syndrome using dual-target bi-pallidal deep brain stimulation**. This is a three-patient pilot, so nobody should pretend it settles target selection. What makes it worth preserving is the framing. The authors implant both anterior and posterior GPi regions, record postoperative local field potentials, and ask whether those subregions may map differently onto tic reduction and psychiatric symptom changes. The signal is small but suggestive: the two patients with the clearest tic reduction showed prominent posterior-GPi high-beta activity, while acute cross-modulatory effects between anterior and posterior GPi were not consistent. The value is less the headline efficacy and more the move toward symptom-component and circuit-subregion thinking.

The third paper worth attention today is **Passivity-Based Control of Electrographic Seizures in a Neural Mass Model of Epilepsy**. It is not a new note today, but it belongs in the digest because it complements the other two papers at the control-logic level. The paper argues that seizure dynamics in the standard Epileptor setting are neither passive nor naturally passivatable, yet sufficiently strong passive feedback can still stabilize them and output redesign can make passivity arguments viable. That matters because a lot of closed-loop seizure-control language jumps directly from simulation to controller righteousness. This paper makes the assumptions legible.

A fourth paper that stayed adjacent but below preserve threshold is **The mechanism of external cueing interventions in improving freezing of gait in Parkinson’s disease: an fNIRS study**. It is not neuromodulation in the stimulation-device sense, but it is still relevant to state-transition framing. The useful part is that single auditory or visual cues improved gait and increased cortical activation in prefrontal and somatosensory regions, while combined cueing did not simply stack benefits. I kept it below note threshold because the mechanistic interpretation is still thin and the intervention is cueing, not stimulation.

## Most relevant paper

**Predicting monopolar local field potential power from bipolar recordings in deep brain stimulation**

This is the top paper because it fixes a real upstream problem rather than decorating a downstream one. In adaptive DBS, people often talk as if sensed physiology maps cleanly onto individual contacts and local generators, but implanted hardware usually gives you bipolar mixtures. The paper uses sixty-four Parkinson cases and shows that a stable subset of bipolar montages can predict monopolar band power with strong regression performance across contacts and across subthalamic and pallidal cohorts. That is not yet evidence that programming gets better, but it is exactly the kind of practical methods work that can make later targeting and biomarker claims less fake.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For adaptive DBS:** the monopolar-from-bipolar paper matters because it turns sensing limitations into an estimation problem. That could become a baseline expectation for future biomarker papers that speak too loosely about contact specificity.
- **For pallidal psychiatric-movement DBS:** the Tourette pilot does not settle target choice, but it supports a more decomposed framing where anterior and posterior GPi may support different symptom-control functions.
- **For seizure-control theory:** the passivity paper remains a useful baseline-correction against numerically flashy but structurally vague closed-loop claims.
- **For intervention design generally:** the freezing-of-gait cueing paper is a mild warning that combining modalities does not guarantee additive benefit and may even obscure the useful mechanism.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The pattern today is that intervention claims get stronger when the representation and control layers are made explicit. The monopolar-reconstruction paper is the best of the set because it improves the measurement logic that many adaptive DBS ideas quietly rely on. The Tourette dual-target paper is underpowered but directionally good because it treats one named target as an internal circuit problem rather than a single coordinate. The passivity-control epilepsy paper remains important because it insists that control language should cash out in system properties, not just in simulation success. Overall, this was a good day for infrastructure and logic, not for grand efficacy claims.
