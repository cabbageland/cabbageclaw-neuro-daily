## Short overview

The new preserve-worthy paper is the positive-bias mechanism study in left dorsolateral prefrontal intermittent theta-burst stimulation for major depressive disorder. It was inspected through accessible full text, and the value is specific. This is not a sham-controlled proof that TMS works by making patients happier in some vague way. What the paper actually shows is narrower and more useful: after roughly eight sessions, later responders showed an increased tendency to classify ambiguous faces as positive, and larger eventual symptom improvement tracked a more positive task-evoked bias in rostral anterior cingulate and default-mode-network activity and connectivity. That makes the paper a serious mechanism candidate, but not a settled one, because the design is open label and symptoms had already started to improve by the time the Week 2 mechanism readout was taken.

That result matters most when read against two existing preserved baselines. The first is the routine-practice anti-subgenual normative-connectivity paper, which is useful precisely because the main result is negative. In messy real clinics, static connectivity folklore explains little by itself. The second is the white-matter-pathways paper, which argues that effective depression TMS sites may work because they have better structural routes into deeper circuitry rather than because they satisfy one scalar resting-state heuristic. Put together, the trio suggests a more serious mechanism stack for depression TMS: dynamic task-evoked process change during treatment, clinically realistic pressure-testing of favorite targeting stories, and a plausible propagation account for how cortical entry points reach depression circuitry.

## Most relevant paper

**Positive bias in brain and behaviour as a mechanism of transcranial magnetic stimulation depression treatment**

This gets top rank because it asks the right timing question. If depression TMS has an actual mechanism story, the field should be able to observe some plausible process shift during treatment before declaring victory from endpoint symptom change. This paper does that more honestly than most of the literature by looking at early behavioral and task-fMRI changes rather than only at resting-state maps or post-treatment snapshots. The central caution is equally important: without sham and with symptoms already improving by Week 2, this is still a mechanism candidate and not permission to talk as if the story is settled.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful pattern for June sixth is that depression TMS mechanism becomes less embarrassing when it is forced to show its work. A credible story probably needs more than one pretty target map: it needs a dynamic readout of what changes during treatment, a negative translational baseline that tests favorite heuristics against real clinical heterogeneity, and a plausible structural route story for how stimulation propagates. The new positive-bias paper supplies the first piece in a nontrivial way, the anti-subgenual routine-practice paper keeps static heuristics on probation, and the white-matter-pathways paper points toward a better propagation account. That is still not a finished precision system, but it is closer to honest intervention logic than most of the field.
