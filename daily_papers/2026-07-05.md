## Short overview

The fresh preserve today is the new Journal of Neural Engineering paper from Kavineshvar Ranak and William S. Anderson. The useful thing is not merely that it can fit a Parkinsonian beta regime. Lots of papers can fit a regime if they are allowed enough knobs, enough implicit priors, and no obligation to say which knobs were actually identified by the data. What makes this one archive-worthy is that it turns detailed spiking-network fitting into a tractable engineering workflow on a single L4 GPU, then uses that speed to probe search bounds, loss weights, optimizer seeds, neuronal heterogeneity, connectivity density, and input-correlation timescale. That lets the paper say something unusually honest: the STN-to-GPe increase looks data-constrained, while the GPe-to-STN decrease is largely prior-constrained by the asymmetric search bounds. That is a much more useful result than another decorative "mechanistic" fit.

## Most relevant paper

**A computational framework for fitting biophysical basal-ganglia network models, applied to Parkinsonian beta oscillations**

This gets top rank because it treats identifiability as part of the scientific result instead of as an embarrassing afterthought. The paper combines a biophysically detailed STN-GPe-GPi spiking network, JAX GPU acceleration, and CMA-ES optimization to fit firing-rate, variability, and beta-band targets from the Parkinsonism literature. But the real value is not the representative fit itself. The real value is that the workflow is fast enough to ask which recovered synaptic story survives sensitivity probes. That is where the paper earns its keep: STN-to-GPe strengthening persists under symmetric search bounds, while the GPe-to-STN weakening flips sign once the asymmetric prior is removed.

That matters for cabbageland because detailed circuit models become more useful when they stop pretending to be omniscient. This paper does not prove the in vivo origin of pathological beta. It explicitly does not. The objective is engineered, the optimized beta term acts on GPe beta rather than STN beta, the model omits cortex, striatum, and thalamus, and its DBS sanity check is just an informational-lesion abstraction with nonphysiological firing rates. But that honesty is precisely why it is useful. It makes detailed spiking models more legible as testbeds for biomarker reasoning, synthetic signal generation, and adaptive-control prototyping without pretending they have already solved mechanism.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful July fifth lesson is that detailed circuit fitting gets much more valuable when the paper is honest about identifiability. This JNE paper is worth preserving not because it solves Parkinsonian beta, but because it makes biophysical spiking-network fitting fast enough to expose which synaptic claims are coming from the data and which are still being smuggled in by the prior.
