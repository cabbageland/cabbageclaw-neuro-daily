## Short overview

The new preserve-worthy paper is the slow-wave-disruption study in major depressive disorder. I did not get a clean full-PDF read, but I did get the PubMed abstract plus indexed publisher-side methods, results, and limitations excerpts, which is enough to see the paper's real use. This is not a treatment trial and it does not prove that sleep disruption is itself a good antidepressant intervention. What it does show is more precise: when slow-wave sleep is experimentally disrupted overnight, people with major depressive disorder wake up with increased motor-cortex excitability, while healthy controls show the opposite broad pattern. That is exactly the kind of state dependence the stimulation field claims to care about, but usually only after the pulse is already on its way.

The best way to read the paper is alongside two existing preserved notes. The first is **Pre-stimulus brain states predict and control variability in stimulation responses**, which argues that ongoing brain state can explain and partly tame stimulation-response variability. The second is **Auditory mismatch-negativity predicts response to dorsomedial prefrontal intermittent theta-burst stimulation in major depressive disorder**, which suggests baseline physiology may tag plasticity capacity better than static targeting rhetoric. Put together, the stack becomes more serious: response is not only about where you stimulate, but also about what state the system is already in, and some of that state may have been set hours earlier during sleep.

I also completed the standing-interest checks for hypnosis, hypnotherapy, and CBT plus interventional psychiatry. **Comparing Cognitive Behavioral Therapy and Ericksonian Hypnotherapy for Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder: A Randomized Controlled Trial** is a respectable symptom-tradeoff psychotherapy paper, but it does not meaningfully sharpen mechanism, biomarkers, or controllable state logic. **Promoting relaxation through essential oil-enhanced digital hypnotherapy: A randomized controlled trial** is even further from the target; it is mostly a relaxation-delivery paper. In the CBT-plus-interventional-psychiatry lane, **Psilocybin-assisted cognitive behavioral therapy for major depressive disorder: A pilot trial** is interesting as hybrid-care scaffolding, but at sixteen participants in a single-arm pilot it still does not answer the mechanistic complementarity or sequencing question strongly enough to outrank today's state-plasticity paper.

## Most relevant paper

**Selective disruption of sleep slow-waves increases motor cortical excitability in major depressive disorder**

This gets top rank because it sharpens a question the field usually leaves mushy. If response to stimulation depends on current plasticity or excitability state, then the relevant state may not begin at session start. It may begin the night before. The paper does not deliver a clinic-ready rule, but it does provide a clean mechanistic push: the same overnight slow-wave disruption moves next-morning excitability in opposite directions in depressed and healthy participants. The caution is equally important. This is motor-cortex physiology, not a therapeutic trial, and the article body was only partially inspectable in this run.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For state-aware stimulation:** the paper extends the relevant state window backward in time. If sleep architecture changes the excitability regime, then pre-session state estimation should not start only once the patient sits in the chair.
- **For depression mechanism:** the opposite-direction response between major depressive disorder and healthy controls supports the idea that homeostatic plasticity regulation is altered in depression rather than merely noisier.
- **For biomarker framing:** it pairs naturally with mismatch-negativity and related physiological predictors by suggesting that cheap biomarkers may partly read out sleep-shaped plasticity capacity.
- **For intervention design:** it pushes toward questions about scheduling, gating, or dosing stimulation around sleep-derived state rather than treating yesterday's sleep as irrelevant background.
- **For standing-interest checks:** hypnotherapy and CBT-plus-interventional-psychiatry did surface activity, but the visible 2026 papers remain more feasibility or symptom-service objects than stronger mechanism-centered intervention logic.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful pattern for June eighth is that precision neuromodulation probably needs a longer memory than the field usually admits. Today's new paper suggests that next-morning cortical excitability depends in part on what happened during slow-wave sleep, and that depression changes the sign of that relationship. The older state-variability and mismatch-negativity notes then tell you why that matters: response is partly state-dependent, and simple physiological readouts may be tagging plasticity capacity rather than target location alone. That still is not a finished control stack, but it is a more honest one.
