## Short overview

The most relevant paper today is **Passive neuromodulation: an energy-driven mechanism for closed-loop suppression of epileptic seizures**. This is not a clinical paper and it does not pretend to be. Its claim is narrower and more interesting: using two seizure models, the authors argue that an analog feedback element can passively drain energy from an epileptic circuit and suppress seizure initiation or spread without the usual active stimulation logic. They report efficacy in both a detailed dentate-gyrus network model and the Epileptor neural mass model, and they also test a responsive version that turns on for brief windows after seizure detection. The reason to keep this is not that it is ready for patients. The reason is that it sharpens a neglected control idea: some pathological dynamics may be better handled by damping than by repeated forcing. That is a better mechanistic frame than a lot of decorative closed-loop rhetoric.

The second paper worth preserving is **Remote Magnetomechanical Neuromodulation Uncovers Therapeutic Mechanisms for Alleviating Parkinsonian Symptoms in Freely Moving Mice**. This one has obvious hardware-hype risk, but it is more substantial than the phrase minimally-invasive DBS without chronically implanted hardware would suggest. The core design uses magnetite nanodiscs that generate local torque under low-frequency alternating magnetic fields, aiming to recruit endogenous mechanosensory pathways rather than electrodes or optogenetics. The paper reports in vitro and ex vivo calcium-imaging evidence, remote control of motor behavior in wild-type mice, and improved motor function in a severe hemiparkinsonian mouse model when targeting the subthalamic nucleus. That is interesting translational engineering, but it is still preclinical, and the jump from mechanosensory torque in mice to clinically meaningful human deep-brain control remains enormous.

The third preserve-worthy paper is **A human subcortical connectome at 400 μm resolution**. This is not a stimulation study, but it may matter more than many nominal stimulation papers because it targets a real bottleneck: the subcortical pathways behind deep brain stimulation and lesion effects are hard to resolve with ordinary diffusion MRI, so the field leans on synthetic or overly smoothed atlases. This paper uses ultra-high-resolution ex vivo diffusion MRI on the Connectome 2.0 scanner to reconstruct basal-ganglia-thalamocortical pathways and then align the resulting atlas to reported deep brain stimulation hotspots and side-effect zones. It should not be mistaken for patient-ready individualized targeting, but it is exactly the kind of anatomical infrastructure that can make future targeting claims less hand-wavy.

A fourth paper that stayed in scope but below the preservation threshold is **Precision phase targeting of event-related oscillations using real-time closed-loop TMS-EEG**. It is useful methods work. The strongest part is that it avoids prediction-heavy phase targeting and instead directly detects phase in real time, improving trigger probability and phase precision for event-related theta in navigation tasks. But the accessible evidence here is validation without active TMS delivery, so for now it is better treated as a promising platform paper than as a direct intervention result.

## Most relevant paper

**Passive neuromodulation: an energy-driven mechanism for closed-loop suppression of epileptic seizures**

This is the top paper because it contributes a real intervention logic rather than a shinier actuator. The authors argue that if seizure dynamics are sustained by pathological energy in the circuit, then a passive analog feedback element may suppress them by draining that energy rather than by imposing another exogenous drive pattern. They show the idea working in both a biophysical dentate-gyrus network and the Epileptor neural mass model, and they report that a responsive version can be triggered in fifty-millisecond windows after seizure detection. This is still simulation work, so nobody should confuse it with validated epilepsy therapy. But it is exactly the kind of mechanistic control framing that could improve how we think about closed-loop neuromodulation design.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For closed-loop epilepsy:** this paper pushes the field to consider damping-based control architectures, not only stimulation-as-forcing.
- **For neuromodulation hardware judgment:** the magnetomechanical paper is interesting because it links a new actuator to mechanosensory pathways and animal behavior, but it should be benchmarked against the translational gap, not against its own futuristic packaging.
- **For targeting logic:** the subcortical connectome paper matters because neuromodulation claims about target pathways remain weak when the underlying anatomy is synthetic, coarse, or population-averaged beyond usefulness.
- **For methods scouting:** the real-time phase-targeting TMS-EEG paper looks promising, but without active stimulation results it remains more like enabling infrastructure than outcome evidence.

## One-paragraph takeaway

Today’s honest pattern is that the strongest ideas were not the loudest hardware claims. The best paper is the seizure-control preprint because it makes the intervention logic more explicit: some pathological brain dynamics may be better damped than driven. The magnetomechanical Parkinson paper is worth keeping because it is a real preclinical modality paper, not pure branding, but it still lives far from human therapeutic proof. The subcortical connectome paper is valuable because targeting cannot improve if the anatomy stays blurry. And the phase-targeting TMS paper is a good reminder that better timing infrastructure is useful, but not every precision method deserves immediate preserve status as an intervention result.
