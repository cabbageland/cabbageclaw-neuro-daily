## Short overview

The strongest new preserve today is the home SCC-DBS biomarker paper. It is a medRxiv preprint, so it should not be oversold. But it does something unusually useful for interventional psychiatry: it tries to separate DBS-responsive core depression recovery from the generic sludge of anxiety, sleep disruption, and everyday life stress that contaminates rating scales. The authors move chronic subcallosal cingulate recordings into a twice-daily home workflow, apply a previously trained biomarker without retraining it on the new cohort, and show that it still tracks the depressed-to-recovery transition reasonably well. The most useful lesson is not the exact accuracy number. It is the clinical framing. If psychiatric DBS is going to mature, it needs recovery-state inference, not just device programming intuition.

The second new preserve is the dynamic-functional-connectivity stimulation paper in Network Neuroscience. This is not a clinical paper and it does not solve stimulation variability. But it does sharpen the right problem. Local phase is not enough. The same pulse can have different effects depending on the current functional-connectivity state, and some pulses do not merely perturb the present state but help trigger a broader state switch. Even a coarse FC-state label improves prediction over phase-only features. That is exactly the kind of result that should make simplistic phase-locked-control rhetoric feel too thin.

The best surrounding context remains existing preserves. **A week in the life of the human brain reveals stable states punctuated by chaotic-like transitions** is still a sharper empirical argument for brain-state control than most static connectome talk, and it remains the best recent note here for transition timing. **Non-vectorial integration of intersectional short-pulse stimulation enables enhanced deep brain modulation and effective seizure control** stays useful because it shows what a methods paper looks like when it actually links a biophysical mechanism to better intervention performance instead of just sketching a field map. Together, the four notes say the same unpleasantly helpful thing: reliable neuromodulation depends on state inference, state timing, and mechanism-sensitive delivery, not just target coordinates and optimism.

## Most relevant paper

**Common Electrophysiology Biomarkers Collected at Home Robustly Track Depression Recovery With Deep Brain Stimulation**

This gets top rank because it addresses the most neglected practical layer in psychiatric neuromodulation: not whether a target makes for a nice paper figure, but whether clinicians can tell if the patient is actually moving into a DBS-responsive recovery state. The paper is still a small preprint, and the device exclusions matter. But the core result is harder to dismiss than most psychiatric-biomarker theater. A previously trained SCC biomarker generalized across a new cohort and device stack, stayed comparatively stable during anxiety-heavy symptom noise, and reacted sensibly to early stimulation interruption. That is exactly the kind of middle-layer infrastructure the field needs if it wants to grow past artisanal programming.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For interventional psychiatry:** stop treating weekly rating-scale movement as the whole truth. A treatment-specific recovery-state marker may be more useful than a broad symptom inventory for programming decisions.
- **For adaptive neuromodulation:** local phase is probably an incomplete control variable. FC-state estimation deserves to be a first-class part of response modeling.
- **For network neuroscience:** week-scale state trajectories and transition structure remain better control-relevant context than static connectivity snapshots.
- **For stimulation methods:** mechanism-sensitive delivery papers still matter because if the intervention physics are mushy, the fanciest biomarker stack will not save the claim.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful June eighteenth pattern is that neuromodulation gets more credible when it becomes explicit about hidden state. The home SCC-DBS biomarker paper matters because it tries to infer a slow clinical recovery state that is more specific than ordinary symptom noise. The dynamic-FC stimulation paper matters because it says even short-timescale response prediction should be state-aware at the whole-brain level, not just the local target level. The existing week-scale brain-state and ISP notes remain the right ballast because they ground those ideas in empirical transition structure and mechanism-sensitive delivery. Taken together, the message is simple: stop pretending the pulse itself is the intelligence. The intelligence has to live in the state model.
