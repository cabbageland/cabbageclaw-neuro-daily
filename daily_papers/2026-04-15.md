## Short overview

The most relevant paper today is **Predictive value of subgenual cingulate normative connectivity to TMS treatment site for antidepressant response in routine clinical practice: a prospective, multisite cohort study**. This is a prospective multisite observational test of one of the most repeated ideas in depression TMS: that left-DLPFC sites with stronger negative functional connectivity to subgenual cingulate should produce better antidepressant outcomes. Sixty-six patients with major depressive disorder received three to eight weeks of routine clinical left-DLPFC rTMS, with actual stimulation sites recorded by MRI neuronavigation. The cleanest result is negative. Normative subgenual-connectivity of the delivered site did **not** predict symptom improvement on its own, and most explained variance came instead from baseline anxiety and psychiatric comorbidity. That does not mean network targeting is useless. It does mean the simple anti-subgenual heuristic looks too thin for real-world heterogeneous clinics.

The second paper is **Causal connectivity maps derived from single-pulse interleaved TMS/fMRI**. Over more than eighty participants, the authors used personalized cortical targets intended to engage either subgenual anterior cingulate cortex or basolateral amygdala, then measured TMS-evoked BOLD responses while controlling for motion, pain, and somatosensory confounds. The useful result is not that they discovered a magic psychiatric target. It is that image-guided TMS can produce reproducible distributed downstream responses and that the resulting maps can serve as a more causal complement to resting-connectivity target stories.

The third paper is **Controlling the human connectome with spatially diffuse input signals**. This is not a stimulation trial, but it is exactly the kind of network-control paper that improves intervention logic. The authors modify optimal-control models so inputs spread spatially with exponential distance decay instead of acting only on single nodes. Under that more realistic assumption, state transitions often require much less energy and many fewer effective input signals, and the derived input-density maps align with independent functional, metabolic, genetic, and neurochemical annotations. The paper matters because real brain stimulation is spatially blurry, and control theory that ignores that blur may be optimizing the wrong object.

A fourth paper stayed below note threshold but remains useful citation material: **Clinical utility and prospective of TMS-EEG: Updated review from an international expert group**. It is broad and competent, but it is still a review rather than a preserve-worthy mechanistic advance.

## Most relevant paper

**Predictive value of subgenual cingulate normative connectivity to TMS treatment site for antidepressant response in routine clinical practice: a prospective, multisite cohort study**

This is the top paper because negative translational results are unusually valuable when they hit a dominant targeting story. The field has spent years acting as if anti-subgenual connectivity was already an operationally robust rule for depression TMS. This study does not kill that idea, but it does show that once you leave cleaner single-site samples and step into a more clinically messy population, normative connectivity alone explains very little. That is exactly the kind of correction worth preserving.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For depression-TMS framing:** today’s lead paper weakens the habit of talking as if anti-subgenual targeting is already a reliable standalone personalization rule in routine practice.
- **For causal-targeting logic:** the interleaved TMS/fMRI paper is useful because it offers stimulation-evoked circuit maps that can pressure-test connectivity-based target claims.
- **For network-control modeling:** the spatially diffuse control paper matters because single-node input assumptions are often a bad proxy for how real stimulation spreads through cortex and white matter.
- **For future baselines:** papers that claim precision targeting should probably be compared against heterogeneous clinical covariates and not just against prettier connectivity maps.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The best papers today all punish overconfident targeting narratives. The depression-TMS cohort study says a famous normative-connectivity heuristic is much less robust in real clinics than people often imply. The interleaved TMS/fMRI paper says if you want to talk about circuits, you should measure stimulation-evoked circuit consequences rather than stopping at resting correlations. And the spatially diffuse control paper says control theory should stop pretending that stimulation enters the brain as perfectly local node-wise impulses. Overall lesson: better intervention logic will require more causal measurement, more realistic control assumptions, and more respect for clinical heterogeneity.
