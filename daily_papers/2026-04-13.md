## Short overview

The most relevant paper today is **mPFC axons drive cognitive control enhancement during striatal stimulation**. This is the one to keep because it sharpens intervention logic instead of just reporting that stimulation helped behavior. In a rodent set-shift task, high-frequency optogenetic stimulation of medial-prefrontal axons in the striatum reproduced the faster response times previously seen with electrical stimulation, while direct stimulation of local striatal neurons impaired performance with sustained use. The paper then links within-session decline in benefit to reduced evoked responses and weaker post-stimulation circuit responsiveness. The useful claim is that at least part of the cognitive-control effect of ventral-striatal-like stimulation is being driven by recruited cortical afferents rather than local striatal cell bodies. That matters for targeting, waveform design, and how not to lie to ourselves about mechanism.

The second paper is **Treatment of Persisting Symptoms after Concussion with Repetitive Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation: A Double-Blinded, Randomized, Controlled Trial**. This is a proper reminder that clinical neuromodulation needs more than plausible targets and patient suffering. Ninety-one adults with persistent post-concussion symptoms were randomized to active or sham left-dorsolateral-prefrontal rTMS across twenty sessions. Both groups improved over time, but active stimulation did not significantly outperform sham on the Rivermead Postconcussion Symptoms Questionnaire at post-treatment, one month, or three months. The useful read is not that rTMS never matters here. It is that this specific open-loop recipe did not separate from expectancy and nonspecific care effects, which should lower confidence in generic DLPFC carryover from depression logic.

The third paper is **Precision phase targeting of event-related oscillations using real-time closed-loop TMS-EEG**. This is a methods preprint, but it hits the right problem. Instead of predicting oscillatory phase from highly periodic signals, the authors directly detect phase in real time and report microsecond-scale triggering. In simulations and human EEG without active TMS delivery, the system achieved higher triggering probability and lower phase-error variability than a prediction-based baseline, especially for event-related theta during spatial-navigation tasks. The meaningful claim is that precision phase-locked stimulation may be able to leave the comfort zone of ongoing alpha rhythms and target briefer cognitive states.

The fourth paper is **Brain Functional Connectivity Signatures of Craving Across Substance Use Disorders: A Transdiagnostic Approach**. In resting-state functional MRI from people with cannabis, opioid, and tobacco use disorders, the authors use connectome-based predictive modeling to derive a distributed craving network that predicts self-reported craving and then generalizes to external alcohol-dependence and smoker datasets. The useful part is the transdiagnostic ambition and the lesion analysis highlighting medial orbitofrontal, dorsal posterior cingulate, and medial frontal contributions. The caution is that this is still a resting-state predictive signature, not a causal circuit map or a clinically deployed relapse model.

Two additional papers were worth reading but stayed below note threshold today. **Experimental Validation of Finite Element Models for Directional DBS: The Critical Role of Boundary Conditions on VTA Accuracy** is useful citation material because it shows that common current-controlled modeling assumptions can badly overestimate activated volume. **Multiscale reorganization of brain and behavior under large-scale electrical perturbation** is ambitious and potentially framing-relevant, but right now it reads more like a large synthesis-and-association map than something I would center a daily intervention digest around.

## Most relevant paper

**mPFC axons drive cognitive control enhancement during striatal stimulation**

This is the top paper because it pushes on a question that psychiatric neuromodulation papers often dodge: what exactly is being driven when you stimulate a capsule-striatal target? The useful result is not just that optical stimulation changed behavior. The useful result is the dissociation between stimulation of medial-prefrontal afferents, which sped set-shifting, and stimulation of local striatal neurons, which impaired performance when sustained. That gives a more concrete mechanistic read on why electrical stimulation may help cognition and why the same anatomical neighborhood can support both benefit and degradation depending on what elements are recruited.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For psychiatric DBS mechanism:** the mPFC-axon paper is a good corrective to vague ventral-striatal target talk. It argues that incoming cortical fibers may matter more than local striatal neurons for cognitive benefit.
- **For concussion neuromodulation:** the randomized rTMS trial lowers confidence in generic DLPFC carryover and raises the bar for symptom-specific or state-dependent intervention logic.
- **For closed-loop TMS:** the phase-targeting preprint matters because event-related oscillations are closer to real cognitive operations than continuous sinusoid assumptions.
- **For addiction and computational psychiatry:** the craving-connectivity paper is a useful biomarker-framing paper, but it still sits on the association side of the fence.
- **For modeling humility:** the directional-DBS FEM validation is a reminder that downstream mechanistic stories are only as good as the field model underneath them.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The better papers today are the ones that cash out mechanism into something testable. The striatal-stimulation paper matters because it separates recruited cortical afferents from local neurons and shows that those are not interchangeable. The concussion trial matters because it refuses the usual neuromodulation optimism and shows a clinically plausible recipe can still fail against sham. The closed-loop TMS-EEG paper matters because timing precision for brief event-related states is a real control problem, not a side detail. The craving-network paper matters because transdiagnostic predictive structure is more useful than diagnosis-by-diagnosis craving folklore, even if it is still not causal. Overall lesson: intervention logic gets sharper when we ask what element, what state, and what benchmark actually changed.
