## Short overview

The new preserve is not a clinical win and not proof that temporal interference has solved deep-target neuromodulation. It is better than that in one narrow way. It fixes an upstream piece of sloppiness that the field often hides inside methods sections. OpTI-Mouse builds a mouse-specific forward-modeling and inverse-optimization pipeline, defines target intensity and focality as competing objectives, and then shows that inherited empirical montages are often nowhere near optimal. For the CA3-CA1 hippocampal target, the optimized solution reaches 10.29 volts per meter versus 2.89 and 4.47 for the two empirical baselines while staying in the same focality band. For dentate gyrus, it holds intensity roughly constant while tightening focality. The cross-model Sim4Life check preserves the same direction of advantage. Good. That means the result is not just one lucky meshing artifact.

The context notes sharpen the lesson. The Parkinson temporal-interference pilot matters because it is one of the few blinded human studies that forces individualized deep-target stimulation to answer a real clinical question. The closed-loop EEG-TMS paper matters because it makes the controller discover the right phase online instead of assuming fixed timing is good enough. And the depression temporal-interference biomarker note matters because it at least asks why some patients respond and others do not. Put together, the pattern is simple. Deep-target neuromodulation should stop hard-coding parameters at every layer. Montage, timing, and patient state all need to earn their place.

## Most relevant paper

**OpTI-Mouse: Optimization for Targeted Temporal Interference Stimulation in the Mouse Brain**

This gets top rank because it solves a more embarrassing problem than most new stimulation papers bother to acknowledge. A lot of preclinical temporal-interference work still inherits electrode layouts from earlier studies and then argues about biology downstream as if the targeting step were already competent. OpTI-Mouse says no. It makes targeting an explicit multi-objective optimization problem and then shows that the performance gap versus empirical montages can be huge even before any physiology enters the story. That does not prove temporal interference works therapeutically. It does make future mechanistic work harder to fake.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For temporal-interference methods:** montage choice should be treated as a Pareto optimization over intensity and focality, not as a legacy recipe copied from the last paper.
- **For preclinical neuromodulation baselines:** a several-fold target-intensity gap under matched current limits means many empirical mouse setups should be treated as weak baselines, not neutral defaults.
- **For deep-target translation:** better field optimization is necessary but still not sufficient. The missing bridge remains actual neural and behavioral validation.
- **For adaptive-control framing:** today's keep complements the closed-loop EEG-TMS note nicely. Parameter search should happen both upstream in montage design and online in state-dependent control.
- **For patient heterogeneity framing:** the depression temporal-interference biomarker paper remains useful mainly because it asks who benefits, not because its right-lingual-gyrus signal is anywhere near decisive.
- **For standing-interest lanes:** the hypnosis and CBT-combination hits are real, but they remain clinically or physiologically interesting side paths rather than stronger mechanism keeps than today's targeting note.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful June twenty-third pattern is that neuromodulation work gets less magical and more credible when parameters stop being inherited and start being earned. Today's new paper matters because it shows that even before you ask whether temporal interference changes behavior, you should ask whether the montage was optimized at all. The Parkinson pilot matters because human deep-target claims need blinded clinical contact. The closed-loop EEG-TMS paper matters because timing should be discovered, not assumed. And the depression heterogeneity note matters because target names alone do not explain responders. The shared lesson is that montage selection, control timing, and patient state all belong inside the problem, not outside it.
