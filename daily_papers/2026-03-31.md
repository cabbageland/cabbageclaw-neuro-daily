## Theme

Today is a **targeting-logic versus evidence-depth** day. The strongest clinical paper is a randomized head-to-head transcranial magnetic stimulation trial in major depression showing that two connectome-derived targets can differentially shift anxiety versus depressive symptom burden. The strongest network paper is a pediatric vagus nerve stimulation study arguing that baseline anterior network integrity and resilience to interictal perturbation help explain who responds. The strongest computational paper is a control-theory preprint that finally tries to put rigorous passivity analysis under seizure suppression rather than merely simulating another closed-loop heuristic. Together they point at the same practical lesson: personalized neuromodulation gets more credible when targetable circuits, state perturbations, and controller assumptions are made explicit rather than hidden inside syndrome labels.

## Short overview

The most decision-relevant paper today is **Circuit-targeted modulation of anxiety symptoms in individuals with major depression: A randomized head-to-head TMS trial** because it prospectively tests a symptom-specific connectomic targeting claim instead of selling it retrospectively. The result is useful but not magical: both targets improved depression similarly, while the dorsomedial anxiosomatic target improved anxiety more strongly. That is a real step toward symptom-selective intervention logic, though the sample is still small and the trial is not a full personalization study.

The most mechanistically useful paper is **Association of Brain Network Perturbations With Response to Vagus Nerve Stimulation in Children With Drug-Resistant Focal Epilepsy**. It does not give a deployable predictor yet, but it is better than generic biomarker fishing because it links response to both baseline alpha-band network organization and post-IED microstate resilience. That makes the heterogeneity story more circuit-legible.

The most conceptually useful paper is **Passivity-Based Control of Electrographic Seizures in a Neural Mass Model of Epilepsy**. It is still model-land rather than clinic-land, but it does something rare and valuable: it asks whether seizure dynamics in a standard neural mass model are even compatible with a passivity-based feedback design, and what sensor and output redesign choices are mathematically required if they are not.

## Most relevant paper

**Circuit-targeted modulation of anxiety symptoms in individuals with major depression: A randomized head-to-head TMS trial**

This is the top paper because it prospectively tests a symptom-circuit targeting story that usually stays in retrospective connectomic marketing slides. Adults with major depressive disorder and substantial anxiety symptoms were randomized to thirty TMS treatments at either a dysphoric target near the conventional left dorsolateral prefrontal cortex site or an anxiosomatic dorsomedial target. Depression improved at similar rates in both arms, but anxiety improved more strongly with the anxiosomatic target. That is not proof of fully personalized psychiatry, but it is a real signal that symptom dimensions may be separable enough to matter for target choice.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For psychiatric TMS targeting:** the head-to-head trial matters because it prospectively tests a connectome-derived symptom-target hypothesis rather than inferring it after the fact.
- **For epilepsy biomarker logic:** the pediatric VNS paper is useful because it links response not just to static connectivity but to how a frontotemporal network gets perturbed after interictal discharges.
- **For seizure-control framing:** the passivity paper matters because it asks whether the controller class matches the system properties at all, which is a question many closed-loop neuromodulation papers politely skip.
- **For long-horizon intervention modeling:** the STEER preprint is worth keeping in the background because it treats plastic adaptation as something to infer and optimize, not just tolerate.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The best work today is trying to earn the word precision by specifying what varies across patients and what exactly a controller or target is supposed to do. The TMS trial suggests symptom-specific circuit targeting may have prospective value, but only modestly and in a small sample. The VNS network paper makes response heterogeneity more intelligible by tying it to anterior network organization and perturbation resilience rather than demographics or seizure topography. The control-theory paper is further from practice, yet useful because it tightens the formal language around seizure suppression. So the field looks strongest when it replaces broad syndromic neuromodulation claims with explicit circuit, perturbation, and control assumptions.
