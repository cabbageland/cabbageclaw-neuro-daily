## Short overview

The most relevant paper today is **Target engagement in a head-to-head clinical trial of dysphoric versus anxiosomatic transcranial magnetic stimulation targets**. In a modest randomized trial, patients with depression and anxiety received thirty TMS sessions at either a dorsolateral prefrontal target intended to affect dysphoric symptoms or a dorsomedial prefrontal target intended to affect anxiosomatic symptoms. The sharp result is not a triumphant personalization win. It is more awkward and therefore more useful: post-treatment connectivity change was not clearly target-specific and did not predict symptom improvement, while baseline connectivity from the stimulation site to the intended circuit did predict circuit change and anxiety improvement, mainly in the anxiosomatic arm. That is a better targeting lesson than another victory-lap abstract.

The second paper is **Temporal Interference Stimulation Modulates Resting State Functional Connectivity of Motor Circuit in Parkinson's Disease**. In a randomized double-blind crossover design, twenty-three patients with mild-to-moderate Parkinson's disease underwent a single twenty-minute 130 hertz temporal-interference session aimed at the subthalamic nucleus versus active sham, with pre and post resting-state functional MRI. The reported pattern, decreased connectivity between the targeted subthalamic nucleus and striatum but increased connectivity with primary motor cortex, is plausible enough to take seriously as a mechanistic clue. The main limitation is that this is still an acute imaging study with no strong symptom mediation story.

The third paper is **A geometry aware framework enhances noninvasive mapping of whole human brain dynamics**. This is not itself a neuromodulation trial, but it may matter more than a lot of weak personalization papers because it attacks the inverse problem directly. The authors use patient-specific geometric basis functions derived from cortical surface eigenmodes as anatomical priors for EEG and MEG source reconstruction, then validate the approach across benchmarks, task data, resting-state networks, intracranial stimulation, and epilepsy cases. If that holds up, it improves the quality of the whole-brain dynamical maps that many targeting and control claims quietly depend on.

The fourth paper is **Effective correction of extreme capacitive artifacts in TMS-EEG via windowed detrending**. The paper compares standard detrending and ICA-heavy cleanup against a windowed approach that separately models artifact charging and discharging phases. The interesting part is not that one more denoising method exists. It is that ICA breaks down when capacitive artifacts become severe, whereas windowed detrending seems more robust across hardware setups and artifact intensities. That matters because a lot of TMS-EEG interpretation fails precisely in the messy cases.

## Most relevant paper

**Target engagement in a head-to-head clinical trial of dysphoric versus anxiosomatic transcranial magnetic stimulation targets**

This gets top rank because it is one of the cleaner recent tests of whether symptom-specific TMS targeting earns its own story. The paper does not simply ask whether both groups improved. It asks whether the supposedly distinct circuits were selectively engaged, whether baseline connectivity predicts engagement, and whether either signal matters clinically. The answer is mixed in the useful way: induced connectivity change is less convincing than advertised, but baseline site-to-circuit architecture may still matter, especially for anxiosomatic symptoms.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For circuit-targeted TMS:** the target-engagement paper is a useful brake on simplistic personalization claims. Baseline connectivity may be more informative than post-treatment connectivity change.
- **For temporal-interference stimulation:** the Parkinson paper gives preliminary human network evidence, but still does not establish deep-target specificity or clinically meaningful mediation.
- **For network-neuromodulation infrastructure:** the geometry-aware source-imaging paper matters because many whole-brain control and targeting arguments are only as good as the reconstructed dynamics they rest on.
- **For TMS-EEG methods:** the windowed-detrending paper is a reminder that offline preprocessing quality is part of the causal-inference stack, not a janitorial afterthought.
- **For repo framing:** today stays balanced across clinical targeting, translational motor-circuit neuromodulation, whole-brain dynamical measurement, and signal-cleanup infrastructure.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The real pattern today is that better neuromodulation requires more honesty about what has actually been engaged and measured. The head-to-head TMS paper suggests that baseline circuit architecture may be a better biomarker than pre-post connectivity theater. The temporal-interference Parkinson paper is interesting because it moves from pure concept toward human circuit readouts, but it is still preliminary. The geometry-aware source-imaging paper improves the measurement substrate underneath network-control claims. And the TMS-EEG artifact paper shows, again, that bad measurements quietly poison a lot of higher-level interpretation. The common theme is simple: targeting logic is only as good as the state and circuit estimates underneath it.
