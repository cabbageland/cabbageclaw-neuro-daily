# 2026-03-25

## Theme

Personalized neuromodulation is slowly becoming more real: invasive mapping and adaptive control papers are starting to specify **where**, **when**, and **why** to stimulate instead of just reporting another average effect size. The good work is still narrow and early. The weak work still loves grandiose claims.

## Short overview

Today’s strongest hit is an OCD paper because it actually links target selection, symptom-linked physiology, and downstream circuit engagement in the same patient. The Parkinson adaptive DBS trial matters because it moves aDBS from short in-clinic demos toward longer at-home use, though the endpoint framing is softer than one would like. The closed-loop tACS-fMRI paper is an interesting noninvasive proof of concept, but the sample is tiny and the behavioral gain is modest. A computational paper on DSR+MPC is worth preserving as adjacent inspiration, not as evidence for deployable intervention design.

## Ranked list

1. **Invasive Brain Mapping Identifies Personalized Therapeutic Neuromodulation Targets for Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder**  
   Directly relevant. Most relevant paper today.  
   Why it matters: It ties personalized target discovery to symptom-linked electrophysiology and to measurable suppression of a plausible circuit biomarker.

2. **Long-Term Personalized Adaptive Deep Brain Stimulation in Parkinson Disease: A Nonrandomized Clinical Trial**  
   Directly relevant.  
   Why it matters: Bigger and more practical than most aDBS studies; shows long-term at-home feasibility and safety, but not yet the kind of clean causal win that settles biomarker/control policy questions.

3. **Closing the loop between brain and electrical stimulation: A proof-of-concept randomized trial of real-time fMRI-guided tACS optimization**  
   Directly relevant, but still early.  
   Why it matters: One of the more concrete attempts at individualized noninvasive network targeting using online neural feedback rather than canned stimulation parameters.

4. **A Data-Driven Closed-Loop Control Approach to Drive Neural State Transitions for Mechanistic Insight**  
   Adjacent inspiration.  
   Why it matters: Useful as computational framing for controllability and target ranking in affective-state transitions; not yet something to trust as a neuromodulation design engine.

## Most relevant paper

**Invasive Brain Mapping Identifies Personalized Therapeutic Neuromodulation Targets for Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder**

It earns attention because it does three things in one package: (1) maps candidate stimulation sites inside the CSTC circuit, (2) identifies a symptom-linked neural readout rather than relying only on anatomy, and (3) shows that the selected sites suppress that readout and translate into an early therapeutic response after chronic implantation. It is still a single-patient proof of concept, so it does not settle generalizability, but it is much closer to an actual circuit-therapeutics workflow than most OCD DBS papers.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For neuromodulation framing:** the strongest current story is not “closed loop solves everything,” but **personalized target engagement plus explicit biomarker logic**.
- **For baseline choice:** fixed-parameter or anatomy-only targeting baselines are looking increasingly weak if a paper claims personalization without showing biomarker engagement.
- **For novelty claims:** invasive mapping + chronic implantation + symptom-linked physiology is still genuinely differentiated. By contrast, a lot of “AI-guided” or “network-guided” stimulation work is still mostly packaging.
- **For noninvasive work:** fMRI-guided tACS optimization is interesting, but the field still needs stronger comparisons against simpler individualized baselines and longer-horizon behavioral effects.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful pattern across today’s papers is that the serious work is converging on a simple requirement: if you claim precision neuromodulation, you should be able to specify a target, a state signal, and evidence that stimulation changes that signal in the predicted direction. The OCD mapping paper does this best, albeit in one patient. The Parkinson aDBS trial shows that chronic sensing-driven stimulation is operationally viable at scale, but the control logic is still relatively simple and the endpoint framing is not pristine. The tACS-fMRI study is a real proof of concept for individualized noninvasive optimization, though still far from a durable translational result. The DSR+MPC paper is most useful as a hypothesis engine for which states or regions may be controllable, not as evidence that we can already steer psychiatric state spaces cleanly.
