## Short overview

The one genuinely new preserve today is STEER. It is interesting because it refuses the usual dodge where long-term stimulation effects get absorbed into a flexible model as vague parameter drift. Instead, it explicitly separates fast within-session activity from slow across-session plasticity, then tries to learn a stimulus-conditioned law for how the slow part evolves. That is exactly the right question for chronic DBS, multi-session TMS, or any adaptive protocol that changes the system it is supposed to control. The main catch is that the paper is still method-heavy and synthetic-heavy. The real-data anchor is a longitudinal Parkinsonian rat DBS dataset, not a rich human intervention cohort.

That new note becomes more useful when placed next to three existing context notes. The SCC DBS white-matter paper says chronic depression-style stimulation may work partly through slow tract remodeling and downstream network rebalancing, not only acute perturbation. The spatially diffuse control paper says many brain-control arguments are physically silly if they pretend the actuator is a perfect point source. The windowed-detrending TMS-EEG paper says your perturbational biomarker can still be fake if your artifact model collapses in the ugly regimes.

## Most relevant paper

**Inferring brain plasticity rule under long-term stimulation with structured recurrent dynamics**

This gets top rank because it sharpens a question that cuts across devices and disorders. If repeated stimulation changes circuit structure over days to weeks, then long-course intervention is not merely state control. It is control of a system whose own transition rules are moving. STEER is not yet definitive biology, and its strongest evidence still comes from synthetic settings plus a modest rat DBS dataset. But it is asking the right question in a much less lazy way than most longitudinal stimulation modeling papers.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For chronic neuromodulation modeling:** stop treating across-session change as nuisance drift by default. Ask whether there is a learnable slow plasticity law and whether it predicts unseen future protocols.
- **For mechanism claims:** the SCC DBS macaque paper is still a useful counterweight because it ties the slow-timescale story to white-matter remodeling and network changes rather than only to latent-space coefficients.
- **For control theory:** the spatially diffuse input paper remains the right baseline check whenever a control model assumes infinitely focal actuation.
- **For perturbational biomarkers:** the TMS-EEG artifact paper stays relevant because a clever state or biomarker model built on unstable cleanup is still decorative.
- **For standing-interest lanes:** today's CBT-plus-intervention hits were interesting but still too feasibility-heavy, and targeted hypnosis and hypnotherapy searching did not surface a new preserve-worthy mechanism paper.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful June thirteenth pattern is that long-course brain intervention should be modeled as slow circuit editing, not only as repeated acute perturbation. STEER gives that idea a strong computational form by separating fast neural response from slow plasticity evolution. The SCC DBS white-matter paper says slow stimulation effects can plausibly live in tract remodeling and network rebalancing. The diffuse-input control paper says actuator realism changes the control landscape, and the TMS-EEG artifact paper says measurement realism changes whether your biomarker claim is even real. Put differently: if you want serious intervention logic, model the slow law, respect the field geometry, and do not trust perturbational readouts built on fragile preprocessing.
