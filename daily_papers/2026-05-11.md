## Short overview

The most relevant paper today is **Subthalamic Signatures of Freezing while Turning in Parkinson’s Disease**. In thirteen Parkinson's disease patients with freezing of gait, the authors recorded subthalamic local field potentials synchronized with gait kinematics while patients were off medication and off stimulation. They compare regular turning, freezing while turning, unimpaired walking, and the transition period before turns. The useful result is that low-beta abnormalities appear both before and during freezing while turning, prolonged bursts mark the freezing state, and a simple mixed-effects logistic model using burst duration plus kinematic features reaches leave-one-subject-out AUROC around 0.84. That is not deployment-ready, but it is closer to a plausible control feature set than generic freezing biomarkers.

The second paper is **Neurofunctional mechanisms of action of trigeminal nerve stimulation (TNS) in children and adolescents with Attention Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD)**. This is a secondary mechanistic analysis from a larger multicenter United Kingdom randomized controlled trial that had already failed clinically. Across sixty-two children and adolescents with ADHD, real versus rigorous sham TNS showed no significant effects on fMRI activation during response inhibition, working memory, or sustained-attention tasks, and no task-performance benefit either. The value here is mostly negative and clarifying. Earlier pilot EEG optimism did not survive a larger sham-controlled fMRI test.

The third paper is **Subthalamic nucleus deep brain stimulation in Meige syndrome: mapping the optimal stimulation sites and network targets**. In sixty-five patients across two centers, the authors relate modeled stimulation geometry and network connectivity to long-term motor improvement, then validate the resulting maps in an independent cohort. The useful part is not that they found a sweet spot. Everyone finds a sweet spot. The useful part is that the local dorsolateral subthalamic target and the cerebellar and somatosensory network pattern both survive external validation reasonably well.

## Most relevant paper

**Subthalamic Signatures of Freezing while Turning in Parkinson’s Disease**

This gets top rank because it is the closest thing today to intervention-usable state estimation. The paper does not solve adaptive deep brain stimulation for freezing of gait, but it identifies a specific low-beta burst abnormality before and during freezing while turning and shows that those features carry classification signal beyond generic description.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For adaptive stimulation framing:** the freezing-while-turning paper supports designing around transition-state detection, not just full freezing episodes. That matters for any control loop that wants to intervene early rather than react after failure.
- **For developmental neuromodulation skepticism:** the ADHD TNS paper is a useful corrective. A larger sham-controlled fMRI study found neither neural task effects nor behavioral gains, so older pilot-level mechanism optimism should be discounted.
- **For targeting work:** the Meige DBS paper remains a strong baseline for what connectomic targeting papers should at least try to do, namely pair local and network models and test them out of sample.
- **Adjacent inspiration only:** **Cardio-respiratory modulation of arousal and excitability states** looks interesting as a perspective on multi-organ state variables, but it is still a conceptual synthesis rather than a directly intervention-ready paper.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful pattern today is that better neuromodulation papers either expose state structure or kill weak stories. The Parkinson's preprint is worth attention because it operationalizes a transition state with concrete LFP and kinematic features that could matter for adaptive control. The ADHD TNS paper is worth preserving because it says, pretty clearly, that a more rigorous trial found neither the expected brain effect nor clinical benefit. And the Meige DBS paper remains a reminder that targeting claims become much more interesting once they survive some version of external validation.
