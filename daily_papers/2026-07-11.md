## Short overview

The main fresh preserve today is the June 26, 2026 arXiv paper **Neuromorphic Energy-Aware Learning for Adaptive Deep Brain Stimulation**. It is a computational neuromodulation paper, not a clinical efficacy paper, but it earns archive status because it asks the right systems question. The authors train a deep spiking Q-network in a biophysical cortico-basal ganglia-thalamic rat model of parkinsonian beta pathology, and the reward does not just penalize uncontrolled oscillations. It also penalizes stimulation energy itself. That sounds obvious, but it is still rarer than it should be. The result is a controller that suppresses pathological beta-band activity by **45.2%** in the acute pathological state, cuts cumulative stimulation charge by **80.0%** relative to continuous deep brain stimulation, beats a dual-threshold adaptive-DBS heuristic in the alternating-state test, and then survives deployment on neuromorphic hardware at **0.52 mW** inference power.

The paper is still a simulation paper, and that caveat matters. It uses a dopamine-depleted rat cortico-basal ganglia-thalamic model, not patient recordings. The benchmark against Jetson-class edge hardware exaggerates the practical hardware gap compared with what a purpose-built low-power conventional controller might do. And the biomarker target remains a narrow one, namely beta-band suppression in the globus pallidus internus. But the paper is still worth keeping because it refuses two common cheats at once. It does not confuse low controller power with low implant power, and it does not confuse raw closed-loop rhetoric with a controller that has to outperform an actual adaptive baseline.

The surrounding fresh papers mostly sharpened boundaries rather than beating the new preserve. Two JAMA Network Open depression trials are real and worth reading: **Prefrontal Transcranial Pulse Stimulation for Major Depressive Disorder: A Randomized Clinical Trial** and **Intermittent Theta-Burst Stimulation and Depressive Symptoms in Major Depressive Disorder: A Randomized Clinical Trial**. But both still operate with broad left-prefrontal treatment logic rather than tighter control or targeting logic. A **Molecular Psychiatry** paper on connectome-based growth models in subthreshold depression remains useful as a heterogeneity reminder, especially because it reports biotypes with different bright-light-therapy responses, but it still lives closer to stratification than to immediately steal-worthy intervention design. The standing hypnosis and CBT-plus-interventional lanes were checked again, and they still came in broader, weaker, or more off-target than the existing archive anchors.

## Most relevant paper

**Neuromorphic Energy-Aware Learning for Adaptive Deep Brain Stimulation**

This gets top rank because it is the only fresh paper today that actually changes the optimization story rather than just widening a familiar efficacy story. The recent JAMA transcranial pulse stimulation and once-daily intermittent theta-burst stimulation trials both look clinically real enough to matter, but their intervention logic is still broad: stimulate the left prefrontal target, beat sham, and interpret circuit changes afterward. The new neuromorphic paper starts with a better engineering question: once controller inference is cheap enough for an implant, what if the stimulator becomes the remaining power cost worth learning against directly?

Methodologically, the paper is cleaner than the average "AI for adaptive DBS" pitch. The controller does not consume precomputed spectral biomarkers. It sees the raw multi-channel spike raster produced by a biophysical cortico-basal ganglia-thalamic simulation, pooled from 80 channels to 16 hardware-compatible channels, and emits discrete increase-maintain-decrease actions for frequency, pulse width, and amplitude. The reward is discontinuous on purpose: if pathological beta power stays above threshold, the policy is punished for failed symptom suppression; if beta power is already controlled, the policy is rewarded for saving stimulation energy. That is a better setup than "maximize clinical effect and maybe be efficient later" because it makes energy an actual part of the control problem.

The paper also earns points for showing where its own strength stops. It is still simulation-only, still rat-model-dependent, still benchmarked against a generous hardware straw man, and still tied to a relatively simple beta-suppression objective. But even with those caveats, the paper clears a bar that a lot of adaptive-neuromodulation work misses: it compares against a clinical dual-threshold heuristic, shows that the non-spiking baselines collapse into bad strategies, includes a sensory-ablation check to rule out open-loop drift, and measures the distilled policy on actual neuromorphic hardware rather than stopping at training curves.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For adaptive neuromodulation framing:** the useful move is not just "spiking network on a chip." The useful move is putting actuator energy directly into the reward.
- **For implant power-budget rhetoric:** this paper argues that sub-milliwatt inference is necessary but not sufficient. Once controller power is low enough, stimulation power becomes the next thing that has to be optimized.
- **For hardware benchmarking claims:** the Jetson comparison flatters the size of the deployment gap. The stronger and safer interpretation is that full edge-AI modules are implausible implant controllers, not that every non-neuromorphic baseline is automatically doomed.
- **For closed-loop DBS baselines:** the dual-threshold adaptive-DBS heuristic remains a useful control baseline, and this paper improves on it mainly because it can modulate amplitude and pulse width continuously instead of only toggling a fixed ON state.
- **For depression-device news this week:** the fresh transcranial pulse stimulation and once-daily intermittent theta-burst trials look legitimate, but they still do much more to support efficacy than to sharpen targeting or control logic.
- **For network-heterogeneity work:** the connectome-growth subtyping paper is useful for personalized-intervention framing, especially because the reported biotypes differ in bright light therapy response, but it is still closer to stratification support than to a note that changes what to stimulate or measure next week.
- **For CBT-plus-interventional psychiatry:** the fresh gambling-disorder rTMS-plus-CBT pilot still does not establish mechanism-rich complementarity in a core psychiatry target that transfers cleanly here.
- **For hypnosis and hypnotherapy:** the recent mindful hypnotherapy meta-analysis may help broad distress calibration, but it still does not offer the state-change, biomarker, or intervention-design leverage that would outrank the existing hypnosis anchor.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful July eleventh lesson is that once controller power stops dominating, the stimulator itself becomes part of the optimization problem. Today's preserve matters because it makes that premise explicit and then turns it into a learnable control objective instead of a discussion-section platitude. It is still a simulation paper and nowhere near a patient-ready adaptive DBS policy. But as a framing object it is stronger than the fresh clinical trials, which mostly widen efficacy evidence while leaving control logic broad, and stronger than the fresh heterogeneity paper, which still stops short of changing next-step intervention design.
