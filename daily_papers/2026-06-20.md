## Short overview

The new preserve is the seizure-forecasting preprint from the Paris group. Its useful move is to treat daily seizure risk as a compact network-state problem. They take ten-minute resting-state intracranial EEG recordings, build phase-locking-value graphs over seizure-onset-zone electrodes, embed those graphs into a two-dimensional Euclidean space, and then ask whether node-level geometry looks more interictal or preictal. The paper matters because it uses a pseudo-prospective setup and gets genuinely decent patient-specific results from short daily sessions rather than from endless continuous monitoring. It is also refreshingly imperfect. The all-band average is near chance, the best results depend on patient-specific frequency-band selection, and stronger baselines like hyperbolic embeddings or SVMs still match or beat it. Good. That makes the paper useful as state-estimation infrastructure rather than as a fake universal solution.

The context notes sharpen what to do with that signal. **Non-vectorial integration of intersectional short-pulse stimulation enables enhanced deep brain modulation and effective seizure control** remains the better actuation paper because it asks whether a mechanistically grounded stimulation design can actually suppress seizures rather than merely decorate field maps. **Quantifying State-Dependent Control Properties of Brain Dynamics from Perturbation Responses** remains the right control-theory ballast because it estimates controllable structure from real perturbations instead of from passive-data fantasy. **Common Electrophysiology Biomarkers Collected at Home Robustly Track Depression Recovery With Deep Brain Stimulation** remains the best adjacent psychiatric reminder that dense home longitudinal sensing can make slow-timescale neuromodulation legible if the biomarker survives transfer. Standing-interest checks for hypnosis, hypnotherapy, and CBT-plus-interventional psychiatry did not surface a stronger mechanism or sequencing paper than this stack.

## Most relevant paper

**Low-dimensional representation of brain networks for seizure risk forecasting**

This gets top rank because it turns a vague ambition into a concrete workflow. Instead of promising seizure prediction from everything all at once, it asks whether brief, standardized, daily resting-state recordings already contain enough network-state structure to estimate next-day seizure risk. The answer is not universally yes. It is yes only if band choice is individualized, and even then the Euclidean model is more competitive than dominant. That is precisely why it is worth preserving. It says the hard part is not only finding a better classifier. It is learning what state should be estimated, on what timescale, from what recording burden, and with what level of interpretability.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For seizure forecasting:** stop assuming only continuous monitoring deserves respect. This paper says short daily recordings may already be enough to estimate a clinically meaningful risk state.
- **For model choice:** do not reward geometric elegance by default. The Euclidean embedding is useful because it is interpretable and competitive, not because it clearly beats hyperbolic or SVM baselines.
- **For closed-loop neuromodulation:** state estimation, actuation, and perturbation-grounded control are separable problems. Today's new note covers the first. The ISP seizure-control paper and the perturbation-response controllability paper remain the right ballast for the other two.
- **For cross-domain transfer:** the home SCC DBS biomarker note remains the best reminder that dense longitudinal sensing can make neuromodulation legible when the marker survives device and cohort transfer.
- **For standing-interest lanes:** current hypnosis, hypnotherapy, and CBT-plus-interventional-psychiatry hits did not produce a sharper mechanistic or sequencing preserve than the new seizure-state note.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful June twentieth pattern is that neuromodulation gets more serious when it admits that a controller needs an estimated state, not just a stimulation recipe. The new seizure-forecasting paper matters because it shows one plausible way to get that state from short daily network snapshots. The old ISP paper matters because a good estimate still needs a believable actuator. The perturbation-response control paper matters because state and controllability should be inferred from what stimulation actually does, not from decorative control metaphors. And the home SCC DBS biomarker matters because this same architecture generalizes beyond epilepsy. The shared lesson is simple: closed-loop credibility comes from the whole stack, not from one clever decoder.
