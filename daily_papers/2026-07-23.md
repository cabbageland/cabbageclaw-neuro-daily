## Short overview

The top fresh preserve today is **Latent class modeling of repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation response trajectories in treatment-resistant depression**. It gets the top rank because it turns a vague and overused complaint into a sharper clinical object. Everybody says rTMS response is heterogeneous. This paper actually models that heterogeneity as weekly trajectory classes in 308 treatment-resistant depression patients treated during an acute course at the UCSD Interventional Psychiatry Program. On raw PHQ-9 scores, the best fit is a three-class quadratic solution: High Baseline, Minimal Improvement; Moderate Baseline, Moderate Improvement; and High Baseline, Large Improvement. The large-improvement class shows the best reduction and response rate, while suicidal-ideation history points toward the worst class. That matters because the useful question is not merely whether rTMS works on average. It is whether some patients look like early pivot candidates before the course is over. The obvious caution is that this note is based on abstract-only inspection after more than 10 full-text attempts, so the class diagnostics, protocol detail, and robustness checks stay partly hidden.

The second fresh preserve is **Learning the Brain's Dynamics as a Port-Hamiltonian System: A GNN-Surrogate Metriplectic Twin for Non-Equilibrium Cortical Dynamics and Closed-Loop Neuromodulation**. This is not a stimulation-validation paper yet, and it should not be oversold as one. What makes it worth keeping is a rarer virtue: the model is forced to answer falsifiable dynamical tests that it did not author. Fitted to more than 1.1 million training phasor samples from the EEG Motor Movement/Imagery database with a by-subject held-out split, it reproduces near-critical avalanche branching but fails the aperiodic 1/f slope and DFA long-range-correlation tests. That mixed result is exactly why the note is useful. It is a cleaner piece of modeling discipline than the usual "physics-inspired" control rhetoric, even if the perturbational rung is still missing and the abstract page's unresolved placeholder strings are sloppy.

The best existing anchor remains **A Whole-Brain Dynamical Framework Linking Resting-State Activity to TMS-Evoked Responses**. That older note is still stronger than today's new port-Hamiltonian paper because it actually links intrinsic dynamics to perturbational spread rather than merely promising to do so later. ## Most relevant paper

**Latent class modeling of repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation response trajectories in treatment-resistant depression**

This gets the top rank because it upgrades a common complaint into an actual decision object. rTMS papers constantly talk about heterogeneity, but too many of them still collapse the course into one final response number or a binary responder label. This paper instead models weekly PHQ-9 trajectories during acute treatment in 308 treatment-resistant depression patients and asks whether distinct shapes exist.

From the accessible abstract, the answer is yes. The raw-score solution prefers three classes: High Baseline, Minimal Improvement; Moderate Baseline, Moderate Improvement; and High Baseline, Large Improvement. The large-improvement class reportedly achieves 65.5 percent symptom reduction and an 84 percent response rate. A baseline-adjusted sensitivity analysis simplifies the picture into moderate-versus-large improvement, and the baseline predictor model reportedly reaches multi-class AUC of 0.82.

The useful part is the clinical implication, not the statistics alone. If high baseline severity and suicidal-ideation history push patients toward the minimal-improvement class, then a stimulation program should stop pretending every patient deserves the same passive wait-and-see course. A trajectory-class framing is much closer to an early-triage logic than mean endpoint change is.

The main caveat is that this note is based on abstract-only inspection after more than 10 real full-text attempts across PubMed, DOI, Elsevier, ScienceDirect, browser-rendered publisher access, and title-search routes. That means the class diagnostics, missing-data handling, protocol specifics, and robustness analyses remain partly obscured. So the result is useful and preserve-worthy, but not yet fully trusted.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For rTMS outcome papers:** average endpoint response rates are a weak baseline once weekly symptom curves can be modeled as distinct trajectory classes.
- **For clinical triage:** high baseline severity plus suicidal-ideation history may matter less as generic severity markers than as signals for early nonresponse risk and faster treatment reassignment.
- **For precision-neuromodulation rhetoric:** explicit class structure is more useful than vague claims that heterogeneity exists.
- **For computational control papers:** the new port-Hamiltonian paper is useful precisely because it does not count kinematic fit as enough; it checks branching, spectrum, and DFA separately and fails two of them.
- **For standing-interest CBT combinations:** the freshest visible hits were still pilots, manualization papers, or broad synthesis, not better mechanism or sequencing logic than the existing stimulation-plus-psychotherapy meta-analysis anchor.
- **For standing-interest hypnosis and hypnotherapy:** the newest July 22 PubMed hits were procedure-oriented randomized trials rather than stronger biomarkers, circuits, or state-estimation work.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful July twenty-third lesson is that precision talk should become more concrete on both the clinical and modeling sides. The best fresh clinical paper says rTMS patients do not just differ in endpoint magnitude; they appear to travel along different response trajectories, and some look like early pivot candidates. The best fresh modeling paper says a control-ready cortical model should be graded against invariants it did not write for itself, not merely praised for elegant structure. If a paper cannot say who is failing early, or which dynamical tests it still flunks, the precision story is still mostly decorative.
