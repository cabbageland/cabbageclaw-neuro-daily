## Short overview

The most relevant paper today is **Multitarget brain implants enable generalized decoding of Parkinson’s disease symptoms from chronic home recordings**. Across 16 Parkinson’s participants and more than 581 hours of home recordings from cortical and deep-brain implants, the authors train cross-patient machine-learning decoders for bradykinesia, tremor, and dyskinesia. The real advance is not just higher accuracy than beta power. It is that the models decode multiple symptoms, work without patient-specific retraining, and are framed as a bridge between adaptive DBS timing and connectomic steering.

The second paper is **Distinct Predictive and Recovery EEG Biomarkers of Motor Function after Noninvasive Brain Stimulation in Subacute Stroke Patients**. Twelve subacute stroke patients received dual-mode noninvasive brain stimulation, and baseline EEG features were used to predict responder status while longitudinal EEG changes were tested against motor improvement. The useful result is conceptual: the feature that best predicts response at baseline, ipsilesional temporal beta power, is not the same feature that tracks recovery over time, which was relative delta power change at ipsilesional occipital cortex. That is a cleaner intervention-logic claim than the usual biomarker pileup.

The third paper is **Safety and feasibility of combined anterior limb of the internal capsule–nucleus accumbens deep brain stimulation in carefully selected children and adolescents with severe, treatment-refractory autism spectrum disorder: a single-arm pilot study**. Twelve pediatric autism patients underwent bilateral ALIC-NAc DBS, 11 completed six months, and exploratory symptom scores improved without serious intervention-related adverse events. This is worth attention because pediatric circuit intervention in severe autism is a real boundary case for neuromodulation, and the paper at least reports tolerability and exploratory local field potential changes. But the design is open-label, single-arm, and small enough that efficacy inference is still fragile.

## Most relevant paper

**Multitarget brain implants enable generalized decoding of Parkinson’s disease symptoms from chronic home recordings**

This gets top rank because it points toward a more realistic architecture for adaptive neuromodulation. Instead of assuming one oscillatory biomarker can stand in for all clinically relevant variation, it uses multisite recordings and wearable-derived symptom labels to decode bradykinesia, tremor, and dyskinesia separately, then connects those decoded states to a proof-of-principle strategy for symptom-specific tract steering. The main caveat is hardware realism: the current setup depends on extra cortical strips and a research implant platform, so translation to standard clinical hardware is not solved.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For adaptive DBS:** the Parkinson paper strengthens the case that generalized multisymptom decoding can outperform beta-based control and may be the better long-run control framing.
- **For biomarker papers more broadly:** the stroke EEG paper is a useful corrective against assuming that one feature should both predict responders and serve as a recovery surrogate.
- **For developmental neuromodulation:** the autism DBS pilot is a reminder that feasibility and early symptom movement are not the same thing as causal efficacy, especially in an open-label pediatric cohort.
- **What did not make the cut:** several adjacent precision-psychiatry and brain-connectivity preprints were interesting in framing, but today’s strongest preserve notes were the ones with accessible manuscript PDFs and clearer intervention logic.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The best papers today reject the lazy idea that one biomarker, one target, or one average score is enough. The Parkinson preprint says adaptive control should decode symptom-specific states from richer multisite signals. The stroke paper says prediction features and recovery-tracking features can be different objects and should be treated that way. The autism pilot says that once neuromodulation enters severe developmental psychiatry, feasibility and safety reporting matter, but uncontrolled symptom improvements still do not earn mechanistic confidence. Different modalities, same lesson: intervention logic gets stronger when state estimation, control target, and evidentiary standard are kept separate.
