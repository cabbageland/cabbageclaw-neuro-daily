## Short overview

The most relevant paper today is **Circuit-targeted modulation of anxiety symptoms in individuals with major depression: A randomized head-to-head TMS trial**. This is a real prospective test of a precision-neuromodulation claim, not a post hoc map. Adults with major depression and substantial anxiety were randomized to a dysphoric left-dorsolateral target or an anxiosomatic dorsomedial target across thirty TMS sessions. Both targets improved depression by roughly the same amount, but the anxiosomatic target improved anxiety more strongly. That does not prove we now know how to personalize TMS at the individual level, but it does support the narrower claim that different symptom dimensions may ride partially separable stimulation-sensitive circuits.

The second paper is **White matter pathways mediating dorsolateral prefrontal TMS therapy for depression**. I could not get full text in this environment despite repeated attempts across the publisher page, direct PDF guess, DOI landing, PubMed, PMC search, Europe PMC, Brave title search, the authors’ code repository, Crossref, and OpenAlex-style open-access lookup. So this remains a lower-confidence abstract-and-landing-page read. Still, it is worth keeping because the framing is strong: route geometry through plausible white-matter communication paths may matter more than treating left-DLPFC targeting as a one-number anticorrelation problem.

The third paper is **The Deep Brain Stimulation Response Network in Parkinson’s Disease Operates in the High Beta Band**. This one matters because it tries to define a treatment network in both space and frequency. Using simultaneous subthalamic local field potentials and MEG from fifty Parkinson’s patients, it argues that the clinically relevant response network involving supplementary motor area, premotor cortex, and primary motor cortex operates specifically in the high-beta range. That is much closer to actionable adaptive-DBS logic than generic connectomic response maps.

The fourth paper is **A Data-Driven Closed-Loop Control Approach to Drive Neural State Transitions for Mechanistic Insight**. This is still adjacent rather than translational evidence, and it remains entirely in silico. But it is useful because it frames psychiatric intervention as a nonlinear state-transition control problem, not a static connectivity ranking exercise.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful pattern today is that intervention logic gets better when papers stop flattening symptoms and stop flattening networks. The anxiety-targeted TMS trial shows that target choice can matter for which symptom dimensions move, even when global depression response looks similar. The white-matter-pathway paper argues that target quality may depend on plausible downstream communication routes rather than static correlation slogans. The high-beta response-network paper says a treatment network should be described in spectral as well as anatomical terms. The state-transition control paper adds that controllability depends on the shape of the state space, not just on picking the right node. Overall, this was a good morning for symptom specificity, path realism, and controller-aware framing.
