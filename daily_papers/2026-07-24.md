## Short overview

The top fresh preserve today is **Subthreshold violations of trajectory predictions are sensitive to TMS of cerebellum Crus I/II**. It gets the top rank because it does something more respectable than the usual prediction-processing sermon. The authors do not just claim that the brain predicts motion. They introduce a subthreshold trajectory violation less than 60 milliseconds before a visual collision, record EEG, and then perturb right cerebellar Crus I and II with neuronavigated intermittent theta-burst stimulation in a sham-versus-verum crossover. The clean result is not a triumphant behavioral intervention effect. The clean result is that the subtle trajectory violation reliably reduces the illusion rate, and perturbed trials show a late positive CP4 ERP signal that verum cerebellar stimulation modulates in an order-dependent way. ## Most relevant paper

**Subthreshold violations of trajectory predictions are sensitive to TMS of cerebellum Crus I/II**

This gets the top rank because it actually earns the word prediction better than most prediction papers do. The authors start with a peculiar but useful illusion: when two squares move toward each other and touch briefly, people can report an illusory gap rather than contact. They then insert a subtle trajectory violation less than 60 milliseconds before the collision and ask whether that tiny irregularity interrupts whatever automatic prediction process helps generate the illusion.

On the behavioral side, the answer is yes, but in a specific way. Across the morning sessions before intervention, perturbed trials reliably reduce the illusion rate relative to non-perturbed trials. That is the cleanest result in the paper. It says the visual system is not just passively waiting for contact. It seems to be checking trajectory regularity on a millisecond timescale, and a tiny deviation is enough to interrupt the illusion.

The cerebellar intervention result is more interesting than strong. The authors recruited 24 healthy young adults, analyzed 19 participants in the behavioral and EEG datasets after exclusions, targeted right cerebellar Crus I and II with MRI-guided intermittent theta-burst stimulation, and compared sham versus verum in a crossover order. They do not get a persuasive overall behavioral TMS effect. What they do get is a more compelling neural signature: perturbed trials produce a late positive ERP over CP4, and on perturbed trials verum TMS increases that amplitude relative to pre-stimulation, with the effect appearing only when verum is delivered in the second session. That order dependence is a real caveat, not a footnote.

So the useful read is not "cerebellar TMS proves visual prediction." The useful read is that a subtle prediction-violation paradigm can expose a cerebellum-sensitive neural check without pretending the behavioral intervention story is already solved. That makes this a preserve-worthy mechanistic note, and also a reminder that a neural effect with weak behavioral translation should be interpreted as a partially opened door, not as the whole house.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful July twenty-four lesson is that mechanistic credibility still comes from putting claims under pressure. The best fresh paper does that by turning a millisecond-scale prediction idea into a subthreshold perturbation task and then nudging the cerebellum instead of merely admiring theory words from a distance. The weaker fresh papers are weak in predictable ways: hardware that is still preclinical, target maps that are still abstract-level, and computational models that are interpretable without yet changing the performance frontier. If a paper cannot show what breaks when the presumed mechanism is stressed, the mechanism is still mostly decorative.
