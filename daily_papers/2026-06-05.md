## Short overview

The new preserve-worthy paper is the mismatch-negativity, or MMN, predictor study in dorsomedial prefrontal cortex intermittent theta-burst stimulation for major depressive disorder. It was inspected through accessible full text rather than abstract scraps, and its value is very specific. The paper does **not** show that MMN distinguishes depressed patients from healthy controls, and it does **not** show that MMN itself changes after active stimulation. What it does show is narrower and more useful: in the active arm, larger baseline MMN amplitudes correlated with greater symptom reduction, while the sham arm did not show that relationship. That makes MMN a plausible cheap proxy for plasticity capacity, not a validated deployment-ready biomarker.

That result matters most when read against two existing preserved notes. The first is the routine-practice subgenual cingulate normative-connectivity paper, which is useful precisely because it is mostly negative. In a messy clinical sample, the field's favorite static anti-subgenual heuristic explained little by itself. The second is the white-matter-pathways paper, which suggests a better mechanistic story: effective TMS sites may matter because of how they communicate with deeper depression circuitry through plausible routes, not because they satisfy a single connectivity slogan. Put together, the trio says that precision depression stimulation probably needs at least three things: a dynamic or physiological capacity readout, a realistic test against clinical heterogeneity, and a mechanistic propagation story.

## Most relevant paper

**Auditory mismatch-negativity predicts response to dorsomedial prefrontal intermittent theta-burst stimulation in major depressive disorder**

This gets top rank because it engages the actual bottleneck. Precision depression stimulation does not just need a prettier target map; it needs some read on whether the patient's system is in a state where the intervention can do useful plastic work. This paper does not solve that problem, but it at least asks it properly in a sham-controlled design. The central caution is equally important: the active-arm predictive correlation is interesting, yet the active-versus-sham difference did not itself reach significance. So the paper is valuable as a serious candidate-biomarker step, not as permission to start claiming MMN-guided TMS.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful pattern for June fifth is that precision depression stimulation should stop confusing a static heuristic with an actual control stack. A serious stack would ask whether the patient has measurable plasticity or state capacity, whether the favorite targeting rule survives real clinic heterogeneity, and whether the stimulation site has plausible routes into the circuit one wants to move. The MMN paper gives a modest physiological candidate, the routine-practice connectivity paper knocks down target folklore, and the white-matter-pathways paper suggests a better structural story. That is still not a finished precision system, but it is much closer to honest intervention logic than the field's usual self-congratulation.
