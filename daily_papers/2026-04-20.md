## Short overview

The most relevant paper today is **Experimental Validation of Finite Element Models for Directional DBS: The Critical Role of Boundary Conditions on VTA Accuracy**. A lot of DBS planning and interpretation work quietly treats field models as if the implementation details are interchangeable. This paper says they are not. In a saline-phantom validation of a directional Boston Scientific lead, the best-matching finite-element setup used a voltage boundary condition with a grounded implantable pulse-generator surface, while conventional current-density formulations substantially overestimated spread. The important point is not just that one simulation stack wins. It is that widely used assumptions can distort the geometry clinicians and researchers think they are stimulating.

The second paper is **An Implantable Wireless Battery-Free Selective Vagus Nerve Stimulator**. This is still early and mostly a platform paper, but it addresses a real bottleneck in vagus nerve stimulation: therapeutic dosing is often limited by off-target activation. The device is an NFC-controlled, wirelessly powered, temporary multichannel implant built from off-the-shelf parts. In pigs it produced selective bradycardia, and in a single human pilot the authors report separation of cardiac efferent, cardiac afferent, and laryngeal effects. That is not proof of clinical benefit, but it is the kind of interface-level selectivity that matters more than generic "bioelectronic medicine" rhetoric.

The third paper is **Developmental and aging changes in brain network switching dynamics revealed by EEG phase synchronization**. This is not directly a neuromodulation paper, so I am keeping it as adjacent inspiration rather than a preserved intervention note. Its value is that it ties lifespan differences in phase-synchrony switching to a structural-connectome-constrained oscillator model with an intermediate metastable regime in young adults. That framing is useful for intervention logic because it treats brain-state flexibility as a dynamical-regime problem rather than just another descriptive connectivity statistic.

A fourth paper stayed below full-note threshold but is worth remembering: **Rapid rescue of despair behaviors by sonogenetic neuromodulation of the mPFC-DRN pathway**. The circuit logic is clean in mice, but because it depends on engineered mechanosensitive channel expression, it is much more a circuit-probe result than an immediately useful therapeutic roadmap.

## Most relevant paper

**Experimental Validation of Finite Element Models for Directional DBS: The Critical Role of Boundary Conditions on VTA Accuracy**

This gets top rank because it attacks a quiet but important failure mode in precision neuromodulation: people often argue about targets, contacts, and pathways using electric-field models whose boundary conditions were never experimentally validated. Here, robotic saline-phantom measurements are used to compare several finite-element configurations for directional DBS, and the choice is not cosmetic. Conventional current-controlled Neumann-style setups overestimate spread and inflate predicted volume of tissue activated by about sixty-seven percent relative to the experimentally supported model. That means some downstream connectomic and programming claims may be less precise than they look.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For neuroengineering interfaces:** the selective VNS platform matters because it treats fascicle specificity as the core problem, not as a future add-on.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful pattern today is that intervention logic gets stronger when the physical and dynamical assumptions are made explicit. The DBS finite-element paper matters because it validates the simulation layer that many targeting claims rest on. The selective VNS paper matters because it tries to separate fibers and functions rather than merely stimulating the whole nerve harder. The EEG switching paper is adjacent rather than direct, but it offers a better language for state flexibility and regime selection than the usual static-network summaries.
