## Short overview

The main fresh preserve today is the July 15, 2026 **Molecular Psychiatry** paper **Optimizing brain functional-structural architecture with cTBS to reduce relapse in alcohol use disorder: a randomized controlled trial**. It gets top rank because it does something addiction-neuromodulation papers too often dodge: it follows a clinically serious outcome for a meaningful window instead of pretending a small post-treatment craving wobble is enough. Fifty participants were randomized to active or sham right-DLPFC continuous theta-burst stimulation, and the active arm showed lower one-year relapse risk with a hazard ratio of **0.426**. That is a real result worth archiving.

The reason the paper is not an easy triumph is that the short-term clinical picture is much weaker than the headline. AUDIT scores did not show a significant time-by-group interaction, craving did not either, and the only clean immediate behavioral win was perceived stress. The mechanistic stack is also oversized for the sample: NMF over ALFF, TabPFN relapse prediction, structural-connectome-constrained network control theory, transcriptomic enrichment, and cross-species phenotype mapping. On **N = 50**, that is a lot of machinery. Still, the paper is worth keeping because the central circuit claim is at least pointed and specific. Active cTBS reduced frontoparietal-to-subcortical control energy and frontoparietal-to-ventral-attention control energy, with the frontoparietal-to-subcortical change mediating AUDIT change. That is a better mechanistic attempt than generic "prefrontal regulation improved" rhetoric.

The fresh surrounding field was thinner. **Synergistic entrainment: a multiscale neurodynamic framework for closed-loop cross-modal neuromodulation** looks more like a conceptual architecture paper than a decisive intervention result. **Feasibility of Interleaving Computerized Cognitive Training With Repetitive Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation** is exactly what the title says: a feasibility pilot, not a mechanism-rich preserve. The standing hypnosis, hypnotherapy, and CBT-plus-interventional lanes were checked again, and nothing fresh outranked the existing archive anchors.

## Most relevant paper

**Optimizing brain functional-structural architecture with cTBS to reduce relapse in alcohol use disorder: a randomized controlled trial**

This gets the top rank because it is one of the rare fresh addiction-neuromodulation papers where the clinical endpoint is serious enough to care about. A one-year relapse analysis is worth much more than the usual pre-post craving slide deck, and this paper actually clears that bar. The active arm's relapse hazard ratio of 0.426 is not huge-sample certainty, but it is a real longitudinal signal.

The paper is also more specific than most of its lane about what it thinks the intervention is doing. Instead of waving vaguely at prefrontal control, it models the stimulation site as frontoparietal-network dominant, then estimates the control energy required to move from that system into subcortical and ventral-attention target states under each participant's structural connectome. The useful mechanistic claim is not the whole stack. It is the narrower one: active cTBS reduced frontoparietal-to-subcortical control energy, and that change mediated AUDIT change.

The problem is that the mechanism package is still too heavy for the trial size. The study has fifty analyzed participants, unbalanced groups, no significant short-term group interaction on AUDIT, no significant short-term group interaction on craving, no formal blinding-integrity check, and no preregistration of the network-control analysis pipeline. Then it piles on NMF, TabPFN, transcriptomics, and mouse-homology mapping. Some of that is hypothesis-generating garnish rather than evidential core. The right read is not "precision cTBS for AUD has arrived." The right read is that this paper earns keep status because it combines a rare clinically meaningful endpoint with a more explicit circuit-control hypothesis than the field usually offers.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful July sixteenth lesson is that a small but real longitudinal clinical endpoint beats a louder mechanistic performance when the field is full of stimulation papers that barely follow people at all. Today's preserve matters because it pairs a randomized one-year relapse signal with a specific, structurally constrained control-path hypothesis instead of generic prefrontal folklore. But it is still not clean proof of precision therapy. The relapse result is the keeper; the bigger multiscale mechanism story should be treated as exploratory until a larger and more targeted trial earns it.
