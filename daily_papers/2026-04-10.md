## Theme

Today is a **state-readout, personalization, and focality-tradeoff** day. The strongest clinical paper is **An EEG-based digital biomarker for personalizing transcranial magnetic stimulation in major depressive disorder**, because it at least tries to stratify patients before treatment rather than pretending bilateral dorsolateral prefrontal cortex repetitive TMS hits everyone with the same leverage. The strongest network-and-propagation paper is **Thalamocortical network dynamics in focal epilepsy: SEEG investigation**, because it links seizure spread to a specific thalamic readout and to directed beta-band interactions across seizure-onset and surrounding cortex instead of treating thalamic neuromodulation as a black-box success. The most useful methods paper is **On the feasibility of temporal interference stimulation of human brains using two arrays of electrodes**, because it does not claim therapeutic proof but does directly confront the practical optimization bottleneck that keeps temporal interference from becoming a serious focal deep-stimulation platform.

## Short overview

The most relevant paper today is **Thalamocortical network dynamics in focal epilepsy: SEEG investigation**. It is not the most clinically polished paper in the set, but it is the one that most improves intervention logic. In sixteen patients with focal epilepsy and two hundred fifty-five seizures, the authors analyzed stereo-electroencephalography recordings with simultaneous thalamic and cortical sampling, including anterior thalamus or pulvinar plus seizure-onset zone, near-seizure-onset cortex, and contralateral controls. They report that seizures involved broad increases in broadband power and low-frequency rhythmic activity across the thalamocortical network, but a more distinctive and consistent change appeared in the thalamus: early and sustained steepening of the aperiodic slope. Inter-regional coupling was dominated less by every-frequency chaos than by beta-band directed interactions, with early seizure-onset-zone outflow toward nearby cortex and reverse feedback in parallel through direct cortico-cortical and indirect transthalamic routes. The useful claim is not merely that the thalamus participates. It is that thalamic aperiodic slope may index propagation-relevant network state and could become a physiology-informed variable for precision neuromodulation in epilepsy. It is still a preprint, and I only inspected the abstract and PMC summary, so this is a mechanistic keep, not a plug-and-play biomarker.

The second paper is **An EEG-based digital biomarker for personalizing transcranial magnetic stimulation in major depressive disorder**. This is the strongest clinical-translational paper today because it tries to prospectively sort patients by a pretreatment biomarker rather than backfilling a responder story after the fact. The authors define a dorsolateral prefrontal cortex sensitivity index, or dSI, from EEG functional-connectivity features using L1-regularized logistic regression. They then prospectively stratify major depressive disorder patients into sensitive, non-sensitive, and sham groups, with all groups receiving a personalized, neuronavigated bilateral dorsolateral prefrontal cortex repetitive TMS protocol over two weeks. According to the abstract, baseline dSI predicted later clinical and cognitive outcomes, and the prospectively labeled sensitive group showed larger improvements in emotional-attention reaction time plus larger reductions in hostility and paranoia than the non-sensitive and sham groups. The reason to keep this is not that it solves depression personalization. The reason is that it makes a pre-treatment stratification claim severe enough to matter. The main caveat is equally important: from the accessible text, the most explicit benefits are on cognitive and symptom-subdomain measures rather than a clean broad antidepressant endpoint, so the paper looks more like a promising stratification signal than a settled clinical selector.

The third keep-worthy paper is **On the feasibility of temporal interference stimulation of human brains using two arrays of electrodes**. This is a methods and engineering paper, not evidence that temporal interference is ready for patients. But it is better than generic temporal-interference hype because it tackles a real problem: the field loves to say temporal interference can reach deep targets, then quietly relies on optimization procedures that are too slow or too awkward to deploy at useful scale. This paper argues that replacing two or a few electrode pairs with two electrode arrays improves focality while keeping optimization fast enough to be practical. The author reports that the existing optimization algorithm naturally extends to the two-array setting, runs in under thirty seconds, and can achieve slightly better hippocampal focality than much more cumbersome multi-pair approaches. There is also a hardware implementation using ten electrodes on an eight-channel device. The cautious read is that this remains computational and platform-level evidence, not physiological proof of deeper or cleaner human target engagement. Still, as a methods note, it is exactly the kind of paper that improves whether a modality can even make fair claims later.

A fourth item stayed below note threshold but remains useful citation material: **Classification of functional brain patterns elicited by deep brain stimulation of the subthalamic nucleus in Parkinson's disease**. It is a respectable network-classification paper with a large DBS-fMRI dataset and external validation, but for today it feels more like infrastructure for parsing DBS ON versus OFF states than a paper that clearly changes targeting or control logic.

## Most relevant paper

**Thalamocortical network dynamics in focal epilepsy: SEEG investigation**

This is the top paper because it does more than note that thalamic stimulation works in epilepsy. It asks what thalamic signals actually track seizure propagation dynamics when the thalamus and cortex are recorded together. That is the right mechanistic question if one cares about physiology-informed programming, biomarkers, or adaptive control. The strongest part of the paper is the combination of local and network readouts: thalamic aperiodic slope as a local signature, beta-band directed coupling as a network signature, and seizure-subtype comparisons that separate more clinically propagative events from subclinical ones. It is still early and preprint-level, but it is much more useful than a generic thalamic-targeting success story.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For epilepsy neuromodulation:** the SEEG paper suggests thalamic aperiodic slope could be a propagation-relevant readout rather than treating the thalamus as a static relay target.
- **For psychiatric personalization:** the EEG-dSI paper is interesting because it tries to stratify dorsolateral prefrontal cortex repetitive TMS sensitivity before treatment, not just model retrospective outcome variance.
- **For noninvasive deep stimulation methods:** the temporal-interference arrays paper is valuable because it focuses on focality, optimization time, and implementability together instead of selling only theoretical depth reach.
- **For skepticism baseline:** none of these papers closes the loop clinically. The epilepsy and temporal-interference papers are preprints, and the depression paper’s accessible results emphasize selective outcome improvements rather than a fully convincing broad antidepressant effect.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The pattern today is that neuromodulation looks stronger when papers stop talking about stimulation as if it were the whole story and instead specify the latent state, the responder stratification logic, or the geometric constraints that determine whether focal control is even plausible. The epilepsy SEEG paper matters because it extracts a candidate thalamic state variable tied to seizure propagation. The depression EEG paper matters because it makes prospective pretreatment stratification a real claim rather than a rhetorical wish. The temporal-interference arrays paper matters because it treats focality and deployability as part of the scientific problem, not an engineering footnote. Across all three, the lesson is the same: intervention claims improve when the paper names the state it is reading, the population it expects to help, or the physical bottleneck it is actually solving.
