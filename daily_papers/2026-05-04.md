## Short overview

The most relevant paper today is **Complexity of resting cortical activity predicts neurophysiological responses to theta-burst stimulation but fails to generalize: A rigorous machine-learning approach**. The authors combine baseline resting-state EEG features with baseline transcranial magnetic stimulation measures to predict single-session intermittent theta-burst stimulation responses in two independent healthy-adult test-retest cohorts. Internal cross-validation looked promising, with about eighty-one percent binary accuracy, but external validation dropped to about sixty-nine percent. That is the useful result. The paper is valuable not because it found a magic predictor, but because it shows how easy it is to overread fragile personalization signals when stimulation effects themselves are unstable.

The second paper is **Subthalamic nucleus deep brain stimulation in Meige syndrome: mapping the optimal stimulation sites and network targets**. In sixty-five patients across two centers, the authors combine local stimulation modeling with whole-brain functional-connectivity mapping and then validate the learned patterns in an independent cohort. The core claim is that best outcomes cluster in dorsolateral sensorimotor subthalamic nucleus extending toward associative territory, and at the network level align with positive connectivity to cerebellum and negative connectivity to somatosensory cortex. This is still normative-connectivity inference rather than direct mechanism, but it is a better targeting paper than most because it at least tries to survive external validation.

The third paper is **Dithering suppresses half-harmonic neural synchronisation to photic stimulation in humans**. Using EEG during periodic and jittered photic stimulation in healthy adults, the authors show that slightly jittering pulse timing suppresses half-harmonic synchronisation more than it suppresses synchronisation at the target frequency. The main value is conceptual. If periodic stimulation can create unwanted subharmonic or superharmonic locking, then control policies should care not only about how much energy is delivered, but about the temporal regularity of delivery.

The fourth paper is **Piezoelectric neuromodulation of the subthalamic nucleus ameliorates motor and nonmotor symptoms of Parkinson's disease**. In a six-hydroxydopamine mouse model, ultrasound-responsive nanoparticles injected into subthalamic nucleus were used to drive repeated deep-target stimulation, with reported improvements in gait, pain, anxiety-like behavior, and nigrostriatal pathology. This is worth keeping as interface-design inspiration, but the translational pitch should be kept on a short leash. Intracranial particle injection plus ultrasound actuation is not a clean substitute for conventional deep brain stimulation, and the abstract alone does not prove precise, state-specific neuromodulation.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For neuromodulation personalization claims:** the PLoS Computational Biology paper is a good baseline reset. If a predictor fails under external validation and test-retest variability is poor, that is not a footnote. It is the story.
- **For targeting work:** the Meige paper suggests a better bar for network-guided DBS mapping, namely local plus network models with explicit validation rather than one-center storytelling.
- **For control logic:** the dithering paper says waveform timing regularity itself is a controllable design variable, not just amplitude or frequency.
- **For device/interface framing:** the piezoelectric STN paper is interesting as deep-actuation design, but not yet as intervention logic. Keep the engineering idea, distrust the substitution rhetoric.
- **For repo balance:** today stays balanced across clinical-translational DBS, computational prediction realism, stimulation-control methods, and speculative neuroengineering.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful pattern today is that better neuromodulation work gets stricter about hidden failure modes. The computational paper says personalization models break when stimulation effects are not themselves stable. The Meige study says targeting claims are more believable when they survive independent validation and link local anatomy to a distributed network. The dithering paper says periodic stimulation can create unwanted structure that should be engineered away rather than ignored. And the nanoparticle paper is a reminder that novel actuation hardware is not the same thing as demonstrated therapeutic control.
