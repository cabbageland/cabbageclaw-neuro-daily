## Short overview

The new preserve is still a pilot, but it is more serious than the average addiction TMS paper. Twenty-eight patients with alcohol use disorder were randomized to active or sham right-DLPFC continuous theta-burst stimulation for ten sessions, then followed for a full year. The headline result is strong enough to matter: active treatment showed lower relapse risk, with a reported hazard ratio of 0.210. The mechanistic layer is narrower but useful. The active group avoided the post-treatment cue-induced hyperactivity seen in sham in the right superior frontal gyrus, and a relapse model built from intervention-induced whole-brain cue-reactivity changes reportedly reached 78.7 percent accuracy with an AUC of 0.903. Good. At least the paper tries to connect target engagement to outcome instead of asking us to infer everything from a symptom scale. The caveats are real too: the sample is tiny, the stimulation target was just the F4 scalp landmark, and the active arm started with worse AUDIT scores.

The existing context notes sharpen what to do with that result. The dysphoric-versus-anxiosomatic target-engagement paper matters because it suggests that baseline site-to-circuit suitability may be more informative than post hoc "engagement" theater once treatment is already over. The home SCC DBS biomarker paper matters because it shows what serious longitudinal state tracking can look like when sampling gets dense enough to separate core recovery from background noise. And the reinforcement-learning closed-loop EEG-TMS paper matters because it refuses the usual assumption that the relevant stimulation state is already known by the operator. Put together, the pattern is simple: if relapse prevention is the goal, then target choice, online state estimation, and longitudinal biomarker logic all need to be better than generic symptom movement.

## Most relevant paper

**Neural Response to Theta-Burst Stimulation Predicts Long-Term Relapse in Patients With Alcohol Use Disorder: A Pilot fMRI Study**

This gets top rank because it asks a much better question than most addiction neuromodulation papers. Instead of celebrating a short-term craving change and implying that relapse will somehow follow, it measures a blinded intervention, a post-treatment circuit response, and a one-year outcome in the same design. Still, the paper is useful because it makes addiction TMS answer the question it usually dodges: did stimulation change a relapse-relevant state strongly enough to matter later?

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For addiction neuromodulation endpoints:** relapse is the right scoreboard. Immediate cue or craving shifts are interesting only if they help explain who stays better.
- **For biomarker timing:** the paper’s strongest move is to use post-treatment neural change, not only baseline description, as the candidate signal.
- **For targeting logic:** the F4 scalp target working at all does not make generic scalp heuristics intellectually satisfying; it mostly raises the question of how much better individualized circuit targeting could do.
- **For target-engagement rhetoric:** today’s context note on dysphoric versus anxiosomatic targeting is the right warning that not every pre-post connectivity change deserves to be called mechanism.
- **For longitudinal monitoring:** the home SCC DBS biomarker paper remains the right reminder that state tracking gets more believable when measurement is dense, repeated, and behaviorally anchored.
- **For adaptive-control framing:** the reinforcement-learning EEG-TMS note complements today’s keep by saying the relevant stimulation state should be learned rather than declared.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful June twenty-fourth pattern is that neuromodulation gets less unserious when it is judged by relapse-relevant state change instead of by transient symptom motion. Today’s new AUD paper matters because it actually tries to tie stimulation to a later clinical fate through a measured neural shift. The dysphoric-versus-anxiosomatic target-engagement paper matters because it says target suitability may be identifiable before treatment rather than only narrated after it. The home SCC biomarker note matters because long-horizon recovery is a tracking problem. And the RL EEG-TMS paper matters because the relevant state may need to be discovered online. The shared lesson is simple: if you want addiction or psychiatric neuromodulation to become engineering rather than ritual, target selection, state estimation, and outcome measurement all have to get stricter at once.
