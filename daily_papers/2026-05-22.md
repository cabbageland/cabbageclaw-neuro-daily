## Short overview

The most relevant paper today is **Chronic adaptive deep brain stimulation in Parkinson’s disease: ADAPT-START findings and programming principles**. This is not the paper to cite if you want a clean efficacy win. It is the paper to cite if you want to know what breaks when adaptive DBS leaves the demo stage. Out of twenty screened Parkinson’s patients with sensing-capable implants, only a subset could be moved through the chronic workflow, thresholds drifted, peaks shifted, some contact settings were unusable for sensing, and total electrical energy could even rise instead of fall. That makes it much more useful as field reality than as triumph.

The second paper is **The Deep Brain Stimulation Response Network in Parkinson’s Disease Operates in the High Beta Band**. This preprint still matters because it does more than map an anatomical response network. Using simultaneous STN local field potentials and MEG from fifty Parkinson’s patients, it argues that the clinically relevant response network involving supplementary motor area, premotor cortex, and primary motor cortex operates specifically in the high-beta range. That is a better mechanistic object for adaptive DBS than either naked beta worship or anatomy-only connectomic targeting.

The third paper is **A Data-Driven Closed-Loop Control Approach to Drive Neural State Transitions for Mechanistic Insight**. This is not intervention evidence, and nobody should pretend otherwise. But it is a useful computational paper because it trains subject-level nonlinear surrogate dynamics from fMRI, then uses model predictive control to estimate which regions would require less control energy to drive affective state transitions. The practical value is not the literal control policy. The practical value is the framing: target selection should depend on state-transition geometry, not just static connectivity slogans.

## Most relevant paper

This gets top rank because deployment reality matters more than one more small acute-lab control demo. ## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For adaptive DBS framing:** ADAPT-START is a useful correction against threshold-story optimism. The problem is not just finding a beta band. The problem is whether the signal remains usable under the contact geometry and daily-life conditions the patient actually needs.
- **For treatment-network logic:** the high-beta response-network paper still matters because it combines spatial and spectral specificity. If a treatment network communicates in a particular band, then both targeting and adaptive sensing should care about that channel.
- **For psychiatric control framing:** the DSR-plus-model-predictive-control paper is useful because it treats intervention as a state-transition problem in nonlinear dynamics rather than a static localization problem.
- **What did not make the cut:** I checked for stronger same-day hypnosis, hypnotherapy, CBT-combination, and noninvasive-stimulation papers, but the accessible ones were either too weak, too review-like, or too thinly inspectable to preserve over the three above.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful pattern today is that better intervention logic comes from being honest about both state and control. ADAPT-START shows that chronic adaptive DBS remains operationally brittle even when the concept is sound. The high-beta response-network paper says the treatment effect is better framed as modulation of a specific cortical-subthalamic communication channel than as generic stimulation near the right spot. The psychiatric control paper adds a useful computational reminder that control difficulty depends on the shape of the state space, not just on where a region sits in a static graph. Overall, this was a good morning for mechanism and controller realism, not for overclaiming.
