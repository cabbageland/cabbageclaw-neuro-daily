## Short overview

The most relevant paper today is **Machine learning-based optimization of dual subthalamic nucleus and substantia nigra targeting in deep brain stimulation**. This is a good translational paper because it addresses a real surgical planning problem instead of selling generic AI. Using 612 reconstructed STN trajectories from 306 Parkinson patients, the authors ask when one lead can reliably cover both the subthalamic nucleus and substantia nigra, and whether dual-target engagement can be made more systematic. The useful contribution is not the classifier itself. It is the conversion of messy multi-target geometry into concrete, image-grounded planning heuristics for SNr versus SNc engagement.

The second paper is **Task-guided accelerated cTBS simultaneously treats depression and social dysfunction in patients with major depressive disorder: a randomized clinical trial**. This is interesting because it does not just stimulate the right DLPFC by convention. It uses an ultimatum-game task to localize an individualized right-sided target tied to social-interaction processing, then tests whether accelerated cTBS changes both symptoms and effective connectivity. The paper is more compelling as a targeted-circuit proof of concept than as a definitive depression treatment study, but that is still more useful than another vague “personalized TMS” claim.

The third paper is **Controlling the human connectome with spatially diffuse input signals**. It is not a new note today, but it stayed in the top set after a broad Brave-first scout because it improves intervention logic at the actuator level. If your control theory assumes pointwise node actuation that no real stimulation device has, the rest of the math can look elegant while being physically silly. This paper fixes that assumption in a simple way and shows that the control landscape changes materially.

## Most relevant paper

**Machine learning-based optimization of dual subthalamic nucleus and substantia nigra targeting in deep brain stimulation**

This gets top rank because it solves a real intervention-design problem at a level close to practice. Many dual-target DBS ideas amount to “aim for STN and hope the lower contacts also land somewhere useful in the substantia nigra.” This paper shows that dual engagement is often feasible, separates SNr from SNc logic, and translates patient-native reconstructions into rules a surgical team could actually use. It is still geometry and predicted engagement, not outcome-validated symptom control. But it is a real upgrade over casual multi-target rhetoric.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For DBS trajectory planning:** the dual-target paper matters because it reframes one-lead STN plus SN stimulation as a probabilistic planning problem with specific geometric tradeoffs, not a lucky byproduct of standard targeting.
- **For individualized TMS:** the task-guided cTBS paper matters because it uses task-evoked functional localization rather than only resting connectivity or generic scalp coordinates, then checks effective-connectivity changes instead of stopping at symptom scales.
- **For network-control framing:** the spatially diffuse control paper still matters because it argues that a realistic actuator model can change estimated control energy and where control leverage appears to live.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful pattern today is that better intervention papers specify the geometry of leverage. The dual-target DBS paper asks what one electrode can actually hit and under what trajectory constraints. The task-guided cTBS paper asks whether the right individualized prefrontal node can be chosen from behavior-linked activation rather than from tradition. And the spatially diffuse control paper asks whether our actuator model is physically honest in the first place. None of these papers solves closed-loop psychiatry. But all three improve the intervention logic upstream, which is more valuable than another average-treatment paper with vague personalization language.
