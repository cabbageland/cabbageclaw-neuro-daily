## Short overview

The most relevant paper today is **A Network Target for Memory Dysfunction Derived from Brain Lesions and Stimulations**. This is one of the better recent examples of network neuroscience actually doing intervention work instead of aesthetic connectomics. Across twelve datasets and 1,247 patients, the authors combine lesion, DBS, and TMS evidence to derive a convergent verbal-memory network, validate it in held-out cohorts, and show that target overlap helps explain effect-size heterogeneity in prior Alzheimer stimulation trials. The result is still normative-connectome targeting, not personalized therapy. But it is a much stronger answer to “where should we stimulate for memory?” than single-modality target folklore.

The second paper is **Transcranial alternating current stimulation can disrupt or reestablish neural entrainment in parkinsonian motor cortex**. This is a useful mechanistic primate paper because it makes tACS less hand-wavy. Weak fields can disrupt pathological beta synchrony, while stronger or frequency-matched fields can instead re-lock spiking to the applied rhythm. That means tACS dosing is not just an intensity problem. It is a competition problem between endogenous oscillations and externally imposed timing.

The third paper is **Control of cortical population activity with patterned microstimulation**. It is not new to the repo, but it still belongs in today’s top set because it gives the cleanest methods-side control frame I found after a broad Brave-first scout. REACH-Ctrl learns a reachable manifold of cortical population states directly from short stimulation-response training epochs and then computes pulse sequences that drive spiking toward target patterns without pretending the full local circuit is known. It remains local-circuit macaque work, not psychiatric closed-loop therapy, but the control abstraction is much better than most recent neuromodulation control rhetoric.

## Most relevant paper

**A Network Target for Memory Dysfunction Derived from Brain Lesions and Stimulations**

This gets top rank because it actually earns the phrase network-guided targeting. The paper does not just correlate resting connectivity with memory scores. It combines causal evidence from lesions, DBS, and TMS, derives a convergent memory network, validates it in unseen datasets, and then asks whether overlap with that target helps explain why prior Alzheimer stimulation trials varied so much. It is still retrospective and normative-connectome based, but it is substantially closer to useful targeting logic than most memory-neuromodulation papers.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For memory neuromodulation targeting:** the memory-network paper matters because it argues that target discovery should use convergent causal evidence across lesions and perturbations, not one modality at a time.
- **For oscillatory-control framing:** the parkinsonian tACS paper matters because it makes the effect of tACS explicitly regime-dependent. Weak fields may compete with pathological beta timing while stronger or matched fields may restore entrainment.
- **For control-methods baselines:** REACH-Ctrl remains important because it gives a concrete finite-horizon reachable-manifold baseline for perturbation design without requiring full system identification.
- **What did not make the cut:** I checked additional recent clinical and translational items, including hypnosis-adjacent, CBT-combination, and broader neuromodulation papers. Some were decent citation material, but none beat today’s top three on mechanistic clarity, full-text accessibility, and preserve-worthy intervention logic.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The useful pattern today is that better neuromodulation papers stop fetishizing the stimulation widget and start clarifying the control problem. The memory-target paper says good targeting should come from convergent causal network evidence, not coordinate folklore. The parkinsonian tACS paper says the same waveform can either disrupt or reinforce pathological timing depending on the field regime, so “stimulation worked” is too blunt a description. And REACH-Ctrl says that for invasive control, the right abstraction may be the set of states your hardware can actually reach, not a fantasy full-brain model. Overall, this was a strong morning for papers that make intervention logic more explicit.
