## Short overview

The most relevant paper today is **Exploratory decoding of TMS-EEG: Predicting TEP response to intermittent and continuous theta burst stimulation**. In a randomized single-blind crossover study of 15 healthy men, the authors ask whether resting-state EEG features before stimulation can predict changes in six TMS-evoked-potential components after intermittent or continuous theta-burst stimulation over left primary motor cortex. Lasso and CatBoost both beat a random-guessing baseline, with protocol- and component-specific differences in which model worked best. This is still small and exploratory, but it is the right kind of question, because it treats stimulation response as state-dependent and partially decodable rather than intrinsically capricious.

The second paper is **Neurochemically informed network control theory reveals energetic dysregulation of altered brain state dynamics in obsessive-compulsive disorder**. Using co-activation-pattern analysis plus network control theory in 198 OCD patients and 109 controls, the authors report more occupancy of ventral-attention and somatomotor states with default-mode suppression, less persistence of a frontoparietal-dominant state, and altered transition energetics across those states. The useful part is not the psychiatry branding. It is the attempt to tie rapid state switching to an explicit energetic-control framing and then test virtual perturbations that partially normalize the abnormal dynamics.

The third paper is **Acoustic Coupling for Double-Blind Human Low-Intensity Focused Ultrasound Neuromodulation**. The paper focuses on coupling hardware rather than physiology, but that is exactly why it matters. The authors build matched gel-plastic verum and sham couplers and show that one configuration stays acoustically transparent while an air-gap sham strongly attenuates transmitted energy without obvious tactile or visual differences. Human ultrasound neuromodulation still has too much sloppiness around blinding and coupling, so a paper that hardens that layer is more useful than another vague efficacy teaser.

The fourth paper is **Subthalamic nucleus deep brain stimulation connectivity related to apathy outcomes in Parkinson's disease**. In 53 Parkinson's patients with bilateral STN DBS, the authors use normative structural-connectivity modeling to link worsening apathy to asymmetric pathways involving right dentatorubrothalamic and supplementary-motor trajectories and left anteromedial-STN connections to anterior cingulate and orbitofrontal regions. The holdout correlation is stronger than many connectomic side-effect papers manage, but the sample is still modest, the model is normative rather than patient-specific, and the result should guide caution and hypothesis-building more than programming dogma.

## Most relevant paper

**Exploratory decoding of TMS-EEG: Predicting TEP response to intermittent and continuous theta burst stimulation**

This gets top rank because it targets a real translational bottleneck rather than just showing another average stimulation effect. If pre-stimulation resting EEG carries predictive information about whether iTBS or cTBS will shift particular TEP components, then protocol assignment and adaptive response tracking can become more principled. The main caveat is that the study is tiny, male-only, motor-cortex-based, and still far from proving clinically useful personalization.

## Novelty / framing / baseline impact

- **For personalized noninvasive stimulation:** the TMS-EEG paper supports the framing that response variability is at least partly decodable from baseline state, not just irreducible noise.
- **For computational psychiatry:** the OCD network-control paper is a useful example of making state-transition claims more explicit, though it still relies on model assumptions that need external stress tests.
- **For ultrasound neuromodulation methods:** the coupling paper is a reminder that blinding and energy delivery are still live engineering problems, not solved background details.

## One-paragraph takeaway

The best papers today are the ones that make neuromodulation less hand-wavy. The TMS-EEG study says baseline physiology may contain protocol-specific predictive signal. The OCD network-control study says psychiatric state dynamics can be framed as transitions with differing energetic costs rather than just altered static connectivity. The LIFU coupling paper says human ultrasound needs better sham and coupling discipline if it wants trustworthy causal claims. The Parkinson apathy paper says side effects may be connectomically structured, but still needs more patient-specific and prospective validation before it becomes programming doctrine.
